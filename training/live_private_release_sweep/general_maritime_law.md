The cargo claimant should notify the carrier immediately, preserve survey and temperature/handling evidence, and identify the lawful bill-of-lading holder. If the Hague-Visby Rules apply, the carrier’s duties, defences, package limitation and one-year time bar will structure the claim.

### Application and right to sue

The Rules have force of law in the United Kingdom through the Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1971 and apply compulsorily to covered bills where the statutory and Convention connecting factors are met (Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1971, s 1 and sch). Shipment from Liverpool is a strong connecting factor, but the document, voyage and any charterparty incorporation must be checked.

The lawful holder of a bill of lading generally acquires the contractual rights of suit as if it had been an original party (Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1992, s 2). Endorsement, delivery, timing of acquisition and whether the claimant suffered the loss must be proved. Title to goods and contractual rights of suit are related but distinct.

### Breach and defences

The carrier must exercise due diligence before and at the beginning of the voyage to make the ship seaworthy, properly manned and equipped, and fit the holds for cargo (Hague-Visby Rules, art III r 1). It must also properly and carefully load, handle, stow, carry, keep, care for and discharge the goods (Hague-Visby Rules, art III r 2). The claimant should link wetting, impact, contamination, delay or temperature excursion to those duties.

The carrier may invoke an Article IV exception, contractual causation points or inherent vice, but bears the relevant evidential burden and cannot contract below the Rules’ minimum protection. Unseaworthiness defeats a defence where causally relevant and due diligence is not proved.

### Notice, limitation and time bar

Apparent damage should be notified in writing at delivery; non-apparent damage should normally be notified within three days. Failure is prima facie evidence of delivery as described, not automatic extinction of the claim (Hague-Visby Rules, art III r 6).

Suit must ordinarily be brought within one year of delivery or the date when the goods should have been delivered, unless a valid extension is agreed (Hague-Visby Rules, art III r 6). Negotiations do not safely stop time. The carrier’s package-or-unit limitation is generally 666.67 SDR per package or unit or 2 SDR per kilogram of gross weight lost or damaged, whichever is higher, subject to declaration and loss-of-limitation rules (Hague-Visby Rules, art IV r 5).

The claimant should retain the original bill, booking and sea waybill records, mate’s receipts, clean/claused bill, packing evidence, surveys, photographs, reefer logs, notices, invoices and mitigation sale evidence. It should issue protective proceedings or obtain a clear time extension before the anniversary, then quantify physical loss, foreseeable consequential loss, salvage and mitigation within the contractual and Convention limits.
