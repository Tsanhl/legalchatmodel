Unless a narrow exception such as a privileged will applies, a handwritten will witnessed by only one person is not formally valid. Its clarity, signature and the deceased’s intention cannot replace the statutory requirement for two witnesses. The estate will therefore pass under an earlier valid will or, if none, intestacy; rectification cannot ordinarily cure defective execution.

### Formal validity

A will must be in writing and signed by the testator, intending to give effect to it, with the signature made or acknowledged in the presence of two witnesses present at the same time; each witness must then attest and sign or acknowledge their signature in the testator’s presence (Wills Act 1837, s 9). The witnesses need not know the document is a will, but one witness is insufficient.

The family should preserve the original and investigate whether a second witness signed elsewhere or acknowledged an existing signature in a manner satisfying section 9. Affidavit evidence from the witness, the drafter and anyone present may resolve uncertainty. Electronic or remote witnessing rules applicable at the execution date should be checked, but they did not abolish the two-witness requirement.

### Rectification, construction and intestacy

Rectification can correct a valid will that fails to carry out the testator’s intentions because of a clerical error or failure to understand instructions (Administration of Justice Act 1982, s 20). It is not a dispensing power allowing the court to validate a document executed with only one witness. Construction and extrinsic evidence can resolve ambiguity in the words of a valid will but cannot supply missing execution formalities (Administration of Justice Act 1982, s 21).

If no earlier valid will exists, the statutory intestacy order determines who inherits (Administration of Estates Act 1925, s 46). The result depends on any spouse or civil partner, descendants and other relatives. A disappointed dependant may separately consider a family-provision claim, but that does not validate the document (Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975).

### Probate steps

No one should alter, staple, write on or discard the original. The family should search the deceased’s papers, solicitors, will registers and safe custody for a later or earlier original; obtain the death certificate and asset/liability schedule; identify intestacy beneficiaries; and warn the probate registry of a genuine dispute through a caveat where appropriate. The purported will remains relevant evidence of wishes and may support consensual variation after advice, but it should not be submitted as valid without disclosing the execution defect.
