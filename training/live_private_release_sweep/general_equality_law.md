The refusal may breach the reasonable-adjustments duty and may also amount to discrimination arising from disability, but neither claim is automatic. The strongest route is likely the adjustments claim because occupational health identified specific equipment; the employer must show that it investigated effectiveness, practicality, cost and alternatives rather than simply refusing.

### Disability and knowledge

The employee must first satisfy the statutory disability definition: a physical or mental impairment with a substantial and long-term adverse effect on normal day-to-day activities (Equality Act 2010, s 6). Medical and occupational-health records, the duration and likely duration of the condition, and its effect without treatment should be preserved.

For employment adjustments, the employer is generally not required to take a step if it did not know, and could not reasonably have been expected to know, both that the person was disabled and likely to face the relevant disadvantage (Equality Act 2010, sch 8 para 20). An occupational-health recommendation sent to management is strong evidence of actual or constructive knowledge, although its wording and recipients matter.

### Reasonable adjustments

The duty contains three distinct requirements: addressing a provision, criterion or practice (PCP) that puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage; addressing a physical feature; and providing an auxiliary aid where its absence creates substantial disadvantage (Equality Act 2010, s 20). Failure to comply is discrimination (Equality Act 2010, s 21).

The employee should identify the precise disadvantage. It may arise from a PCP requiring home working without suitable equipment, from the absence of an auxiliary aid such as specialist hardware or software, or from both. “Substantial” means more than minor or trivial. The comparison is with non-disabled people, but this is not a direct-discrimination comparator exercise.

Reasonableness is objective and fact-sensitive. Relevant matters include whether the equipment would remove or materially reduce the disadvantage, its cost, the employer’s resources, implementation time, disruption, available external assistance and viable alternatives. The employer normally bears the cost of a required employment adjustment. An Access to Work contribution may be relevant, but possible public funding does not justify refusing to investigate.

Occupational-health advice is persuasive rather than conclusively binding. The employer may obtain clarification, discuss alternatives and trial equipment, but should consult the employee and make a reasoned decision. Disability equality may require more favourable treatment to remove disadvantage; it is not limited to treating everyone identically (*Archibald v Fife Council* [2004] UKHL 32, [2004] ICR 954).

### Discrimination arising from disability

Section 15 applies where the employer treats the employee unfavourably because of something arising in consequence of disability, unless the treatment is a proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim (Equality Act 2010, s 15). No less-favourable comparator is required. Depending on the facts, the “something” might be the need to work at home, reduced tolerance of standard equipment or disability-related absence; the unfavourable treatment could be refusal, discipline or loss of opportunity.

The employee must connect the treatment to that disability consequence. The employer has a defence if it did not know and could not reasonably have known of the disability. Otherwise it must identify a real legitimate aim and show proportionality, including why a funded or less intrusive adjustment would not achieve it. General cost or administrative convenience without evidence is unlikely to suffice.

Direct discrimination should not be added merely because the employee is disabled. It requires less favourable treatment because of disability and an appropriate comparator (Equality Act 2010, s 13). The same refusal may support more than one route, but each test must be proved separately.

### Evidence, process and remedies

The employee should retain the occupational-health report, requests, medical evidence, equipment quotations, employer responses, policies, examples of equipment provided to others, evidence of the disadvantage and proposals for a trial. They should make a focused written request and grievance, explaining how each proposed item would help.

Before a tribunal claim, the employee normally notifies ACAS for early conciliation. Employment claims have short limitation periods, commonly three months less one day from the act complained of, subject to the statutory early-conciliation adjustment and any continuing-act argument. Prompt advice is essential (Equality Act 2010, s 123).

If facts permit an inference of discrimination, the burden can shift to the employer to provide a non-discriminatory explanation (Equality Act 2010, s 136). Remedies may include a declaration, compensation for financial loss and injury to feelings, and an appropriate recommendation (Equality Act 2010, s 124). The practical priority is an urgent, documented equipment trial while preserving the right to conciliate and claim.
