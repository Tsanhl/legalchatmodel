The museum should freeze any disposal, loan or conservation work that could affect evidence; secure the object and acquisition file; and commission a privileged provenance and title review. Unlawful export is a serious warning, but it does not automatically establish that the source state owns the object or can recover it in an English court.

### Build the object, title and situs chronology

The review should identify the object precisely, its likely excavation context, every possessor and transfer, the location (**lex situs**) at each transfer, the date and law of export, import documentation, dealer warranties, payment records and any gaps or altered provenance. Independent specialists should test inscriptions, soil, restoration and ownership documents. The museum should preserve originals, metadata and communications and avoid alerting intermediaries in a way that risks evidence destruction.

English conflicts rules generally look to the lex situs to determine whether a transfer of a movable passed title. In *Winkworth v Christie, Manson & Woods Ltd*, a foreign transfer could determine title even after stolen works returned to England (*Winkworth v Christie, Manson & Woods Ltd* [1980] Ch 496). The museum therefore needs advice on the law of each country where a material transfer occurred, not simply the law of the object’s origin.

If English law governed a transfer, a seller who was not the owner normally could not pass better title, subject to statutory exceptions (Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 21). Good faith is important to criminal exposure, equitable relief and settlement, but it is not a universal cure for defective title.

### Ownership law is different from an export prohibition

The central question is what the source-country law did. A foreign **patrimony law** may vest ownership of defined antiquities in the state from excavation or enactment. A genuine proprietary right can be recognised in England: the Court of Appeal treated Iran’s patrimony legislation as capable of creating ownership rather than merely asserting a penal claim (*Government of the Islamic Republic of Iran v The Barakat Galleries Ltd* [2007] EWCA Civ 1374, [2009] QB 22).

By contrast, a law merely prohibiting export may not transfer title to the state. English courts do not ordinarily enforce a foreign sovereign’s public or penal law as though it were an English property claim; that distinction defeated the attempted recovery route in Ortiz (*Attorney-General of New Zealand v Ortiz* [1984] AC 1). The museum therefore needs a certified translation, expert evidence on the foreign law, its commencement date and proof that this object fell within it.

The 1970 UNESCO Convention can inform state cooperation and ethical expectations, but it is not a self-executing private title code in England and should not be applied retrospectively (Convention on the Means of Prohibiting and Preventing the Illicit Import, Export and Transfer of Ownership of Cultural Property (adopted 14 November 1970, entered into force 24 April 1972) 823 UNTS 231). Treaty, diplomatic and ethical restitution routes must be separated from a presently enforceable proprietary claim.

### Limitation, criminal and regulatory exposure

Limitation requires a claim-specific analysis. English law has special rules for actions concerning stolen goods; it is unsafe to assume that every claim expires six years after export or museum acquisition (Limitation Act 1980, s 4). Counsel should identify the proposed claimant, cause of action, alleged theft, first good-faith purchase and any concealment or later conversion before calculating time.

The museum must also assess whether anyone dishonestly dealt in a tainted cultural object knowing or believing it to be tainted (Dealing in Cultural Objects (Offences) Act 2003, s 1). Current sanctions, proceeds-of-crime, customs and import/export rules may be relevant depending on origin, removal date and persons involved. The treasure regime is not the default merely because an item is an antiquity; it principally concerns qualifying finds within its territorial scheme (Treasure Act 1996).

### Response and due diligence

The museum should notify its trustees, insurer and specialist counsel; consider a confidential approach to police or the relevant enforcement body if criminality is reasonably suspected; and engage the source state through a controlled channel without admitting liability or ownership. It should check acquisition-policy compliance, dealer due diligence, databases of stolen art, export licences, excavation permits, customs declarations and whether contractual warranties or indemnities can be enforced.

If the evidence supports foreign state ownership, negotiated return may avoid cost and reputational harm while preserving agreed research, credit or loan arrangements. If only an export breach is shown, diplomatic or ethical restitution may still be appropriate, but the legal recovery case is weaker. The final decision should be recorded transparently by unconflicted trustees after receiving provenance, foreign-law, limitation and public-law advice.
