The tenant should not leave merely because a notice has arrived. They should obtain the notice and tenancy documents, check when it was served and which post-1 May 2026 possession ground is relied on, preserve disrepair evidence, and respond to any court claim. Serious disrepair may defeat or delay a discretionary claim and can support a counterclaim or set-off, but it does not automatically answer every mandatory ground.

### Current possession regime

For England, the current reforms substantially changed private assured tenancies from 1 May 2026 and abolished the ordinary section 21 no-fault route for the new regime (Renters’ Rights Act 2025). A landlord now generally needs a valid statutory ground and the prescribed notice before issuing proceedings (Renters’ Rights Act 2025). A notice served before 1 May 2026 may fall within transitional rules and deadlines, so its date is critical ([Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Government, ‘Repossessing your privately rented property after 1 May 2026’](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/repossessing-your-privately-rented-property-after-1-may-2026)).

The tenant should check the correct form, named parties and property, ground, particulars, notice period, service method and commencement date. They should ask whether the ground is mandatory or discretionary and whether its facts existed both when required and at the hearing. Rent arrears records, sale or occupation evidence, deposit information and compliance with any applicable landlord obligations may matter. Only a court can order possession, and eviction normally requires a warrant executed lawfully.

### Disrepair and fitness

The landlord ordinarily must keep the structure and exterior and installations for water, gas, electricity, sanitation and space/water heating in repair once it has notice of the defect (Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, s 11). The dwelling must also be fit for human habitation throughout the tenancy, judged by statutory hazards and all the circumstances (Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, ss 9A-10).

The tenant should prove notice and condition through dated photographs, videos, repair reports, messages, inspection records, medical evidence, damaged-property receipts and an expert report where proportionate. They should report hazards to the local authority’s environmental-health team and allow reasonable access for inspection and works.

A counterclaim may seek general damages for inconvenience and loss of amenity, special losses, personal-injury damages where causation is proved, and an order for works. The tenant may seek to set damages against rent arrears, but should not withhold rent without advice because additional arrears can strengthen possession grounds. Disrepair can also be relevant to reasonableness on a discretionary ground.

### Urgent steps

The tenant should file any defence and counterclaim by the court deadline, attend the hearing, and seek housing advice immediately. The court may dismiss, adjourn, suspend or make a possession order depending on the ground and facts. If homelessness is threatened, the tenant should approach the local authority before eviction. Gas, electrical, collapse or severe damp risks require urgent reporting. The final merits depend on the exact ground and whether the notice is governed by the pre- or post-1 May 2026 regime.
