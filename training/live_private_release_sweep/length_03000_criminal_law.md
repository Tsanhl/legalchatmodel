The analysis of the facts and legal principles in this case raises several critical issues. First, it is essential to consider the interplay between causation, intoxication, accessorial liability, loss of control, and diminished responsibility. Each of these elements must be examined separately before considering their cumulative impact on the culpability of each party.
In assessing causation, it is crucial to determine whether Gus's act was a substantial cause of Hana's death. The chain of events leading to Hana's death can be traced back to Gus throwing the bottle at her. Although Hana later died due to complications from an operation, the initial injury caused by the bottle remains a significant factor. Under the established law, the original wound still constitutes an operating and substantial cause unless it becomes insignificant through medical intervention (Smith [1959] 2 QB 35). In contrast, if the medical treatment breaks the chain of causation, the original wound may no longer be considered a substantial cause (Cheshire [1991] 1 WLR 844). Given that Hana underwent surgery after being struck by the bottle, it would be necessary to examine whether the medical treatment rendered the original wound insignificant. If the surgery did not significantly mitigate the harm caused by the bottle, then Gus could be held liable for manslaughter under unlawful act manslaughter principles. Since the offence of manslaughter does not require specific intent, voluntary intoxication alone is unlikely to exonerate Gus.
