The defendant should distinguish two applications. A strike-out application attacks the statement of case under CPR r 3.4; summary judgment asks whether the claimant has a realistic evidential and legal prospect under CPR r 24.3. The same defects may support both applications, but the tests, materials and possible orders are not interchangeable.

### Strike out

The most direct ground is that the particulars disclose no reasonable grounds for bringing the claim. CPR r 3.4(2) also covers abuse of process or conduct likely to obstruct the just disposal of proceedings, and failure to comply with a rule, practice direction or court order. The question states only that the defendant considers the particulars legally insufficient, so r 3.4(2)(a) is the proper starting point; abuse or non-compliance should not be alleged without supporting facts (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, r 3.4(2)).

For a contractual claim, the particulars should identify the agreement relied on, the material express or implied terms, the alleged breach, causation and recoverable loss. The defendant should make a schedule showing which indispensable allegation is absent or incoherent. Merely denying the pleaded facts is not normally enough for this ground: a fact-sensitive dispute is ordinarily resolved through evidence, not by treating strike out as a paper trial.

Strike out is discretionary. If a defect can fairly be cured, the court may permit amendment or make an unless order instead of terminating the claim. Conversely, a pleading that remains legally untenable even if its material facts are assumed may be struck out. The jurisdiction should be used consistently with deciding cases justly and at proportionate cost, while recognising that striking out denies a trial (*Three Rivers District Council v Governor and Company of the Bank of England (No 3)* [2001] UKHL 16, [2003] 2 AC 1).

### Summary judgment

Under the current Part 24 structure, the substantive grounds are in CPR r 24.3, not r 24.2. The court may give summary judgment only if the claimant has no real prospect of succeeding on the claim or issue and there is no other compelling reason why it should be disposed of at trial. Rule 24.2 now identifies the types of proceedings in which the procedure is available (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, rr 24.2–24.3; Ministry of Justice, ‘CPR Part 24—Summary Judgment’ <https://www.justice.gov.uk/courts/procedure-rules/civil/rules/part24> accessed 14 July 2026).

“No real prospect” excludes a case that is merely fanciful, but the court must not conduct a mini-trial. It may critically assess whether the claimant’s case has substance, while asking whether disclosure, cross-examination or fuller factual investigation could materially affect the result (*Swain v Hillman* [2001] 1 All ER 91 (CA); *Easyair Ltd v Opal Telecom Ltd* [2009] EWHC 339 (Ch), [2009] BCC 700). Here, summary judgment is stronger if the relevant contract documents are complete and show that an essential term, breach or loss case cannot succeed. It is weaker if formation, meaning, oral communications or performance depend on contested evidence.

### Application, evidence and response

The application should identify whether it seeks strike out, summary judgment or both in the alternative, specify the exact rules and attach a draft order. For summary judgment, the application notice must identify the point of law or document relied on, include or attach the applicant’s written evidence, state the no-real-prospect position and say that no reason is known why trial is required. The respondent must be told of the right to oppose with evidence (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, r 24.5).

The defendant should exhibit the contract, incorporated terms, relevant correspondence and any admissions, but avoid inviting the court to resolve witness credibility. The claimant should be given the required notice and opportunity to respond. Current Part 24 ordinarily requires at least 14 days’ notice of the hearing; respondent evidence is generally due at least seven days before it and applicant reply evidence at least three days before it (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, rr 24.4–24.5).

The possible outcomes include dismissal of the application, strike out of all or part of the statement of case, summary judgment on a claim or issue, permission to amend, a conditional order, or further case-management directions. The court may give directions rather than enter final judgment where that better serves justice (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, rr 3.1, 3.4 and 24.6).

### Costs and practical assessment

Costs are discretionary. The usual starting point is that the unsuccessful party pays the successful party’s costs, but the court considers conduct, partial success and proportionality. An overbroad application that should have been resolved through clarification or amendment may attract an adverse costs order even if it identifies a genuine pleading defect (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, r 44.2).

The defendant’s best immediate step is therefore to identify the precise legal defect and invite a focused correction before applying, unless the defect is incurable or urgent disposal is justified. Strike out is strongest against a legally defective pleading; summary judgment is strongest where the complete evidence shows no realistic route to success and no material reason for trial.
