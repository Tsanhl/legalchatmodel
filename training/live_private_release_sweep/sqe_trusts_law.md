**Single best answer: the every-effort rule is most relevant—the transfer may be effective in equity once T has done everything required of T to transfer the shares (*Re Rose* [1952] Ch 499).**

Equity normally will not perfect an imperfect gift. The recognised qualification is that, where the transferor has executed the proper transfer and put it beyond the transferor’s control so that only third-party registration remains, equity treats the beneficial transfer as complete (*Re Rose* [1952] Ch 499).

The conclusion depends on what “everything within T’s power” means. If T retained a required form, could countermand the process, or failed to deliver the documents through the prescribed route, the rule may not apply. Registration remains necessary to complete legal title, but R can hold the benefit for B once the equitable transfer is complete.

The broader “unconscionability” reasoning in Pennington is not the best first answer because the facts expressly point to the narrower every-effort rule (*Pennington v Waine* [2002] EWCA Civ 227, [2002] 1 WLR 2075).
