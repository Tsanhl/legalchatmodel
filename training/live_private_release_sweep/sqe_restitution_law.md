**Single best answer: change of position is the relevant defence.**

A payment made under a fundamental mistake may prima facie be recoverable in unjust enrichment. A recipient who, in good faith and because of the receipt, has irreversibly changed position may have the restitutionary claim reduced or defeated to the extent that repayment would be inequitable (*Lipkin Gorman v Karpnale Ltd* [1991] 2 AC 548).

The defence is fact-sensitive and generally operates pro tanto, not automatically as an all-or-nothing bar. D must connect the irreversible expenditure or other detriment to the receipt. Ordinary spending that would have occurred anyway is insufficient, and bad faith after learning of the mistake prevents reliance on later changes.

Estoppel and bona fide purchase are weaker answers on these facts: the question expressly identifies an irreversible good-faith change following receipt, which is the classic change-of-position defence.
