The court follows the offence-specific guideline, then applies guilty-plea credit, aggravating and mitigating factors, totality and the statutory purposes of sentencing. “First-time offender” is mitigating context, not an automatic right to a non-custodial sentence; the offence category and custody threshold remain decisive.

### Offence category and starting point

The judge first identifies the applicable Sentencing Council guideline and determines culpability and harm from the proved or admitted facts. That produces a category starting point and range. The court must not aggravate sentence on disputed facts without resolving them fairly, and the factual basis of plea should be agreed or determined where material.

The court then adjusts within the range for statutory and guideline aggravating and mitigating factors. Planning, abuse of trust, vulnerability and relevant previous convictions may aggravate. Genuine remorse, age or maturity, mental or physical condition linked to offending, steps to address behaviour, caring responsibilities and a previously good character may mitigate where evidenced. A pre-sentence report can be important to risk, rehabilitation and the feasibility of a community order.

### Guilty plea

A guilty plea reduces sentence according to its timing and circumstances (Sentencing Act 2020, s 73). Under the guilty-plea guideline, the maximum reduction is generally one-third for a plea indicated at the first stage of proceedings, falling as the case progresses and ordinarily reaching one-tenth on the first day of trial. The reduction is applied to the punitive sentence, not by changing the offence category, and it does not erase ancillary orders.

### Custody, totality and reasons

A custodial sentence may be imposed only where the offence is so serious that neither a fine nor community sentence can be justified (Sentencing Act 2020, s 230). If custody is unavoidable, the court considers whether it can lawfully be suspended, taking account of risk, rehabilitation, personal mitigation and the realistic requirements available.

Where several offences or an existing sentence are involved, the totality principle requires an overall sentence that is just and proportionate, using concurrent or consecutive terms as appropriate. The judge must give reasons, explain credit and address mandatory ancillary orders, compensation, surcharge, costs and any disqualification.

The offender should provide a verified chronology of the plea, references addressing character rather than verdict, medical and caring evidence, employment consequences, compensation or reparation, and a realistic rehabilitation plan. Counsel should calculate the category, range and plea adjustment explicitly and identify any disputed prosecution assertion before the hearing.
