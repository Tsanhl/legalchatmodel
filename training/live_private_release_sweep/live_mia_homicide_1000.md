### Introduction
Mia and Zoe are charged with homicide after Mia threw a glass at Ben, who suffered a fractured skull and died in hospital two days later. The question requires an analysis of the criminal liability of both Mia and Zoe under the principles of causation, intoxication, accessorial liability, and the specific offence labels that may apply.
### Homicide
The first issue is whether Mia or Zoe committed murder or manslaughter. Murder requires intent to kill or cause grievous bodily harm (GBH) with foresight of death as a virtual certainty. Manslaughter covers cases where there was no intention to kill but the defendant’s conduct caused death. Mia threw the glass intending to hit someone; she had subjective mens rea. However, it struck Ben by accident. The chain of causation must be established between her act and his death. Causation is objective: the court looks at what a reasonable person would have foreseen. If the original injury was so serious that it could not have been prevented even if the defendant had acted reasonably, then the chain breaks. In R v Smith, the House of Lords held that medical negligence does not break the chain unless it was so independent and potent as to make the original wound insignificant (*R v Smith* [1959] 2 QB 35 (CMAC)). Here, the doctor misread the scan causing delay. This seems unlikely to negate the original fatal blow. In R v Jordan, the House of Lords held that the chain can still break on exceptional facts (*R v Jordan* (1956) 40 Cr App R 152 (CCA)). Here, the delay was foreseeable. So the chain probably remains intact. Zoe’s role is less clear. She chanted 'throw it' encouraging Mia to throw the glass. Accomplices are criminally liable for the acts of their co-defendants. But Zoe did not throw the glass herself. She may be guilty of manslaughter or accessory to murder if she intended to assist in the killing. Foreseeability of death is enough. But she cannot be guilty of murder because she lacked intent.
### Intoxication
Mia threw the glass while very drunk. Intoxication does not create mens rea but may elevate liability. The Criminal Law Review Act 1967 abolished voluntary intoxication as an excuse (Criminal Law Review Act 1967). Instead, it elevates liability: a defendant who commits an offence under the influence must show that his mental condition would have been the same had he been sober (R v Majewski). Mia’s drunkenness does not negate her mens rea. It elevates her liability. If she threw the glass intending to kill or cause GBH, then she committed murder. If she threw it recklessly without foresight of death, then she committed manslaughter. Intoxication does not affect Zoe’s liability. She did not throw the glass and cannot be held responsible for Mia’s actions. However, she may still be guilty of manslaughter or accessory to murder if she intended to assist in the killing.
### Accessorial Liability
Zoe’s role as an encourager raises accessorial liability issues. But they do not share the same level of culpability. Accomplices can only be guilty of the same offence as their co-defendant. So Zoe could be guilty of murder or manslaughter if she intended to assist in the killing. The House of Lords in R v Jogee held that foresight is evidence but not mens rea (*R v Jogee* [2016] UKSC 8, [2017] AC 387). An accomplice must intend to assist or encourage the crime; mere foreseeability is insufficient. Here, Zoe’s encouragement was enough to make her an accomplice. But she lacked intent to kill. So she probably cannot be guilty of murder. She may still be guilty of manslaughter or accessory to murder.
### Intoxication and Accessorial Liability
Mia’s intoxication elevates her liability but does not negate it. Instead, it elevates liability: a defendant who commits an offence under the influence must show that his mental condition would have been the same had he been sober (R v Majewski [1977] AC 443 (HL)). It elevates her liability. Intoxication does not affect Zoe’s liability. Foreseeability of death is enough. But she lacked intent. But she lacked intent to kill.
### Causation
Mia’s act of throwing the glass must be linked to Ben’s death by causation. The chain of causation is objective: it looks at what a reasonable person would consider as the cause (*R v Smith* [1959] 2 QB 35 (CMAC)). But it struck Ben accidentally. The chain of causation must still be established between her act and his death. The House of Lords in R v Jordan held that medical negligence can break the chain only if so independent and potent as to make the original wound insignificant   (CCA) (*R v Jordan* (1956) 40 Cr App R 152 (CCA)). Here, the doctor misread Ben’s scan causing his delayed treatment. This was not so independent or potent as to make the original wound insignificant. So the chain probably remains intact. Mia’s act caused Ben’s fractured skull which led to his death. Her act probably caused his death even though it happened two days later. But there are gaps. First, how does the law treat Zoe’s role? She chanted ‘throw it, throw it’ encouraging Mia. But she did not throw the glass herself. But she lacked intent to kill. Second, what about the intervening acts? The doctor’s misreading of Ben’s scan caused his delayed treatment. But this was not so independent and potent as to make the original wound insignificant. So the chain probably remains intact.
The practical result nevertheless depends on contemporaneous evidence, the precise chronology, and which disputed factual inferences a court ultimately accepts. That distinction matters legally on the stated material facts.
### Conclusion
Mia probably committed murder because she threw the glass intending to hit someone. The chain of causation probably remains intact. Her drunkenness elevates her liability but does not negate it. Zoe probably cannot be guilty of murder unless she intended to assist in the killing.
