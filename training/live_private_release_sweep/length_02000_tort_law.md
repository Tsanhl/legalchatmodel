### Introduction
The problem raises several interrelated issues of negligence law. The primary question is whether Priya, a junior doctor at the Royal Northern Hospital, owed Tom, an inpatient, a duty of care to read his scan accurately and discharge him safely. If she did owe such a duty, it must be assessed whether her breach of that duty caused Tom's stroke, which in turn led to Una developing PTSD after witnessing Tom's collapse. Finally, the employer's loss due to Tom's injury must also be evaluated for recoverability.
### Duty of Care
#### Existence of a Duty
Firstly, it needs to be determined whether Priya owed Tom a legal duty of care. In clinical negligence cases, the existence of a duty of care can often be established by reference to Bolam/Bolitho principles (*Bolam v Friern Hospital Management Committee* [1957] 1 WLR 582 (QB); *Bolitho v City and Hackney Health Authority* [1998] AC 232 (HL)). These principles hold that a medical professional will not be found negligent merely because another reasonable practitioner would have acted differently. Instead, the court should consider what a body of skilled professionals would accept as acceptable practice under similar circumstances. In this case, Priya was acting within her role as a junior doctor. She had called upon her consultant to review the scan, which is consistent with Bolam/Bolitho principles. The fact that she was a junior doctor does not automatically negate the existence of a duty of care. However, it may be argued that her inexperience and lack of senior oversight could reduce the scope of her duties or even eliminate them entirely if she failed to recognise her own limitations and seek further advice proactively. The Royal Northern Hospital's policy on junior doctors' responsibilities must also be considered. If there are clear guidelines stating that junior doctors must call their consultants when they encounter scans requiring expert interpretation, then Priya would likely owe Tom a higher standard of care than if such guidance were absent.
#### Scope of the Duty
Even assuming a duty exists, it needs to be assessed whether it extended to Tom's discharge process. Discharge procedures typically involve multiple steps beyond simply reading a scan, including assessing patient stability, ensuring adequate follow-up plans, and communicating effectively with other healthcare providers involved in the patient's care. Given that Tom suffered a stroke shortly after his discharge, it can be argued that Priya's failure to properly assess his condition before discharge breached an established duty of care. This breach would need to be evaluated under Bolam/Bolitho principles by considering what reasonable medical professionals would have done in similar circumstances.
### Breach of Duty
Once the existence and scope of the duty are established, the next step is to determine whether Priya breached that duty. The objective standard of care required of a doctor in taking a patient's history should be applied here (*Nettleship v Weston* [1971] 2 QB 691 (CA)). A learner doctor like Priya may still be held to this standard unless she demonstrates that her actions fell within an acceptable range of professional practice. In this case, Priya misread the scan, which led to Tom being discharged prematurely. If it can be shown that a competent junior doctor would not have made the same mistake given the same information, then Priya likely breached her duty of care. However, if there was no clear consensus among experts about how to interpret the scan correctly, then Priya's actions might fall within an acceptable range of professional opinion.
### Causation
Assuming a breach of duty has been established, causation must be assessed. Factual causation asks whether but-for Priya's actions, Tom would not have suffered his stroke. In this scenario, it seems likely that Priya's premature discharge directly contributed to Tom's condition deteriorating rapidly after leaving hospital. However, factual causation alone does not necessarily establish legal responsibility. Legal causation requires considering whether the kind of harm caused by Priya's actions was reasonably foreseeable. Given that strokes are known risks associated with certain medical conditions and treatments, it is arguable that the kind of harm suffered by Tom was indeed foreseeable. The foreseeability of the exact sequence or extent of harm, however, remains less clear. The Wagon Mound (No 1) [1961] AC 388 establishes that only the kind of damage needs to be foreseeable, not every possible outcome. Therefore, while it may be difficult to predict precisely when Tom would suffer a stroke, it can still be argued that the kind of harm he experienced was foreseeable.
### Psychiatric Injury
Una witnessed Tom's collapse and subsequently developed PTSD. To assess whether Priya owed Una a separate duty of care, her relationship to Tom as well as the nature of her injury must be considered. In cases involving secondary victims like Una, the Alcock test applies (*Alcock v Chief Constable of South Yorkshire Police* [1992] 1 AC 310 (HL)). This test requires showing close ties between the defendant's conduct and the plaintiff's injury, proximity in time and space, and direct perception of the event causing the injury. Given that Una witnessed Tom's collapse directly, she likely meets these criteria. However, the question arises whether her psychiatric condition qualifies as an accident caused by Priya's actions. Paul v Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust sets out stringent controls for establishing such liability, emphasizing the need for a sufficiently independent act breaking the chain of causation (*Paul v Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust* [2024] UKSC 1, [2024] 2 WLR 417). The employer's loss due to Tom's injury raises questions about economic loss recoverability. Pure economic loss is generally not recoverable in negligence unless there are special circumstances justifying its inclusion within the scope of the defendant's duties. The Murphy v Brentwood DC case establishes that pure economic loss arising from damage to another's property is irrecoverable absent an assumption of responsibility or other exceptional factor.
#### Bolam/Bolitho Principles
The standard of care for junior doctors, as outlined in *Wilsher v Essex County Council*, is not necessarily lower than that expected from more senior colleagues. Junior doctors are expected to train 'on the job' and their actions should be judged by an objective standard rather than a subjective one based on personal experience or lack thereof (*Wilsher*). This means that Priya's actions must be assessed against what would be considered reasonable practice within her field. In applying Bolam/Bolitho principles, it is crucial to consider whether Priya's interpretation of Tom's scan was within the range of acceptable professional opinion. If she can demonstrate that her reading was consistent with the views of other competent professionals, then she may avoid liability even if it later turns out to have been incorrect. The key here lies in establishing that her actions were not unreasonable given the information available at discharge.
#### Scope of Professional Practice
The scope of Priya's duties extends beyond merely diagnosing and treating patients; it also includes ensuring safe patient discharge. In cases like Nettleship v Weston), where a learner driver negligently injured an instructor, the Court of Appeal held that drivers are judged by the objective standard of the reasonably competent driver (*Nettleship v Weston* [1971] 2 QB 691 (CA)). This principle suggests that Priya owed Tom a duty to ensure his safe discharge, which she breached by discharging him despite recognizing potential risks. However, it is important to note that Bolam/Bolitho principles do not automatically equate to negligence. A junior doctor who calls their consultant upon recognizing their own limitations has discharged their duty, as responsibility will move to the more experienced colleague (*Wilsher*). Therefore, Priya must show that she did not breach this established category of care before considering whether her actions fell outside the expected range of practice.
#### Counterargument: Public Policy Considerations
A counterargument to Priya's defence based on Bolam/Bolitho principles could be rooted in public policy considerations. If Priya's actions led to significant harm, it might be argued that the current system inadequately protects patients from negligent practices by junior doctors. The Scottish case of Andrews v Greater Glasgow Health Board ([2015] UKSC 11) highlights concerns about the harshness of legal positions towards junior health professionals. While Bolam/Bolitho principles provide some protection for medical practitioners, they may also perpetuate an environment where junior staff feel pressured to act without adequate support or guidance. In conclusion, Priya's liability hinges significantly on demonstrating that her actions were within the acceptable range of professional practice under Bolam/Bolitho principles. However, the broader context of public safety and trust in healthcare systems should also inform any assessment of her conduct.

The key question here is whether Priya's actions in interpreting the scan fell within this range. In clinical negligence cases, it has been held that junior doctors are expected to call upon their senior colleagues when they recognize their own limitations or uncertainty about a diagnosis or treatment plan. This principle reflects the need for continuous learning and supervision in healthcare professions, especially where patients' lives may be at stake (*Andrews v Greater Glasgow NHS Board*). If Priya recognized her inexperience but did not seek input from more senior staff before discharging Tom, she could argue that she discharged her duty by seeking expert advice. However, the scope of the duty must also be considered beyond mere consultation. In Nettleship v Weston), the Court of Appeal established that even learners driving under supervision can still owe a duty of care to third parties (*Nettleship v Weston* [1971] 2 QB 691 (CA)). Applying this objective standard to Priya's conduct, it would seem that she owed Tom a duty to ensure his safe discharge based on an accurate assessment of his condition. Failure to do so would constitute a breach of that duty. The Bolam/Bolitho test allows for flexibility in determining what constitutes acceptable practice.
### Conclusion
#### Advising Priya
Priya should seek to demonstrate that her actions fell within an acceptable range of professional practice under Bolam/Bolitho principles. If she can show that her interpretation of the scan was reasonable given the information available at discharge, then she may avoid liability entirely. Alternatively, if she can establish that her breach did not cause Tom's stroke but merely contributed to it, she might still be found liable only for the proportionate share of damages attributable to her actions.
#### Advising Tom and Una
Tom likely has a strong claim against Priya based on factual causation alone. However, legal causation will require proving that the kind of harm suffered by Tom was reasonably foreseeable. If this can be established, then Priya would owe Tom a duty of care breached by her premature discharge. Una also has grounds to sue Priya under Alcock principles. Her direct perception of Tom's collapse meets the criteria set out by the test. Whether her PTSD qualifies as an accident caused by Priya's actions depends heavily on whether any intervening act sufficiently independent and potent broke the chain of causation. Given the stringent controls in Paul v Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust, it seems unlikely that Una's claim will succeed unless there is clear evidence of such an event occurring after Tom's discharge (*Paul v Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust* [2024] UKSC 1, [2024] 2 WLR 417).
#### Advising Tom's Employer
Pure economic loss arising from damage to another's property is generally irrecoverable absent an assumption of responsibility or other exceptional factor. The Murphy case establishes that such losses are not automatically compensable even if they arise directly from negligence causing personal injury. Therefore, Tom's employer should carefully consider whether any special circumstances justify including their business losses within the scope of Priya's duties before pursuing a claim.
