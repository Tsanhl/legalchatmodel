### Introduction
Parliamentary sovereignty, the cornerstone of the UK constitution, has been qualified by the rule of law. The Human Rights Act 1998 (HRA) and EU membership have significantly altered the landscape of constitutional governance (Human Rights Act 1998). Parliamentary sovereignty remains supreme within the domestic legal system but is now subject to the rule of law, which requires that laws must be compatible with fundamental human rights as enshrined in the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR). This essay critically examines how parliamentary sovereignty interacts with the HRA, EU membership, Jackson v Attorney General, Miller No 1, Miller No 2/Cherry, Privacy International, and devolution. The HRA introduced a mechanism for directly incorporating ECHR provisions into English law. Section 3(1) states: "In so far as it is possible to do so, primary legislation and subordinate legislation must be read and given effect in a way which is compatible with the Convention rights." However, this compatibility test does not automatically invalidate incompatible legislation; rather, it requires Parliament to interpret existing laws in a manner consistent with the Convention rights. The extent of this flexibility depends on the nature of the legislation at issue. For example, in Bank Mellat v HM Treasury, the Supreme Court held that financial restrictions imposed by the government were proportionate under Article 8 because they were necessary to protect national security interests (*Bank Mellat v HM Treasury (No 2)* [2013] UKSC 39, [2014] AC 700). (Bank Mellat)
EU membership has further qualified parliamentary sovereignty. Under the European Communities Act 1972 (ECA), EU law takes precedence over conflicting domestic statutes (European Communities Act 1972). In Factortame No 2, the House of Lords confirmed that when an EU directive conflicts with a UK statute, the latter must be disapplied while the former applies. This principle was reaffirmed in Miller No 1 [2017] UKSC 5, where the Supreme Court held that prerogative powers cannot remove statutory rights conferred by Parliament. Jackson v Attorney General and Privacy International v Investigatory Powers Tribunal illustrate how the rule of law can limit parliamentary sovereignty. Jackson involved a challenge to the Hunting Act 2004 on the grounds that it violated Article 10 of the ECHR (Hunting Act 2004). The Privy Council upheld the Act but expressed reservations about the absolute nature of parliamentary sovereignty. Similarly, Privacy International challenged an ouster clause preventing judicial review of decisions made by the Investigatory Powers Tribunal. The Supreme Court held that such clauses are not protected by the HRA unless they serve fundamental constitutional principles like access to justice. Devolution further complicates the relationship between parliamentary sovereignty and the rule of law. Under the Sewel Convention, Westminster cannot legislate for devolved matters without consent. However, in Thoburn v Sunderland CC, the House of Lords held that constitutional statutes are not impliedly repealable by subordinate legislation. This principle was reaffirmed in AXA General Insurance v Lord Advocate, where the Supreme Court confirmed that Acts of the Scottish Parliament are not reviewable on common-law grounds save for fundamental constitutional principles. Thus, while parliamentary sovereignty remains supreme within each jurisdiction, it is subject to the rule of law at both the domestic and devolved levels.

#### Operation of the Human Rights Act 1998
The HRA requires that all legislation be interpreted consistently with the ECHR unless it is incompatible. Section 3(1) states: "In so far as it is possible to do so, primary legislation must be read and given effect in a way which is compatible with the Convention right." Section 4(1) extends this requirement to subordinate legislation. However, section 2(1) allows for derogations from certain rights under Article 15, which can only be made when there are exceptional circumstances affecting the nation's life or security. The HRA also introduces a new remedy mechanism. Section 7 enables individuals to seek judicial review of public bodies' actions if they have been engaged by the state. Section 8 empowers courts to make declarations of incompatibility where an enactment breaches a convention right. Importantly, section 10(1) provides that such declarations are not binding on other tribunals or courts. The HRA thus creates a dual system of remedies — direct relief through judicial review and indirect relief via declarations of incompatibility (Human Rights Act 1998).

The Court of Appeal held that the freezing measures were incompatible with Article 1 Protocol 1 ECHR because they lacked sufficient clarity as to their scope. The Supreme Court agreed but found that the measures could still be justified under Article 17 ECHR. This example demonstrates how the HRA requires clear legislative drafting while allowing for proportionate restrictions on fundamental rights. Another illustrative example is R (Nicklinson) v Ministry of Justice (*R (Nicklinson) v Ministry of Justice* [2014] UKSC 38, [2015] AC 657). Nicklinson sought judicial review of the decision not to grant him assisted dying. The House of Lords held that the decision was engaged by Article 8 ECHR but declined to make a declaration of incompatibility. Instead, it emphasised the separation of powers between the judiciary and legislature. This case highlights the HRA’s role in balancing individual rights against state autonomy.
