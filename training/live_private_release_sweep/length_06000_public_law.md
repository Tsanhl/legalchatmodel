Substantive rights include the right to life, liberty, privacy, family life, education, work, property, free movement, asylum, and non-discrimination. Substantive review examines the compatibility of legislation with the ECHR, while procedural review assesses the fairness of the decision-making process.

The Human Rights Act 1998 (HRA) introduced the European Convention on Human Rights into English law (Human Rights Act 1998). It requires that all public bodies must interpret their powers consistently with the Convention rights unless it would be incompatible to do so. The HRA thus provides a mechanism for individuals to challenge actions of public authorities that are inconsistent with the Convention.
#### Operation of the Human Rights Act 1998
The operation of the HRA is governed by section 2(1), which states: "In so far as it is possible to do so, primary legislation and subordinate legislation must be read and given effect in a way which is compatible with the Convention rights." This means that if an Act or regulation can be interpreted in a manner consistent with the Convention rights, then it should be done. However, section 3 allows the courts to declare that certain provisions are incompatible with the Convention rights. Such declarations are not binding on the legislature but they are capable of influencing future legislative drafting. The HRA also provides a remedy through judicial review. Under section 4, the High Court has jurisdiction to grant relief against any act or omission by a public authority that is unlawful because it is incompatible with the Convention rights. The remedy available under section 4 includes quashing the impugned decision, declaring the decision invalid, prohibiting the public authority from acting unlawfully again, and making such other order as the court thinks fit. Importantly, the HRA does not provide a private right of action; only the courts have the power to enforce the Convention rights (Human Rights Act 1998).
#### Examples of human rights issues arising under the Human Rights Act 1998
The operation of the HRA has led to numerous cases challenging the actions of public authorities. For example, in Bank Mellat v HM Treasury, the Supreme Court held that financial restrictions imposed on Bank Mellat were proportionate (*Bank Mellat v HM Treasury (No 2)* [2013] UKSC 39, [2014] AC 700). The Court engaged in a structured proportionality analysis: first, identifying the legitimate aim pursued by the measure; second, determining whether there was a rational connection between the means employed and the aim sought to be achieved; third, assessing whether the measure was necessary in a democratic society; and fourth, evaluating whether the measure was proportionate to the aim. The Court found that the measures were proportionate because they were designed to prevent the financing of terrorism, which was a pressing social need, and the measures were necessary and proportionate to achieve that aim. Another example is Privacy International v Investigatory Powers Tribunal (IPt). In this case, the IPt had refused to disclose certain documents to Privacy International. The House of Lords held that the IPt's decision was not protected by an ouster clause. The Court reasoned that the IPt's decision-making process involved the exercise of discretion, which could be subject to judicial review. The decision highlights the importance of the rule of law principle underpinning the HRA — access to justice must be preserved even where public bodies are empowered to make decisions that engage Convention rights (*Privacy International v Investigatory Powers Tribunal* [2019] UKSC 22, [2020] AC 491).
#### Doctrinal developments arising from the Human Rights Act 1998
The operation of the HRA has led to significant doctrinal developments. One key area concerns the interpretation of the Convention rights. For instance, in R (Ullah) v Special Adjudicator, the House of Lords held that the right to a fair trial under Article 6 required that the Special Adjudicator's decision should be made on the basis of evidence adduced at the hearing (*R (Ullah) v Special Adjudicator* [2004] UKHL 26, [2004] 2 AC 323). This ruling established that the Convention rights can impose substantive obligations on public authorities beyond the mere absence of legal error. Another example is AXA General Insurance v Lord Advocate.

These developments reflect the ongoing evolution of human rights law in response to changing social conditions and the need to balance individual freedoms with the demands of democratic governance.

#### Foundations of EU law: Supremacy and Direct Effect
The supremacy of EU law over national law is a fundamental principle established by the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU). In Factortame No 2 \[\], the CJEU held that the Merchant Shipping Act conflicted with EU law because it gave the UK courts the power to disapply the Act while EU law applied. This decision demonstrated the primacy of EU law within the legal system. The CJEU's reasoning was based on the fact that the European Communities Act 1972 (ECA) had created a gateway into the domestic legal system, allowing EU law to take precedence over conflicting national provisions (European Communities Act 1972). The ECA effectively made EU law part of the UK legal order, subject only to the principles of the rule of law. This principle of supremacy has been further reinforced by the Miller cases. In R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union \[\], the Supreme Court held that ministers cannot trigger Article 50 without parliamentary authority (*R v Secretary of State for the Home Department, ex p Doody* [1994] 1 AC 531 (HL); *R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union* [2017] UKSC 5, [2018] AC 61). The court reasoned that the prerogative powers are limited by the sovereignty of Parliament, as expressed in the ECA. Similarly, in R (Jackson) v Attorney General \[\], the House of Lords considered whether the Hunting Act could be challenged through the Parliament Acts (*R (Jackson) v Attorney General* [2005] UKHL 56, [2006] 1 AC 262). Lord Steyn and Lord Hope raised doubts about the absolute nature of parliamentary sovereignty but ultimately concluded that the Acts were valid. These decisions highlight the tension between parliamentary sovereignty and the rule of law, which must be balanced against the need for democratic legitimacy and accountability.
