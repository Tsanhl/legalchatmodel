The second issue is certainty of objects. Certainty of objects means the beneficiaries must be ascertainable at the date of death. This does not mean they must be named, but they must be capable of being identified. For example, in *Knight v Knight* [1840] 3 Beav 148, 49 ER 58, the court held that three certainties were required: clear intention, subject matter, and objects (*Knight v Knight* (1840) 3 Beav 148, 49 ER 58). The first £500,000 disposition should be scrutinised for certainty of objects. If it fails, the beneficial interest goes to Asher’s personal representatives under intestacy rules.
#### Purpose trusts
Purpose trusts are governed by the Trustee Act 2000, s 1(1) (Trustee Act 2000, s 1(1)). A purpose trust arises when an individual transfers property to trustees to achieve a particular purpose. The purpose must be certain, legal, and capable of being achieved. The testator must have intended to create a purpose trust, which can often be inferred from the language used. In *Re Weston's Settlements / Goulding v James* (CA), the court approved variations affecting protected beneficiaries if the benefit was broadly understood (*Re Weston's Settlements* [1969] 1 Ch 223 (CA)). The £100,000 cricket advancement is likely to be valid as a purpose trust because it meets these criteria. Secret trusts arise where the settlor intends to keep the existence of the trust confidential. Half-secret trusts involve some disclosure but not full public knowledge. The secret or half-secret nature of the disposition affects its validity. The first £500,000 disposition should be scrutinised for secret or half-secret status.
#### Breach and tracing
The final issue is breach and tracing. Breach occurs when a trustee breaches their fiduciary duty. Fiduciaries owe a no-profit/no-conflict rule, which requires them to avoid conflicts of interest and refrain from making profits out of the trust. In *Boardman v Phipps*, the Supreme Court held that trustees must account even if they acted in good faith (*Boardman v Phipps* [1967] 2 AC 46 (HL)). The certainty of objects test is a fundamental principle in trust law, requiring that the settlor must specify who will benefit from the trust property with sufficient clarity to enable the trustee to identify the beneficiaries. This test ensures that the trust can be enforced by the intended beneficiaries and distinguishes between clear and vague dispositions. The certainty of objects test has been refined over time through leading cases such as *McPhail v Doulton* and *Re Adams and the Kensington Vestry* (CA) (*Re Adams and the Kensington Vestry* (1884) 27 Ch D 394 (CA); *McPhail v Doulton* [1971] AC 424 (HL)). In this section, we will explore the foundations, definitions, and leading authority on the certainty of objects test.
### Foundations of Certainty of Objects Test
The certainty of objects test was first established by Lord Blackburn in *McPhail v Doulton*, where he stated: "There must be some ascertainable person or persons for whose benefit the property is held." This requirement ensures that the trust can be enforced by the intended beneficiaries (*McPhail v Doulton* [1971] AC 424 (HL)). The certainty of objects test requires not only that the objects are ascertainable but also capable of being ascertained at the date of creation of the trust. In other words, the class of beneficiaries must be defined so precisely that it can be determined whether any particular individual falls within it at the point when the trust comes into existence (*Re Adams and the Kensington Vestry* (1884) 27 Ch D 394 (CA)).
### Definitions of Certainty of Objects Test
The certainty of objects test has been further refined over time through various cases. For example, in *Re Adams and the Kensington Vestry* (CA), it was held that the objects must not only be ascertainable but also capable of being ascertained at the date of the creation of the trust (*Re Adams and the Kensington Vestry* (1884) 27 Ch D 394 (CA)). This means that the settlor cannot create an indefinite class of beneficiaries; instead, they must define the class so precisely that it can be determined whether any particular individual falls within it at the point when the trust comes into existence. In more recent cases, the certainty of objects test has been applied to modern discretionary trusts (MDTs). These are trusts created under letters of wishes, which allow the settlor to direct how their assets should be distributed after death without specifying exact beneficiaries. The leading authority on MDTs is *Saunders v Vautier* 4 Beav 115, 49 ER 282 (*Saunders v Vautier* (1841) 4 Beav 115, 49 ER 282). Here, Lord Denning MR explained that a letter of wishes does not create a binding obligation on the trustee to follow its terms unless the settlor intends it to do so. Therefore, while letters of wishes can provide guidance for trustees, they do not automatically confer enforceable rights on the persons mentioned therein.
### Certainty of Objects Test in Purpose Trusts
The certainty of objects test also applies to purpose trusts, where the beneficiaries are not individuals but rather defined purposes or causes. In such cases, the court will look beyond the wording of the trust document to determine what the settlor intended by the reference to these purposes. For example, in *Re Astor's Settlement Trusts*, the Court of Appeal held that the words "for the advancement of education" were sufficiently certain to support a purpose trust, even though no particular educational institution was named (*Re Astor's Settlement Trusts* [1952] Ch 534).
### Certainty of Objects Test in Secret and Half-Secret Trusts
When dealing with secret or half-secret trusts, the certainty of objects test must still be satisfied. A secret trust arises when the settlor transfers property to another person under an agreement that the recipient holds the property on trust for someone else.
In conclusion, the certainty of objects test plays a crucial role in determining whether a trust has been properly constituted. It requires clear identification of who will benefit from the trust property at the time of creation. While letters of wishes and purpose trusts can provide useful guidance, they do not automatically confer enforceable rights unless the settlor’s intention is clear. Secret or half-secret trusts also require evidence of the true intention regarding beneficial ownership. Formalities must be observed to ensure the disposition complies with statutory requirements. Failure to meet these criteria can result in the trust failing, leaving the settlor as the ultimate beneficiary.
### Certainty of objects — operation, examples and doctrinal development
The certainty requirement is a fundamental principle in trust law. The terms must be precise enough for the trustees to know what they must do; if uncertain, the intended trust fails   (3 Beav 148, 49 ER 58) (*Knight v Knight* (1840) 3 Beav 148, 49 ER 58). This certainty applies to both the subject matter and the objects of the trust.
#### Operation: Examples and Doctrinal Development
Certainty of objects requires that the beneficiaries are ascertainable at the time of creation. If not, the trust fails unless an exception applies. For example, in Re Rose, the court held equity may treat a transfer as effective where the transferor has done everything required, provided it would be unconscionable to deny the transfer because the recipient was unknown or unascertainable (*Re Rose* [1952] Ch 499 (CA)). This reflects the equitable nature of trusts, which seeks to give effect to the settlor's intention even when formalities fail. In contrast, Re Weston’s Settlements illustrates the strictness of the certainty rule (*Re Weston's Settlements* [1969] 1 Ch 223 (CA)). Here, the court refused to approve a variation affecting protected beneficiaries without their consent, despite the settlor’s desire. The doctrine of certainty protects vulnerable beneficiaries by ensuring they can enforce the trust terms. The doctrine of certainty also extends to purposes. In Re Astor’s Settlement Trusts, the court enforced a purpose trust for the advancement of education, despite vagueness (*Re Astor's Settlement Trusts* [1952] Ch 534). However, in Re Denley’s Trust Deed, the court declined to enforce a vague purpose, reflecting the principle that purposes must be ascertainable and beneficial (*Re Denley's Trust Deed* [1969] 1 Ch 373).
#### Doctrinal Development: Modern Discretionary Trusts
Modern discretionary trusts (MDTs) have emerged as a response to these traditional certainties. MDTs allow property to be distributed by way of powers of appointment rather than fixed or discretionary distributions under the trustees’ discretion. Letters of wishes and protectors are common features, providing flexibility while maintaining certainty. The Law Commission’s Consultation Paper on Modern Discretionary Trusts (2018) highlighted the doctrinal developments contributing to this shift. It argued that the traditional certainties were too rigid, stifling innovation and adaptability. The paper proposed reforms to clarify how MDTs fit within the existing framework, balancing certainty with flexibility. In practice, MDTs often involve letters of wishes from the settlor, which guide the protector’s exercise of powers. These letters can specify beneficiaries, purposes, or both, ensuring some degree of certainty even if not formalised into the trust deed itself. For example, in Re Lipinski’s Will Trusts, the court enforced a letter of wishes despite its informal nature, recognising it as an effective means of communicating the settlor's intentions (*Re Lipinski's Will Trusts* [1976] Ch 235). However, the enforceability of such arrangements depends heavily on their clarity and precision. In Re Barlow’s Will Trusts, the court refused to enforce a vague letter of wishes, highlighting the importance of clear communication for certainty (*Re Barlow's Will Trusts* [1979] 1 WLR 278).

A purpose trust is an interest in property created for purposes beyond mere personal advantage. The objects of a purpose trust must also be certain. They should not be so broad as to encompass any person or entity. ### Secret/half-secret trust analysis — foundations, definitions and leading authority
Secret trusts are a category of constructive trusts that arise when the settlor’s intention to create a trust is not expressed in writing or communicated to the trustee. The key distinction between secret trusts and half-secret trusts lies in whether the terms of the trust are clear on the face of the will.
#### Definition and Classification
A **fully secret trust** occurs where the settlor does not communicate the trust to the trustee at all, meaning the trustee has no idea that they hold property on trust. Conversely, a **half-secret trust** arises when the settlor communicates the trust to the trustee but the terms of the trust are not clear on the face of the will. In other words, it is only apparent to the trustee after the fact that the property was held on trust. The seminal case for distinguishing between these categories is *McCormick v Grogan* HL 82 (*McCormick v Grogan* (1869) LR 4 HL 82). Lord Blackburn explained that the crucial difference is whether the trustee knows the identity of the beneficiary before the will is executed. If the trustee knows who the beneficiary is, even if the terms of the trust are unclear, it constitutes a half-secret trust. If the trustee remains unaware until after execution, it is a fully secret trust.
#### Leading Authority: McCormick v Grogan (1869)
In *McCormick v Grogan*, the testator left his estate to his son, George, with instructions to distribute it among his friends (*McCormick v Grogan* (1869) LR 4 HL 82). The Court of Appeal held that although George knew about the trust, since he did not know which friends were intended beneficiaries, this constituted a half-secret trust rather than a fully secret one. This decision underscores the importance of communication and clarity in establishing the nature of the trust.
#### Communication and Acceptance
For a half-secret trust to arise, the settlor must communicate the trust to the trustee, but the terms must be ambiguous or unclear enough so that they do not appear on the face of the will. Once communicated, the trustee must then accept the trust, meaning they agree to hold the property on trust despite the lack of clear documentation. The leading case here is *Re Rose* (*Re Rose* [1952] Ch 499 (CA)). In this case, the settlor’s will was silent as to the identity of the beneficiaries, but the executor had been told by the settlor who should benefit. The Court of Appeal held that because the executor accepted the trust based on the settlor’s communications, it was a half-secret trust. However, if the executor had simply followed the terms of the will without being informed of the settlor’s intentions, it would have been a fully secret trust.
#### Fraud Rationale and Wills Act Interaction
Secret trusts are often justified on the basis of preventing fraud. The dehors the will theory posits that the settlor’s true intention can be discerned from extrinsic evidence, while the fraud theory suggests that the settlor intended to conceal their true purpose. Viscount Sumner in *McCormick v Grogan* argued for the fraud theory, stating that the distinction between fully and half-secret trusts is merely formal rather than substantive (*McCormick v Grogan* (1869) LR 4 HL 82).

### Breach and tracing. default to full parenthetical oscola — foundations, definitions and leading authority
**Breach**: A trustee breaches their duty when they act in conflict with the settlor’s purpose or personal interest, or profit from confidential information. The **no-conflict rule** prohibits trustees from using trust property to benefit themselves at the expense of beneficiaries. *Keech v Sandford* (1726) Sel Cas Ch 61, 25 ER 223 establishes that any profit made by a trustee must be accounted for. *Boardman v Phipps* reinforces this by holding that even honest trustees must account for profits (*Boardman v Phipps* [1967] 2 AC 46 (HL)). *Rukhadze v Recovery Partners GP Ltd*, clarifies that the Supreme Court prefers strict disgorgement over an equitable allowance, which would mitigate injustice but undermine single-minded loyalty (*Rukhadze v Recovery Partners GP Ltd* [2025] UKSC 10, [2025] 2 WLR 529). **No-profit rule**: Trustees cannot profit personally from trust assets without accounting. *Boardman* also sets out the no-profit rule, requiring trustees to return any unjust enrichment. *Stevens v Hotel Portfolio II UK Ltd* reiterates that an unauthorised profit held on constructive trust causes compensable loss (*Stevens v Hotel Portfolio II UK Ltd* [2025] UKSC 28). *Hopcraft v Close Brothers Ltd*, restates these principles while defining fiduciary duties more precisely (*Hopcraft v Close Brothers Ltd* [2025] UKSC 33, [2025] 3 WLR 423). **Tracing**: Tracing involves identifying the source of assets to determine their rightful owner. It can be used to recover trust property wrongfully disposed of. *McKeown* [2001] 1 A.C. 102 HL at 127 establishes that tracing is an equitable remedy, not a legal one. *Birks*, ed., *Underhill and Hayton's Law of Trusts and Trustees* (London: LexisNexis, 2010), at para.90.4 explains that tracing requires proof of mixing or confusion of assets. *Millet*, ‘Tracing the proceeds of fraud’ (1991) 107 LQR 71 discusses the complexities of tracing in fraud cases. *Smith*, The Law of Tracing (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1997) provides a comprehensive guide on tracing principles.
