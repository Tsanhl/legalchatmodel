### Severance — foundations, definitions and leading authority
The Law Commission Report No. 254 (1998) outlines that land registration in England and Wales operates under the Land Registration Act 1925, with amendments including the Land Registration Act 1997 and the Land Registration Act 2002 (Land Registration Act 1925). The primary means of severing a joint tenancy at law is by notice served upon one of the joint tenants. Under section 196 of the Law of Property Act 1925, a written notice of severance is deemed served if left at the last known address or place of business of the joint tenant (Law of Property Act 1925). Importantly, service does not require receipt or reading by the joint tenant but merely delivery. This contrasts with the situation where the notice must be registered as required by section 197 for the severance to take effect at equity. In Kinch v Bullard (1999), Mr Johnson wrote to Mrs Johnson informing her he wanted their home sold. The letter was delivered to Mrs Johnson’s home but she had moved out. The court held that since the letter was properly served on Mrs Johnson's last known address, it constituted an effective notice of severance. The case illustrates that service can occur without actual receipt by the joint tenant, provided the notice is delivered. The second method of severance involves mutual agreement between co-owners. Mutual agreement requires clear evidence of intent to sever the joint tenancy. In Burgess v Burgess, an oral agreement to sell the property was sufficient to sever the joint tenancy at law. Conversely, in Gore & Snell v Gore & Snell, divorce negotiations failed because there was no finality to the discussions. These cases highlight the uncertainty surrounding informal arrangements and the risk of litigation when mutual intent is not clearly expressed. The third method of severance arises from a course of dealing. In Hunter v Babbage, a concluded agreement to divide proceeds was enough to sever the joint tenancy. This demonstrates that a formal agreement or even an implied understanding based on conduct over time may suffice for severance. However, the lack of clarity often leads to disputes about whether such agreements were truly intended as severances rather than mere arrangements for sharing profits (Law of Property Act 1925; Law of Property Act 1997; Law of Property Act 2002).
