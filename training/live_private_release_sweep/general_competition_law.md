The retailer has a credible competition-law concern, but parallel prices alone do not prove unlawful coordination. It should preserve the underlying communications and pricing evidence, obtain privileged specialist advice if it may have participated, and distinguish a report to the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) from its own compensatory claim.

### The Chapter I prohibition

The principal rule is the Chapter I prohibition. It catches agreements between undertakings, decisions by associations of undertakings and concerted practices which may affect trade within the United Kingdom and have as their object or effect the prevention, restriction or distortion of competition within the United Kingdom (Competition Act 1998, s 2). “Concerted practice” matters because suppliers need not have signed a formal cartel agreement; evidence of knowing coordination replacing independent commercial conduct may suffice. Parallel pricing by itself is equivocal, however, because competitors may independently react to the same costs or market conditions.

The facts raise two legally distinct possibilities. First, three suppliers may be coordinating horizontally with each other about the prices at which retailers should resell. Horizontal price coordination is especially serious because its object may be restrictive without proof of actual market effects. Secondly, each supplier may separately be imposing vertical **resale price maintenance (RPM)** on the retailer. RPM normally involves requiring or inducing a reseller not to sell below a fixed or minimum price. A genuinely non-binding recommended retail price is not automatically RPM, but pressure, threats, withdrawal of discounts, monitoring, incentives or coordinated complaints may show that the “recommendation” is binding in practice. The CMA’s current retailer guidance treats RPM as a serious competition infringement and explains the distinction between recommended and imposed resale prices ([CMA, ‘Resale price maintenance: advice for retailers’](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/resale-price-maintenance-advice-for-retailers/resale-price-maintenance-advice-for-retailers)).

The section 9 exemption is unlikely to rescue core price coordination unless evidence establishes all four cumulative conditions: efficiencies, a fair share for consumers, indispensability and no elimination of competition (Competition Act 1998, s 9). It should not be assumed merely because a supplier asserts that common prices protect brand image or retailer margins.

### Evidence to preserve

The retailer should preserve original emails, messaging records, meeting notes, price lists, threats to withhold supply, discount conditions, monitoring spreadsheets, complaints about discounting and evidence showing when it independently set prices. Metadata and an unbroken record of collection may later matter. Staff should be told to preserve material, not to rewrite accounts, coordinate stories, contact suspected participants about the complaint or obtain documents unlawfully.

The strongest evidence would connect the suppliers or show that each disclosed future pricing intentions on an expectation of reciprocal conduct. Evidence showing that a supplier policed the retailer’s discounting would support RPM. The retailer should also record lawful alternative explanations, including common wholesale-cost changes, because an allegation based only on price similarity may be weak.

If the retailer or its staff agreed to implement the arrangement, it should seek privileged competition advice before approaching the CMA. Counsel can assess exposure, the current leniency regime and whether an application should be made before another participant reports. The retailer should not destroy evidence or continue coordination while deciding what to do.

### CMA reporting and public enforcement

The retailer may submit its evidence to the CMA, which decides whether and how to investigate according to its statutory powers and priorities. A report does not guarantee a formal investigation or infringement decision. If infringement is established, public enforcement can include directions to end or modify the conduct and financial penalties, subject to the statutory conditions and procedure (Competition Act 1998, ss 32 and 36). The CMA’s role is public enforcement; it does not automatically calculate or pay the retailer’s private loss. The regulator itself distinguishes public enforcement from private actions ([CMA, ‘Private actions and public enforcement’](https://www.gov.uk/government/speeches/private-actions-and-public-enforcement)).

### Private remedies

The retailer may bring a competition claim in the Competition Appeal Tribunal (CAT) under section 47A (Competition Act 1998, s 47A). Depending on the relief and procedure, a High Court claim may also be possible. A claim can be standalone, in which the claimant proves the infringement, or follow-on, relying on a binding infringement finding. A final CMA finding binds the court or CAT on the established infringement within section 58A, but it does not by itself prove the retailer’s individual causation and quantum (Competition Act 1998, s 58A).

Damages are compensatory, not the treble damages associated with United States antitrust law. The retailer must show that the infringement caused recoverable loss, quantify the overcharge or lost margin, and address whether any overcharge was passed to its customers. The Supreme Court has confirmed that pass-on is dealt with as a question of compensatory damages and evidence, avoiding both over- and under-compensation (*Sainsbury’s Supermarkets Ltd v Visa Europe Services LLC* [2020] UKSC 24, [2020] Bus LR 1196). Depending on urgency and proof, declaratory or injunctive relief may also be relevant, particularly if supply threats continue.

### Practical next steps

The retailer should immediately preserve evidence, stop any non-independent pricing conduct, prepare a chronology identifying who communicated what and when, and quantify the commercial impact. It should then obtain privileged competition advice to decide whether to approach the CMA, pursue leniency if exposed, seek urgent relief, or prepare a standalone or follow-on damages claim. Its prospects will turn on evidence of coordination or coercive RPM, not on similar prices alone, and any private recovery will require proof of causation and actual loss.
