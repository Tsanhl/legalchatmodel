**Single best answer: the thin-skull rule applies.**

Once personal injury of the relevant kind is reasonably foreseeable, the defendant takes the claimant as found and remains liable for the full extent of injury caused by an unforeseeable physical vulnerability (*Smith v Leech Brain & Co Ltd* [1962] 2 QB 405). C’s unusually fragile skull therefore does not reduce the careless driver’s liability merely because the severity was unforeseeable.

The ordinary remoteness inquiry still asks whether the kind of damage—personal injury—was reasonably foreseeable (*Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v Morts Dock and Engineering Co Ltd (The Wagon Mound (No 1))* [1961] AC 388). The rule does not make the driver liable for a wholly unforeseeable kind of harm or dispense with factual causation. Here, however, the stated vulnerability affects extent, so thin skull is the best answer.
