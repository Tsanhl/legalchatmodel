Changing award criteria after tenders closed is a serious potential breach because suppliers must compete against disclosed, stable criteria. The bidder should demand the assessment summary and contemporaneous record immediately, send a focused pre-action letter, and protect the 30-day claim period. Proceedings notified during the standstill can prevent contract signature automatically.

### Breach and disclosure

Under the Procurement Act 2023, the authority must assess tenders by reference to the published award criteria and assessment methodology within the statutory scheme (Procurement Act 2023, ss 23 and 31). A post-deadline change can breach transparency and equal treatment, particularly if it altered weight, scoring or the characteristics rewarded rather than merely clarifying a disclosed method.

Before publishing the contract award notice, the authority must give each assessed bidder an assessment summary explaining its own assessment and, where different, that of the most advantageous tender (Procurement Act 2023, s 50). The bidder should request the tender documents, summaries, evaluator guidance, moderation record, conflict declarations and documents showing when and why the alleged change occurred. Disclosure is controlled and may protect genuine confidentiality, but the court can order proportionate disclosure.

### Standstill, suspension and time

Publication of the contract award notice normally starts the statutory standstill, generally eight working days under the Act’s regime (Procurement Act 2023, s 51). The bidder should not wait for full voluntary disclosure before protecting time.

Most claims must be commenced within 30 days from when the supplier first knew or ought to have known of the circumstances, subject to a limited good-reason extension; specified set-aside claims also have a six-month longstop (Procurement Act 2023, s 106). A pre-action letter or complaint does not stop time.

If proceedings are commenced and the authority is notified during an applicable standstill, the authority is automatically prohibited from entering the contract unless the court lifts or modifies the restriction (Procurement Act 2023, s 101). The court considers interim-relief principles, including seriousness, adequacy of damages, balance of convenience and public consequences.

### Remedies and strategy

Before contract entry, the court can set aside a decision, order corrective action or suspend the procedure; damages may be available for causally proven loss (Procurement Act 2023, pt 9). After entry, remedies narrow and statutory set-aside conditions become critical. Loss of a chance must be evidenced rather than assumed.

The bidder should create a scoring comparison showing the published criterion, alleged replacement, affected scores and likely ranking; preserve portal downloads and clarification answers; and instruct procurement counsel immediately. The strongest early objective is often a lawful re-evaluation under the original criteria, but the appropriate remedy depends on whether signature has occurred and whether the change could have affected the outcome.
