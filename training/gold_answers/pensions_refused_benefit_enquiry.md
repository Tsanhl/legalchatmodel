The member should begin with the scheme’s trust deed, rules, amendments, benefit statement and the trustees’ written reasons. The internal dispute resolution procedure (IDRP) is normally the first formal route; the Pensions Ombudsman can then determine disputes of fact or law and complaints of maladministration, while a court may be needed for issues outside that jurisdiction or an appeal on law.

### Rules and trustee decision

Occupational pension benefits are primarily governed by the scheme’s governing documents, read with overriding legislation. The member should identify the exact eligibility, calculation or discretion provision, the applicable version on the relevant date, and any amendment or transfer history. Informal booklets cannot simply override the rules, although inaccurate communications may support maladministration, estoppel or misstatement arguments in an appropriate case.

If the provision gives trustees a discretion, the issue is not whether the Ombudsman or court would have made the same decision. The review asks whether the trustees directed themselves correctly, considered relevant matters, excluded irrelevant matters, acted for a proper purpose and reached a rational decision. If they misunderstood the rule, reconsideration under the correct construction may be required.

### IDRP and Ombudsman

Trustees or managers must maintain an internal dispute resolution procedure for prescribed pension disputes (Pensions Act 1995, s 50). The member should use the scheme’s current form, identify the decision challenged and remedy sought, and attach the governing provision, correspondence, calculations and chronology. A representative may assist, and deadlines in the scheme procedure should be followed.

The Pensions Ombudsman can investigate and determine qualifying complaints of maladministration and disputes of fact or law concerning occupational pension schemes (Pension Schemes Act 1993, pt X). The member will normally be expected to complete IDRP first, though the Ombudsman controls its process. Complaints generally need to be made within the Ombudsman’s time rules, commonly three years from the act or omission or from actual/constructive knowledge, subject to discretion.

The Ombudsman may direct steps, revisit a decision and award compensation for proven injustice within jurisdiction. A final determination is binding and enforceable through the court machinery. An appeal lies to the High Court on a point of law, not as a full rehearing of disputed merits, and has a short deadline.

### Evidence and court issues

The member should obtain all rule versions, trustee minutes relevant to the decision, calculation inputs, medical/employment evidence, disclosure records and comparable decisions where lawfully available. Requests should be targeted and respect confidentiality.

Court proceedings may be appropriate for authoritative construction, rectification, breach of trust, recovery of benefits or an appeal on law. Limitation and costs can differ sharply from the Ombudsman route. The practical next step is a rule-by-rule IDRP submission that separates entitlement under the text, defects in discretionary decision-making, maladministration and financial loss; a vague fairness complaint is less effective.
