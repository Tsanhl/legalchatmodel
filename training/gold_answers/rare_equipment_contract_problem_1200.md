### Introduction

The decisive questions are whether the parties reached agreement through their three communications or later conduct, which delivery and payment terms govern, whether August delivery was a repudiatory breach, and whether the seller can claim the price while the buyer can reject, set off damages, or withhold payment pending a reasonable inspection. The better view is that the buyer’s second email was the last counter-offer and the seller accepted it by delivery, but that conclusion and the remedy for delay depend on how an objective observer would understand “in July” and “after inspection”.

### The buyer’s offer and the seller’s reply

The buyer’s first email appears sufficiently definite to be an offer: it identifies the equipment and price and invites assent rather than further negotiation. The seller’s words “Agreed, provided delivery is in July” do not mirror that offer. “Provided” ordinarily makes July delivery a condition of assent, so the reply is probably a counter-offer, not an acceptance accompanied by a mere request. A counter-offer rejects the original offer and proposes a new bargain (*Hyde v Wrench* (1840) 3 Beav 334). By contrast, a question seeking information without changing the proposed obligation leaves the offer alive (*Stevenson, Jacques & Co v McLean* (1880) 5 QBD 346). The seller can argue July was a request, although “provided” suggests otherwise.

### The buyer’s response and the governing terms

“Fine” objectively accepts July delivery, but “payment will be after inspection” introduces a payment condition. The word “but” indicates qualified assent rather than a neutral inquiry. The buyer therefore probably made a further counter-offer comprising the equipment, £40,000 price, July delivery, and payment after inspection. Although the question mentions a battle of forms, there are no stated standard forms. The useful analogy is the orthodox “last shot” analysis: the court identifies the final counter-offer accepted by words or conduct, while examining the transaction as a whole rather than mechanically counting documents (*Butler Machine Tool Co Ltd v Ex-Cell-O Corporation (England) Ltd* [1979] 1 WLR 401).

The inspection term is not necessarily void for uncertainty. Courts ask objectively whether the parties intended legal commitment and whether the remaining detail can be made workable; conduct may establish a contract even where paperwork is incomplete (*RTS Flexible Systems Ltd v Molkerei Alois Müller GmbH & Co KG* [2010] UKSC 14, [2010] 1 WLR 753). Conversely, an essential term that supplies no workable standard may defeat formation (*G Scammell & Nephew Ltd v Ouston* [1941] AC 251). Here “after inspection” can plausibly mean after the buyer has had a reasonable opportunity to examine the rare equipment, not whenever the buyer chooses. The statutory right to a reasonable opportunity to examine goods before acceptance supports that construction (Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 34). The buyer cannot postpone inspection indefinitely; the seller should request inspection within a specified reasonable period.

### Acceptance by delivery and August performance

The seller never expressly accepted the buyer’s counter-offer. Delivery can nevertheless communicate acceptance by conduct where it is objectively referable to the proposed bargain (*Brogden v Metropolitan Railway Co* (1877) 2 App Cas 666). Sending the identified equipment after this exchange strongly supports acceptance of the buyer’s last terms. Delivery in August can simultaneously accept the bargain and perform it late: non-conforming performance does not necessarily prevent formation. The seller may respond that the July counter-offer lapsed at the end of July, making August delivery a fresh offer. That argument is plausible if July fixed the only permitted acceptance period. If so, the buyer’s knowing retention or use of the goods could accept the fresh offer by conduct, but the court would then have to infer its payment and delivery consequences from the exchange and the Sale of Goods Act.

If the buyer’s counter-offer remained open, July delivery became a contractual term. Whether time was “of the essence” depends on the agreement and commercial context; time of payment is not ordinarily essential unless a contrary intention appears, while other time stipulations depend on the contract’s construction (Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 10). The rarity of the equipment alone does not prove that July was critical. The conditional word “provided” gives the buyer a strong argument that timely delivery was important, especially if the seller knew of a July-dependent purpose. Yet, without such facts, one month’s delay may justify damages but not termination. The buyer’s response also matters: prompt rejection preserves the termination argument, whereas retaining, using, or reselling the equipment may amount to acceptance and affirmation (Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 35).

### Payment, breach and remedies

The seller cannot simply ignore the agreed inspection condition. Delivery and payment are concurrent by default, but the parties may agree otherwise (Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 28). On the likely contract, the price becomes due after a reasonable inspection opportunity. If that opportunity has passed and the buyer keeps the equipment without a valid termination, the seller has a strong debt claim where property has passed and the price is due (Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 49). If the statutory conditions for an action for the price are not met, the alternative is damages for non-acceptance (Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 50).

The buyer should inspect immediately, record any defect, and notify rejection or acceptance unequivocally. If July delivery was a condition and the buyer has not affirmed, rejection and recovery of any payment are arguable. If the delay was not repudiatory, the buyer must pay but may claim proved loss caused by late delivery. Contract damages protect the expectation interest by placing the claimant, so far as money can, in the position due performance would have produced (*Robinson v Harman* (1848) 1 Exch 850). Loss must arise naturally or have been within both parties’ reasonable contemplation from communicated special circumstances (*Hadley v Baxendale* (1854) 9 Exch 341). The buyer must mitigate and prove any loss rather than treating the whole £40,000 as automatically withheld (*British Westinghouse Electric and Manufacturing Co Ltd v Underground Electric Railways Co of London Ltd* [1912] AC 673).

If no contract was formed, the seller cannot recover the contractual price merely by sending goods after negotiations failed. The seller can ordinarily seek return of its property and may have a restitutionary claim only to the extent the buyer freely accepted a non-gratuitous benefit; the precise outcome would depend on retention, use, knowledge, and any rejection. That alternative makes the parties’ post-delivery communications important evidence.

### Conclusion

The most likely analysis is a sequence of counter-offers: the seller added July delivery; the buyer accepted that condition but added payment after inspection; and the seller accepted the resulting bargain by delivery. August delivery was therefore a breach, but termination is strongest only if July was objectively essential and the buyer rejected promptly. Otherwise, the buyer should inspect within a reasonable time, pay the due price, and quantify a set-off or counterclaim for foreseeable, mitigated delay loss. The seller should allow inspection, demand a clear response, and claim the price under section 49 only once it can prove that property passed and the agreed payment point arrived. If a court finds the last offer had lapsed, both parties must instead address whether August delivery and the buyer’s subsequent conduct created a fresh contract or only restitutionary obligations.
