### Introduction

Dana is very likely liable for the collision, Eli's fractured wrist and the destroyed equipment. The difficult questions are whether his six-week earnings and lost contract are recoverable, how far his failure to wear a seat belt reduces personal-injury damages, and whether Farah is liable for the proved worsening caused by the ambulance delay. Duty, breach, factual causation, scope of liability, remoteness and quantum must be kept separate.

### Dana: duty and breach

A driver owes other road users an established duty to take reasonable care. There is no need to construct a novel duty through the *Caparo Industries plc v Dickman* framework: *Robinson v Chief Constable of West Yorkshire Police* confirms that established categories should be applied first. (*Caparo Industries plc v Dickman* [1990] 2 AC 605; *Robinson v Chief Constable of West Yorkshire Police* [2018] UKSC 4, [2018] AC 736.) The relevant standard is the objective standard of the reasonably competent driver; inexperience or a self-created distraction does not lower it. (*Nettleship v Weston* [1971] 2 QB 691.)

Dana was texting, crossed a red light and collided with Eli's van. Each fact strongly indicates breach, and together they make breach overwhelming unless the evidence reveals an extraordinary explanation, such as an unforeseeable mechanical failure wholly unrelated to Dana's conduct. Texting diverts attention, while a red signal is a direct traffic-control warning. On the stated facts Dana fell below the reasonable-driver standard.

### Factual causation of the collision losses

Eli must prove on the balance of probabilities that Dana's breach caused each head of damage. The ordinary counterfactual asks what would probably have happened but for the breach. (*Barnett v Chelsea and Kensington Hospital Management Committee* [1969] 1 QB 428.) But for Dana driving through the red light while distracted, the vehicles would not have collided. The fractured wrist and destruction of equipment were immediate consequences. Dana cannot treat Eli's non-use of a seat belt as the cause of the collision: it may affect the extent of injury, not why the vehicles collided.

The six weeks away from work requires medical and financial evidence. Eli should prove that the wrist injury, including any later aggravation, made him unable to work for that period. The profitable contract requires the contract itself, evidence that it would probably have been performed, its net value rather than gross revenue, and proof that replacement labour or other reasonable mitigation was unavailable.

### Farah: duty, breach and factual causation

Farah also owed an established road-user duty when driving or controlling her vehicle. The facts expressly describe her obstruction as negligent, so the central dispute is causal. Her conduct did not cause the original collision, fractured wrist or destruction of the equipment. Her possible liability is confined to additional injury and consequential loss that timely treatment would probably have avoided.

Eli needs medical evidence comparing his actual condition with the probable condition had the ambulance arrived without Farah's obstruction. Merely showing a delay is insufficient. If prompt treatment would probably have reduced pain, shortened recovery or prevented a complication, Farah's obstruction is a factual cause of that difference. If the ultimate condition and recovery period would have been the same, breach would produce no compensable personal-injury loss.

### Legal causation and intervening acts

Dana may argue that Farah's independent negligence is a novus actus interveniens that ends Dana's responsibility when the road was blocked. The label does not decide the issue. A later act breaks the chain only when it is sufficiently independent and potent to make the original breach no longer a legal cause of the relevant damage. Courts are slow to treat ordinary rescue or medical events following an injury as wholly superseding causes; only exceptionally independent treatment will do so. (*Webb v Barclays Bank plc* [2001] EWCA Civ 1141.) Conversely, a claimant's unreasonable later conduct can sometimes break the chain, but Eli did not choose the obstruction. (*McKew v Holland & Hannen & Cubitts (Scotland) Ltd* [1969] 3 All ER 1621 (HL).)

The better analysis distinguishes losses. Dana plainly remains liable for the collision injury and equipment damage existing before the delay. As to aggravation, an ambulance journey and the possibility of delay are not so divorced from a road accident that Farah's negligence must automatically extinguish Dana's causal responsibility. Yet Farah's deliberate sphere of control and the seriousness and duration of the obstruction matter. A court could regard her negligence as a concurrent cause of indivisible worsening, leaving both defendants liable to Eli for that component, or as producing a distinct aggravation for which Farah is solely responsible. The medical evidence on divisibility is crucial. It is unsafe to assert either that all later road negligence is foreseeable or that any independent negligence necessarily breaks the chain.

### Remoteness and scope of liability

In tort, the relevant question is whether the kind of damage was reasonably foreseeable, not whether the precise sequence or amount was predicted. (*Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v Morts Dock and Engineering Co Ltd (The Wagon Mound No 1)* [1961] AC 388 (PC).) Personal injury and physical damage to a vehicle and its contents are foreseeable kinds of harm from driving through a red light. Dana therefore cannot avoid the wrist or equipment claim merely because the equipment was specialist or the precise fracture was unexpected.

Eli's earnings and contract loss should not be misclassified as stand-alone pure economic loss. Financial loss consequential on damage to Eli's own person or property is in principle recoverable, subject to factual causation, scope, proof and mitigation. The exact contract value need not have been foreseeable. The real questions are whether loss of income or profit is a foreseeable kind of consequence of personal injury or destruction of working equipment and whether Eli can prove this loss resulted from the collision. Six weeks' ordinary net earnings are a strong claim if medically supported. The particular profitable contract is more contestable because Dana can challenge probability, net profit, overlap with the ordinary earnings claim and reasonable alternatives, but it is not categorically too remote.

For Farah, foreseeable personal-injury aggravation caused by delaying an ambulance falls within the risk created by negligently obstructing it. She is not responsible for equipment destruction, which was complete before her breach. Earnings attributable solely to the additional recovery period may fall within her scope of liability if proved.

### Eli's contributory negligence

Failure to wear a seat belt engages the Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act 1945. (Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act 1945, s 1(1).) Under *Froom v Butcher*, the question is causal and proportionate: did the omission cause or worsen the injury, and what reduction is just and equitable? (*Froom v Butcher* [1976] QB 286.) It does not defeat liability and does not reduce damages for destroyed equipment or other loss unaffected by the omission.

Expert evidence should address whether a seat belt would probably have prevented the wrist fracture, reduced its severity or made no difference. A meaningful reduction to personal-injury damages is likely if proper restraint would have avoided or materially reduced the injury. If the wrist would have fractured to the same extent, there should be no seat-belt reduction. Any earnings or contract loss must follow the same causal allocation: the reduction should affect it only insofar as the relevant inability to work resulted from the avoidable part of the injury. Farah cannot rely on seat-belt non-use to escape liability for a distinct worsening caused by her later obstruction, although the court must avoid compensating Eli twice for overlapping harm.

### Damages and contribution

Eli may claim general damages for pain, suffering and loss of amenity, treatment and other reasonable expenses, proved net earnings, and the reasonable repair or replacement value of the destroyed equipment. Betterment must be allowed for if replacement leaves him with something more valuable than the lost equipment. Future loss requires evidence and an appropriate contingency assessment. Eli also has a duty to take reasonable steps to mitigate, such as obtaining suitable treatment, seeking replacement equipment or labour where commercially sensible, and preserving the lost-contract evidence.

Where Dana and Farah are each legally responsible for the same indivisible aggravation, Eli may recover that loss without having to identify an impossible precise share between them; allocation between tortfeasors can then be addressed under the Civil Liability (Contribution) Act 1978. (Civil Liability (Contribution) Act 1978, s 1.) If the harm is divisible, Dana bears the original collision loss and Farah bears only the additional worsening and its consequential financial effects. Neither defendant should pay twice for the same injury or earnings period.

### Conclusion

Dana's claim to avoid liability is weak: duty, breach and causation of the collision losses are clear. Eli has strong claims for the wrist injury, equipment and medically supported earnings, subject to a causally justified seat-belt reduction. His lost-contract claim is arguable but evidence-sensitive. Farah is likely liable only if expert evidence proves what additional injury and loss her obstruction caused. The principal uncertainty is therefore allocation of the aggravated injury, not Dana's basic responsibility for the accident.
