The contractor will usually have a statutory right to refer the payment dispute to adjudication at any time, but it must first identify the relevant contract, payment notices and crystallised dispute. Adjudication is deliberately rapid and its decision is normally enforced immediately, subject only to narrow jurisdictional and natural-justice objections.

### Statutory right and preliminary checks

Section 108 of the Housing Grants, Construction and Regeneration Act 1996 (HGCRA) gives a party to a qualifying construction contract the right to refer a dispute arising under the contract to adjudication “at any time” (Housing Grants, Construction and Regeneration Act 1996, s 108). There is no general rule that an unpaid contractor must wait eight weeks. The first checks are whether there is a construction contract within the Act, whether any statutory exclusion applies, who the contracting parties are, and whether the dispute has crystallised through a claim and rejection or non-response.

The contractor should separately analyse the payment regime. It should collect the contract, application, payment notice, pay-less notice, valuation, due date and final date for payment. A failure to serve an effective payment or pay-less notice may support an adjudication for the notified sum, but that is not the only form of adjudication: a party may also seek a true valuation. The payment merits and the adjudicator’s jurisdiction must not be conflated.

### Notice, referral and timetable

The process begins with a notice of adjudication identifying the dispute and relief referred. If the contractual procedure does not comply with section 108, the statutory Scheme supplies the procedure (Scheme for Construction Contracts (England and Wales) Regulations 1998, SI 1998/649, sch 1). Under the Scheme, the referring party must secure appointment of the adjudicator and refer the dispute within seven days of the notice. The referral should include the contract, chronology, notices, valuation evidence and the legal and factual basis of the requested decision.

The adjudicator ordinarily has 28 days from referral to decide. That period may be extended by 14 days with the referring party’s consent, or for a longer period with all parties’ consent (Housing Grants, Construction and Regeneration Act 1996, s 108(2)). The timetable is intensive: the responding party may have only days to submit a response, followed by any reply or targeted disclosure directed by the adjudicator. The exact timetable comes from the applicable contractual rules or Scheme and the adjudicator’s directions; there is no universal seven-day merits-response period.

### Effect and enforcement

The decision is temporarily binding: the parties must comply until the dispute is finally determined by litigation, arbitration or agreement (Housing Grants, Construction and Regeneration Act 1996, s 108(3)). It is therefore not a final merits appeal-proof judgment, but neither may the losing party simply ignore it because it believes the adjudicator made a factual or legal error.

If payment is not made, the successful party normally brings expedited enforcement proceedings in the Technology and Construction Court and seeks summary judgment. The courts support “pay now, argue later” by enforcing adjudicators’ decisions despite ordinary errors within jurisdiction (*Macob Civil Engineering Ltd v Morrison Construction Ltd* [1999] EWHC Technology 254, [1999] BLR 93). The Court of Appeal has similarly emphasised that challenges should be restricted so that the statutory objective of prompt enforcement is not defeated (*Carillion Construction Ltd v Devonport Royal Dockyard Ltd* [2005] EWCA Civ 1358, [2006] BLR 15).

Interest depends on the contract, the decision and applicable judicial powers; the contractor should not assume a non-existent HGCRA section or an automatic prescribed rate. Costs of the adjudication and court enforcement also require separate analysis under the applicable rules.

### Jurisdictional objections

A genuine jurisdictional challenge may arise where there was no qualifying construction contract, no crystallised dispute, an invalid appointment, a referral outside the notice, multiple disputes impermissibly bundled under the applicable procedure, or a decision made outside the adjudicator’s referred authority. A party that knows of a jurisdictional objection should reserve it promptly and consistently; participation without reservation may create a waiver issue.

A material breach of natural justice may also defeat enforcement. The threshold is high. The objecting party must ordinarily identify a serious procedural failure—such as deciding on a decisive basis that it had no fair chance to address—and show materiality. Disagreement with the reasoning, an incorrect factual finding, a tight but procedurally permitted timetable, or ordinary unfairness in the colloquial sense will not usually suffice.

The court may consider whether an invalid part can be severed while enforcing the remainder. Insolvency may affect the practical terms of enforcement but does not automatically remove the statutory adjudication right (*Bresco Electrical Services Ltd v Michael J Lonsdale (Electrical) Ltd* [2020] UKSC 25, [2020] 1 WLR 2434).

### Practical next steps

The contractor should prepare a payment-notice table, confirm the contractual adjudication clause, formulate one crystallised dispute precisely, preserve project records, and obtain an adjudicator nomination immediately after serving a compliant notice. It should budget for an accelerated response cycle and prepare enforcement papers in parallel. The employer should pay a valid decision unless it has a properly reserved, evidence-based jurisdictional or material natural-justice objection; a merits complaint can be pursued later in the agreed final forum.
