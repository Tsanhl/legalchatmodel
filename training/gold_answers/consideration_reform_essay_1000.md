### Introduction

Consideration should be reformed, not abandoned. Its bargain requirement supplies a recognisable boundary between enforceable exchanges and gratuitous promises, but its operation is technically uneven. The contrast between promises to pay more and promises to accept less shows the defect: *Williams v Roffey* accommodates commercial practical benefit, while *Foakes v Beer* preserves a formal rule for part-payment of debt (*Williams v Roffey Bros & Nicholls (Contractors) Ltd* [1991] 1 QB 1; *Foakes v Beer* (1884) 9 App Cas 605). Promissory estoppel mitigates hardship but is deliberately incomplete. A first-class account must therefore distinguish the doctrine’s legitimate functions from rules that no longer perform them coherently.

### What consideration still contributes

Consideration identifies an exchange: the promisee must provide an act, forbearance or counter-promise requested as the price of the promisor’s undertaking. Its classical language describes value moving from the promisee, but courts do not ordinarily test economic adequacy (*Currie v Misa* (1875) LR 10 Ex 153; *Thomas v Thomas* (1842) 2 QB 851). Even trivial stipulated value may suffice (*Chappell & Co Ltd v Nestlé Co Ltd* [1960] AC 87). This has evidentiary and cautionary value. It helps distinguish bargains from gifts, signals deliberation, and lets commercial parties know that reciprocal commitments are presumptively enforceable.

Yet nominal consideration weakens the claim that the doctrine reliably proves reciprocity or seriousness. Intention to create legal relations, certainty, deed formalities, duress and estoppel already perform overlapping screening functions. Consideration can therefore exclude seriously intended reliance while validating a contrived exchange. The issue is not that every gratuitous promise should be enforceable, but that the present boundary sometimes turns on form rather than the reasons for enforcement.

### Existing duties and *Williams v Roffey*

The traditional rule is that performing an existing contractual duty owed to the promisor supplies no fresh consideration for a promise of additional payment (*Stilk v Myrick* (1809) 2 Camp 317, 170 ER 1168). It discourages opportunistic demands after a bargain is struck. However, it can defeat sensible variations made because performance has become commercially difficult.

A contractor obtained practical benefits from promising a subcontractor more to complete existing work, including avoiding delay and the expense of replacement; absent fraud or economic duress, those benefits could constitute consideration (*Williams v Roffey Bros & Nicholls (Contractors) Ltd* [1991] 1 QB 1). The decision recognises commercial reality, but risks circularity: the promisor’s benefit is often simply receiving the performance already due. Economic duress does important policing work, yet replacing a clear consideration rule with fact-sensitive benefit and pressure inquiries reduces predictability.

### Part-payment and the *Foakes* anomaly

The law treats a promise to accept less more strictly. Part-payment of an undisputed debt on the due date is not consideration for a creditor’s promise to relinquish the balance (*Foakes v Beer* (1884) 9 App Cas 605). That rule follows the older principle in *Pinnel’s Case*, while allowing orthodox exceptions where the debtor gives something different, pays early at the creditor’s request, or uses a binding composition.

The asymmetry is difficult to defend. A creditor may obtain practical benefits from assured prompt part-payment just as an employer obtains benefit from timely performance. Nevertheless, *Williams v Roffey* has not overruled *Foakes* (*Williams v Roffey Bros & Nicholls (Contractors) Ltd* [1991] 1 QB 1; *Foakes v Beer* (1884) 9 App Cas 605). The Supreme Court in *Rock Advertising* declined to resolve whether practical benefit supports a promise to accept less because the agreed no-oral-modification clause disposed of the variation (*Rock Advertising Ltd v MWB Business Exchange Centres Ltd* [2018] UKSC 24, [2019] AC 119). The anomaly therefore remains binding-law architecture, not a licence to apply *Williams* to debt cases.

### Promissory estoppel: mitigation, not replacement

Promissory estoppel may restrain a party who clearly promised not to enforce strict rights where the other party relied and it would be inequitable to resile (*Central London Property Trust Ltd v High Trees House Ltd* [1947] KB 130). It is equitable rather than statutory and does not arise from the Misrepresentation Act 1967 (Misrepresentation Act 1967). It ordinarily operates as a shield, not a sword (*Combe v Combe* [1951] 2 KB 215). Equity also refuses relief where the promise was extracted unfairly (*D & C Builders Ltd v Rees* [1966] 2 QB 617).

Estoppel consequently cannot replace consideration wholesale. Its requirements and effect remain context-sensitive; rights may be suspended rather than extinguished, and reliance does not automatically justify enforcement of the whole promise. *Collier* suggests possible protection after agreed part-payment, but should not be treated as abolishing *Foakes* (*Collier v P & MJ Wright (Holdings) Ltd* [2007] EWCA Civ 1329, [2008] 1 WLR 643). Flexibility prevents some injustice while preserving uncertainty about remedy and scope.

### Reform

Complete abolition would remove a familiar bargain marker and force intention, reliance and vitiating doctrines to carry greater weight. A better response is targeted reform. Legislation could make a good-faith, non-coerced variation binding where it is recorded or materially relied upon, and could rationalise debt compromises while protecting creditors against pressure. That would confront the pay-more/pay-less distinction directly instead of stretching “practical benefit”. Deeds could remain the clear route for enforceable gratuitous promises, while estoppel retains a protective role where strict insistence is inequitable.

Reform must preserve commercial planning. A writing or reliance requirement could supply evidence, while continued scrutiny for duress would prevent threatened breach being rewarded. That would make the policy choice explicit instead of concealing it within elastic “benefit” reasoning.

### Conclusion

Consideration is neither useless nor coherent enough to escape reform. Its bargain, evidentiary and cautionary functions justify retaining a threshold for ordinary contracts. However, nominal value, existing-duty doctrine and the unresolved divide between *Williams v Roffey* and *Foakes v Beer* show that the threshold is technically unstable (*Williams v Roffey Bros & Nicholls (Contractors) Ltd* [1991] 1 QB 1; *Foakes v Beer* (1884) 9 App Cas 605). Promissory estoppel alleviates hardship but, as a defensive and discretionary doctrine, cannot supply a complete substitute. Targeted statutory validation of fair variations and debt compositions would preserve certainty while aligning enforceability more closely with commercial intention and justified reliance.
