UK tax residence is determined tax year by tax year under the statutory residence test, not simply by immigration status or a single day-count. Since 6 April 2025, the former remittance basis has been replaced for new foreign income and gains by a four-year regime for qualifying new residents, while historic remittance-basis funds require separate tracing and current advice.

### Statutory residence test

The individual should apply the tests in order: automatic overseas tests, automatic UK tests, then the sufficient-ties test (Finance Act 2013, sch 45). Relevant facts include UK days, full-time work abroad or in the UK, a UK home, overseas homes, family, available accommodation, substantive UK workdays, prior UK residence and whether UK days exceed days in any other country.

Arrival part-way through a tax year does not automatically split the year. Split-year treatment applies only if one of the statutory cases and conditions is met (Finance Act 2013, sch 45). The individual should maintain a day-by-day travel calendar, flight records, work hours, home availability, family movements and Hong Kong residence evidence.

### Foreign income, gains and remittances

For qualifying individuals in their first four UK-resident tax years after at least ten consecutive non-UK-resident tax years, the foreign income and gains regime may provide relief on eligible foreign income and gains if claimed under the current rules (Finance Act 2025). A claim can affect personal allowances and annual exempt amounts, so modelling is required. New foreign income and gains under this regime are not governed by the old remittance-basis charging model.

Historic foreign income and gains arising in earlier remittance-basis years remain traceable when money or value is brought to or used in the UK. Mixed accounts, offshore transfers, gifts to relevant persons and assets bought with historic funds require specialist analysis. Transitional facilities and rates are time-limited and must be checked for the 2026-27 tax year against current HMRC guidance.

### Practical planning

Before moving, the individual should inventory accounts and assets by source—clean capital, income, gains and historic remittance-basis amounts—avoid unnecessary mixing, obtain valuations, review disposals and pension interests, and coordinate UK/Hong Kong treaty and double-tax-relief advice. They should also consider domicile-independent inheritance-tax changes based on long-term residence, which are separate from annual income-tax residence.

No transaction should be undertaken solely on this overview. The adviser needs the exact arrival date, previous ten years’ residence, day counts, homes, work pattern, family, asset base and historic claims, then should verify the Finance Act and HMRC manuals current on the transaction date.
