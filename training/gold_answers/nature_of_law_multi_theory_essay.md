### Introduction

The claim is persuasive, but only if “one theory” and “explanation” are handled carefully. A theory may explain a particular dimension of law without explaining law exhaustively. Positivism gives the strongest account of institutional validity; natural law tests law’s claim to moral authority; interpretivism explains a characteristic form of adjudicative reasoning; realism examines official behaviour; and feminist, critical race, Marxist and postcolonial theories reveal structures of power obscured by more abstract accounts. The right conclusion is therefore disciplined pluralism, not an indiscriminate compromise. Theories should be ranked by the question they answer, while their incompatible claims remain visible.

### Positivism: validity and institutional structure

Legal positivism’s central insight is that what law *is* can be identified through social facts without making moral merit a necessary condition of validity. Austin’s command theory captured coercion but could not adequately explain power-conferring rules, legal continuity or officials’ rule-governed practices. Hart’s union of primary and secondary rules is more convincing: a legal system exists where rules governing conduct are organised by rules of recognition, change and adjudication, and officials adopt an internal attitude towards those standards. (HLA Hart, *The Concept of Law* (3rd edn, OUP 2012).)

This explains why an immoral enactment can be legally valid and yet morally condemnable. The separability thesis asserts no *necessary* connection between law and morality; it does not deny frequent contingent connections. Hart’s inclusive positivism also permits a rule of recognition to incorporate moral criteria. Raz’s exclusive positivism is stricter: law must be identifiable through sources without reopening the moral merits if it is to mediate authoritative reasons. (Joseph Raz, *The Authority of Law* (2nd edn, OUP 2009).)

Positivism therefore supplies a powerful threshold account of pedigree, institutional continuity and legal disagreement about what sources control. Its limitation is equally important. A social rule may identify a statute, but source-based validity alone does not explain why it creates an obligation, how an open-textured standard should be interpreted, or why formally valid rules reproduce patterned subordination. Positivism is a necessary institutional map, not a complete theory of law’s nature.

Wicked legal systems sharpen the point. Calling an oppressive enactment legally valid can promote candour by separating the question of social source from the question whether anyone ought to obey it. Yet a regime that systematically abandons publicity, consistency and congruence may cease to govern through law in any rich sense. Positivism can record that institutional degeneration, but its concept of validity deliberately leaves much of the evaluative explanation to another theory. Its strength is disciplined identification; its incompleteness is partly methodological rather than an accidental defect.

### Natural law: legality, reason and moral authority

Natural-law theories challenge any sharp conceptual isolation of legality from practical reason. Classical natural law does not need to make every unjust enactment literally nonexistent. Its subtler claim is that law is centrally an ordinance of reason directed towards a common good; radical injustice makes a purported law defective as law and may defeat moral obligation. Finnis reconstructs this position through basic goods, practical reasonableness and authority oriented towards coordinating a community. (John Finnis, *Natural Law and Natural Rights* (2nd edn, OUP 2011).)

Fuller offers a procedural bridge. Generality, publicity, prospectivity, clarity, consistency, practicability, relative constancy and congruence between announced rule and administration constitute an “internal morality” of law. (Lon L Fuller, *The Morality of Law* (rev edn, Yale University Press 1969).) These principles explain why secret, contradictory or impossible commands fail as modes of legal governance. Yet procedural legality can make wicked government more effective; it does not guarantee substantive justice. Positivists can also treat Fuller’s principles as functional conditions for an effective system rather than moral criteria of validity. Natural law best explains law’s aspiration to justified authority, but cannot by itself settle institutional validity or persistent moral disagreement.

Natural law also distinguishes coercive success from legitimate authority. Citizens may have prudential reasons to comply with an effective regime while lacking a moral obligation to obey its gravely unjust measures. That distinction matters because a complete theory of law must explain both law’s institutional existence and its characteristic demand for fidelity. Natural law illuminates the second question, but becomes less determinate when plural communities disagree about common good and practical reason.

### Interpretivism: principles and adjudication

Dworkin rejects the positivist picture where hard cases simply exhaust rules and release strong discretion. Legal practice includes principles—such as fairness and equality—that have weight rather than rule-like all-or-nothing operation. Judges constructively interpret institutional history by seeking an account that both fits existing materials and justifies them, treating law as integrity. (Ronald Dworkin, *Law’s Empire* (Harvard University Press 1986).) This captures why advocates argue that one interpretation makes legal doctrine morally coherent, rather than merely predicting what an unconstrained official will prefer.

Interpretivism consequently explains legal reasoning better than a source inventory alone. The constitutional recognition of access to justice in *R (UNISON) v Lord Chancellor* illustrates reasoning in which statutory text, constitutional principle and justification interact. (*R (UNISON) v Lord Chancellor* [2017] UKSC 51, [2020] AC 869.) But Dworkin risks idealising appellate adjudication and understating reasonable disagreement about political morality. “Fit and justification” can conceal the interpreter’s own values, while routine administration often turns on hierarchy, resources and settlement rather than a Herculean search for integrity. Interpretivism explains an important practice of justification, not the whole legal order.

### Realism: law in action

American legal realism shifts attention from authoritative formulations to what officials actually do. Holmes’s “bad man” perspective treats law as prediction of public force, while Llewellyn emphasises recurrent institutional practices and situation-sense. (Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr, ‘The Path of the Law’ (1897) 10 *Harvard Law Review* 457; Karl N Llewellyn, ‘Some Realism about Realism’ (1931) 44 *Harvard Law Review* 1222.) Frank’s fact scepticism adds that uncertainty can arise from contested evidence and trial-level judgment, not merely vague doctrine. (Jerome Frank, *Law and the Modern Mind* (Brentano’s 1930).)

Realism corrects theories built almost entirely from appellate reasons: enforcement priorities, bargaining, professional culture and access to representation affect law’s practical meaning. Its weakness is reductionism. Prediction presupposes the legal categories and institutions whose behaviour is predicted, and officials frequently treat rules as reasons rather than camouflage. Realism is thus an indispensable empirical method, but an incomplete account of validity, normativity and legal argument.

### Feminist jurisprudence: gendered power

Feminist jurisprudence asks who is represented by law’s supposedly universal subject. The public/private divide can hide power exercised through family, sexuality, care and dependency; apparently neutral standards may adopt male experience as their baseline. MacKinnon’s dominance theory portrays law as helping constitute, not merely regulate, gender hierarchy. (Catharine A MacKinnon, *Toward a Feminist Theory of the State* (Harvard University Press 1989).) Smart similarly warns that translating experience into legal categories can expand law’s power to define women and their injuries. (Carol Smart, *Feminism and the Power of Law* (Routledge 1989).)

This approach explains dimensions missed by pedigree, coherence and prediction: how legal concepts construct subjects and distribute vulnerability. Yet “feminism” is not one theory. Liberal equality, dominance, relational, queer and intersectional approaches disagree about sameness, difference, autonomy and reliance on rights. A theory that treats “women” as homogeneous can reproduce racial and colonial exclusions. Feminist analysis enriches an account of law’s social power, but needs intersectional and institutional specification.

### Critical race theory: racial formation and rights

Critical race theory rejects the reduction of racism to exceptional individual prejudice. Law has historically constituted racial categories and can preserve hierarchy through formally neutral rules. Crenshaw’s intersectionality shows why analysing race and gender on separate axes can make those located at their intersection legally invisible. (Kimberlé Crenshaw, ‘Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex’ (1989) 1989 *University of Chicago Legal Forum* 139.) Bell’s interest-convergence thesis argues that racial reform is more likely when it also serves dominant interests. (Derrick A Bell Jr, ‘Brown v Board of Education and the Interest-Convergence Dilemma’ (1980) 93 *Harvard Law Review* 518.)

CRT reveals that neutrality and colour-blindness may stabilise historically produced inequality. It also exposes a dilemma: rights discourse can legitimate existing institutions, yet rights remain strategically important to subordinated groups. The theory’s American historical formation should not be transplanted mechanically to England and Wales, where empire, migration and distinct institutions matter. Its deeper account of structural power is essential, but it does not replace a theory identifying legal sources or resolving every doctrinal dispute.

Feminism and CRT overlap without collapsing into each other. Both challenge an abstract legal subject and expose how baseline rules distribute power, but intersectionality shows that gender cannot merely be added to race after separate analyses. Their internal debates also test the essay’s pluralist thesis: critical theories illuminate different axes and mechanisms, while no single axis predicts every legal outcome. Used carefully, they convert “neutrality” from an assumption into a proposition requiring historical and institutional proof.

### Marxism: political economy and legal form

Marxist theory situates law within material relations of production. Property, contract and the formally equal legal person are not neutral foundations: they organise commodity exchange and can mask unequal control of productive resources. Pashukanis’s commodity-form theory connects legal personality and abstract equality to exchange relations. (Evgeny Pashukanis, *Law and Marxism: A General Theory* (Barbara Einhorn tr, Ink Links 1978).) Ideology helps explain why historically specific arrangements appear natural and universal.

Crude economic determinism, however, cannot explain law’s relative autonomy, conflicts within ruling groups, welfare legislation or successful claims that constrain capital and the state. Thompson’s defence of the rule of law shows that legal forms may be ideological yet still impose real restraints and furnish resources for resistance. (EP Thompson, *Whigs and Hunters* (Penguin 1975).) Marxism powerfully explains legal form and distribution in political economy, but needs institutional and cultural accounts to avoid turning every doctrine into a direct reflection of class interest.

### Postcolonial theory: empire, sovereignty and legal knowledge

Postcolonial theory shows that modern law was formed through empire rather than later exported from a complete European centre. Anghie argues that doctrines of sovereignty developed through the colonial encounter, creating hierarchies between “civilised” and non-European peoples. (Antony Anghie, *Imperialism, Sovereignty and the Making of International Law* (CUP 2005).) TWAIL scholarship traces those structures into contemporary international institutions and economic governance. (BS Chimni, ‘Third World Approaches to International Law: A Manifesto’ (2006) 8 *International Community Law Review* 3.)

This lens explains imposed property regimes, suppression of legal pluralism and the authority accorded to some forms of knowledge over others. It also prevents English legal history from being narrated as purely domestic. Its risks are treating “the West” and “the postcolonial” as internally uniform, romanticising pre-colonial orders, or underplaying local agency and hybrid legal forms. Postcolonial theory supplies an indispensable genealogy of legal authority, but not a self-sufficient criterion of present validity.

It also changes legal method. Choosing which archives, institutions and normative orders count as “law” is itself an exercise of power. A source-based theory can identify the rules recognised by imperial institutions, while postcolonial analysis explains how those institutions displaced rival legalities and made their own criteria appear universal. The accounts are therefore complementary at one level, even though the genealogy unsettles positivism’s seemingly neutral starting point.

### Conclusion: a ranked pluralism

No single theory explains law’s institutional, normative, interpretive, behavioural and structural dimensions. Positivism remains the best starting point for identifying a legal system and its valid sources. Natural law explains why legality claims moral authority; interpretivism explains principle-based adjudication; realism tests doctrine against official practice. Feminism, CRT, Marxism and postcolonial theory then identify gendered, racial, class and imperial mechanisms that abstract theories can render invisible.

This conclusion is not that all theories are equally correct. Their claims sometimes conflict: Dworkin disputes positivist foundations, natural law contests moral separability, and critical theories challenge idealised coherence. The defensible method is to state the explanatory question, use the theory suited to it, test its blind spots through rivals, and preserve contradictions rather than dissolve them. Law is at once an institutional system of sources, a justificatory practice, a pattern of official behaviour and a structure of social power. One theory may illuminate one of those features exceptionally well; none can plausibly exhaust them all.
