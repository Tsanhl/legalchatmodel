Proprietary estoppel can give a claimant rights in land where the legal owner created or encouraged an expectation, the claimant reasonably relied on it, and the claimant would suffer detriment if the owner were allowed to withdraw. It is not a way to enforce every family promise. The court asks whether, viewed as a whole, it would be unconscionable for the owner to insist on strict legal title, and then chooses a proportionate remedy.

### What the claimant must prove

#### 1. An assurance or encouraged expectation

The owner must have made an assurance, representation or course of conduct concerning the claimant's rights in identified property. The assurance must be clear enough in its context, but it need not use conveyancing language or amount to a concluded contract. Words, conduct and sometimes knowing acquiescence can be considered together. In *Thorner v Major*, indirect statements and conduct were capable of conveying an assurance because their meaning was sufficiently clear in the parties' farming context. (*Thorner v Major* [2009] UKHL 18, [2009] 1 WLR 776.)

The precise expectation matters. “You will always have a home here”, “this farm will be yours”, and “I will look after you” may generate different equities. A claimant should identify who spoke, the exact words as closely as possible, when and where they were used, what property they concerned, whether they were repeated, and what both sides understood. Vague hopes or statements of present intention are weaker, particularly where the speaker expressly reserved freedom to change plans.

#### 2. Reasonable reliance

The claimant must have changed position because of the assurance or encouraged expectation. Reliance is a causal question: would the claimant probably have acted differently without it? Where an assurance was calculated to influence conduct and the claimant then acts consistently with it, reliance may be inferred, but the owner can rebut that inference with evidence that the claimant acted for another reason.

Reliance must also be reasonable in context. Relevant matters include the parties' relationship, their knowledge and sophistication, whether independent advice was available, whether the arrangement remained deliberately informal, and whether later events contradicted the original expectation. Reliance can continue over many years; the court should assess the whole course of dealings rather than isolate one transaction.

#### 3. Detriment

The claimant must suffer a substantial detriment linked to the reliance. Detriment is not confined to direct expenditure. It may include unpaid work, caring responsibilities, improvements, moving home, abandoning education or employment, accepting reduced wages, giving up another property opportunity, or organising one's life around the promised future. Benefits received—such as accommodation, wages or use of land—must also be brought into account. *Gillett v Holt* stresses that detriment is assessed in the round and is not reducible to a narrow financial calculation. (*Gillett v Holt* [2001] Ch 210 (CA).)

The claimant should explain the counterfactual: what would have happened without the assurance? A bare assertion of sacrifice is insufficient. The court will ask whether the alleged acts were genuinely induced by the expectation, whether they exceeded ordinary incidents of the family or commercial relationship, and whether benefits already received offset some or all of the disadvantage.

#### 4. Unconscionability

Assurance, reliance and detriment are the main analytical elements, but the ultimate question is whether withdrawal would be unconscionable. Unconscionability is not a free-standing licence to decide what feels fair. It is a conclusion drawn from the parties' words, conduct, knowledge, reliance, detriment and any change of circumstances. Deliberate encouragement followed by repudiation after years of induced sacrifice is a strong case; a misunderstood, provisional statement followed by fully paid work is much weaker.

The civil standard of proof applies. The claimant bears the burden and should plead the assurance, reliance, detriment and requested remedy with specificity. Land-registration, limitation, succession and tax consequences may require separate advice depending on the facts.

### What remedies the court can award

The remedy is flexible because the court satisfies the equity rather than mechanically enforcing a contract that was never made. Possible orders include transferring the freehold or a share, granting a lease or licence to occupy, declaring a beneficial interest, ordering payment of money, reimbursing expenditure, or combining relief with conditions. *Pascoe v Turner* illustrates transfer of the property where that was necessary to secure the promised home; *Jennings v Rice* illustrates a monetary award where full enforcement of a broad expectation would have been disproportionate. (*Pascoe v Turner* [1979] 1 WLR 431 (CA); *Jennings v Rice* [2002] EWCA Civ 159, [2003] 1 P & CR 100.)

The court seeks the minimum relief necessary to avoid unconscionability, while giving proper weight to the claimant's expectation. The relationship between expected benefit and detriment is central, but there is no single arithmetic formula. *Guest v Guest* confirms that remedy must respond to the repudiation of the induced expectation and remain proportionate; timing and accelerated receipt can affect the order. (*Guest v Guest* [2022] UKSC 27, [2022] 3 WLR 911.) Practical considerations include whether the property can be divided, the needs of third parties, occupation, affordability, tax, valuation, and whether a money award would force a sale.

A claimant should therefore plead a primary remedy and realistic alternatives. For example: transfer of the promised property; alternatively a life interest or right to occupy; alternatively a sum reflecting the proven equity. An interim injunction or restriction may be considered urgently if disposal of the land threatens to defeat the claim, but the requirements and cross-undertaking need specific litigation advice.

### Evidence to preserve now

Preserve the evidence in its original form and build a dated chronology. The most useful material commonly includes:

- the exact words of each assurance, with dates, places, surrounding conversations and everyone present;
- letters, emails, text messages, messaging-app exports, cards, diary entries, recordings lawfully made, draft wills and notes from solicitors or advisers;
- witness details and early signed statements from relatives, neighbours, employees, carers, accountants and professionals who heard the promise or observed reliance;
- Land Registry title documents, transfers, mortgages, tenancy or licence documents, planning papers, farm or business records and successive valuations;
- bank statements, invoices, receipts, tax records and photographs proving improvements, expenditure, reduced wages or unpaid work;
- employment, education, housing and pension records showing opportunities rejected or life choices changed;
- care records, calendars and medical or social-services material evidencing time and responsibilities, while preserving confidentiality;
- records of benefits received, including accommodation, pay, gifts, use of vehicles or land, because the court will consider offsets;
- evidence of repudiation: changed wills, sale instructions, eviction demands, denials of the promise and the claimant's immediate response; and
- metadata and backups. Keep original devices/files, avoid editing screenshots, and record how each copy was obtained.

Do not contact witnesses with a suggested version of events or alter documents. Preserve adverse material as well as helpful material. If litigation is reasonably contemplated, obtain advice before destroying routine records and before covert recording, entering the property, lodging a restriction, or making threats about sale. Early valuation and a carefully particularised letter before claim can clarify whether mediation is realistic.

### Practical assessment

A strong claim usually has a property-specific assurance repeated or knowingly encouraged over time, conduct that is difficult to explain without the assurance, substantial net detriment, and contemporaneous witnesses or records. The greatest risks are vague wording, an alternative reason for the claimant's conduct, benefits that offset the alleged sacrifice, and seeking a remedy far beyond what is needed to avoid unconscionability. The immediate priorities are to secure the documentary record, obtain witness accounts while memories are fresh, protect against disposal where genuinely necessary, and formulate proportionate primary and alternative remedies.
