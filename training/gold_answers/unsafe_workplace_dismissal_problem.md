### Introduction

The employee’s strongest claim is automatic unfair dismissal for refusing to return while workplace danger persisted, not merely unfair dismissal for an unreasonable disciplinary decision. The decisive questions are whether she reasonably believed the danger was serious and imminent, whether she could reasonably have been expected to avert it, and whether that protected conduct was the principal reason for dismissal. The employer’s description of its instruction as “lawful” is not conclusive. It must prove the actual reason for dismissal. The employee should preserve risk evidence and act promptly because procedure may determine an otherwise strong claim.

### Status and statutory gateway

The question describes the claimant as an employee. That is important because the right not to be unfairly dismissed applies to employees, while a worker who is not an employee generally has protection against health-and-safety detriment but not the same dismissal claim (Employment Rights Act 1996, ss 94, 100 and 230). Unless status is disputed, analysing control, substitution or contractual labels would distract from the facts. If the employer contests status, the tribunal would examine the practical relationship rather than treating written labels as decisive (*Autoclenz Ltd v Belcher* [2011] UKSC 41, [2011] ICR 1157).

Ordinary unfair dismissal generally requires the applicable period of continuous employment. On 14 July 2026, the ordinary period remains two years; the statutory reduction to six months is scheduled for dismissals from 1 January 2027 (Employment Rights Act 2025, s 25). That does not defeat this employee’s principal case. Section 108 disapplies the ordinary qualifying requirement for specified automatically unfair reasons, including the health-and-safety grounds in section 100 (Employment Rights Act 1996, ss 100 and 108). The employee should plead the automatic route expressly rather than assume a tribunal will infer it.

### Refusal to return because of serious and imminent danger

Section 100(1)(d) addresses the facts directly. A dismissal is automatically unfair where the reason, or principal reason, is that, in circumstances of danger which the employee reasonably believed to be serious and imminent and which she could not reasonably have been expected to avert, she left, proposed to leave, or—while the danger persisted—refused to return to the workplace or a dangerous part of it (Employment Rights Act 1996, s 100(1)(d)). If instead she took other appropriate steps to protect herself or others, section 100(1)(e) may apply. If she brought harmful or potentially harmful circumstances to the employer’s attention through the relevant route, section 100(1)(c) may also be engaged. The precise subsection should follow the evidence rather than being pleaded as an undifferentiated safety complaint.

The statutory test does not require the employee finally to prove that the workplace was objectively unsafe. Her belief must genuinely have been held and objectively reasonable, and it must concern danger that she reasonably regarded as both serious and imminent. The tribunal will therefore examine what she knew at the time: reported incidents, photographs, risk assessments, medical or technical warnings, communications from managers, protective equipment, advice from a safety representative, and what remedial measures the employer offered. A remote or speculative concern is weaker than a concrete risk capable of materialising at work. Conversely, evidence that the employer knew of an unresolved hazard, ignored reports or provided no credible control measures strongly supports reasonableness.

The inability reasonably to avert the danger also matters. The employer may argue that she could have used protective equipment, worked in another area, followed a safe system or raised the matter through a representative. Those facts could weaken section 100(1)(d) if the proposed measure genuinely removed the danger. They do not assist if the measure was unavailable, inadequately explained or itself unsafe. The words “while the danger persisted” make chronology critical: the employee should identify when the risk arose, what she reported, the response, why she believed the risk remained and when the refusal occurred.

### Was the protected refusal the reason for dismissal?

The employee must connect the protected act with the dismissal. The employer’s stated reason—that she refused an instruction—may itself establish the necessary link if the relevant instruction was to return despite the danger she reasonably perceived. Calling the conduct insubordination cannot remove statutory protection. The tribunal considers the real reason operating on the dismissing decision-maker, using the dismissal letter, meeting notes, messages, disciplinary allegations, earlier risk reports and the timing of the decision.

The employer may contend that it dismissed her for an independent reason, such as dishonesty, threatening conduct, prolonged unexplained absence after the risk was removed, or refusal of a demonstrably safe alternative. Such a case must be supported by contemporaneous evidence. A reason reconstructed after dismissal will carry less weight. If protected refusal was the principal reason, the dismissal is automatically unfair even if the employer sincerely considered the instruction reasonable.

### The employer’s ordinary misconduct argument

If section 100 is not established, the employer may rely alternatively on conduct as a potentially fair reason under section 98. It would then need an honest belief in misconduct based on reasonable grounds following a reasonable investigation (*British Home Stores Ltd v Burchell* [1980] ICR 303 (EAT)). The tribunal would assess whether investigation, procedure and dismissal fell within the range of reasonable responses, without substituting its own preferred sanction (*Iceland Frozen Foods Ltd v Jones* [1983] ICR 17 (EAT)).

That alternative is fact-sensitive. A clear and safe instruction, a fair risk investigation, consultation, an offered safe alternative and an unreasonable continuing refusal would improve the employer’s position. Failure to investigate the reported danger, predetermination, denial of an opportunity to explain, or immediate dismissal despite available alternatives would undermine both substance and procedure. A procedural defect can establish ordinary unfairness even where a fair process might ultimately have produced dismissal, although compensation may then be reduced to reflect that possibility (*Polkey v AE Dayton Services Ltd* [1988] AC 344 (HL)). *Burchell* and the range-of-responses test cannot, however, convert a dismissal for conduct protected by section 100 into a fair dismissal.

### Health-and-safety duties and contractual instructions

The employer owes a general duty, so far as reasonably practicable, to ensure employees’ health, safety and welfare at work (Health and Safety at Work etc Act 1974, s 2(1)). That duty supports the context but should not replace the section 100 analysis. Nor is the case resolved by saying that every instruction involving risk is automatically unlawful. Employment often involves controlled risk. The questions are what danger existed, what precautions were reasonably practicable, what the employee reasonably believed and whether refusal fell within the statutory protection.

An implied obligation to obey lawful and reasonable instructions is therefore subject to statute and the particular circumstances. If section 100 applies, the employer cannot avoid liability by drafting a broad return-to-work clause or characterising protected refusal as breach of contract. If the risk was resolved and the employee knew or should reasonably have understood that it was resolved, continued refusal may fall outside section 100(1)(d) and become a potential conduct matter. The evidence and chronology, not the contractual label alone, decide that boundary.

### Remedies, adjustments and immediate steps

The employee should obtain the dismissal letter, contract and policies; retain emails, messages, photographs and risk reports; identify witnesses; request the relevant risk assessment; and prepare a dated account of reports, responses and the refusal. She should appeal internally without allowing that process to obscure the tribunal deadline. She will normally need to notify ACAS for early conciliation before presenting the claim; that process affects calculation of the limitation period (Employment Tribunals Act 1996, s 18A). As at 14 July 2026, an unfair-dismissal claim is ordinarily required within three months less one day of the effective date of termination, subject to the statutory ACAS adjustment (Employment Rights Act 1996, s 111). The announced general move to six-month tribunal limits is scheduled for 1 October 2026 and should not be applied before commencement.

If liability is established, the tribunal may consider reinstatement or re-engagement, but neither is automatic. It considers practicability, the employee’s wishes and the employment relationship (Employment Rights Act 1996, ss 112–117). Financial relief ordinarily comprises a basic award and a compensatory award for proven loss, subject to mitigation and applicable statutory rules (Employment Rights Act 1996, ss 118–124). Contribution may reduce compensation where blameworthy conduct materially caused the dismissal; a *Polkey* reduction concerns the chance that a fair process would have led to the same outcome. Neither adjustment should be assumed merely because the employer disputes the safety concern.

### Conclusion

The employee has a strong section 100(1)(d) case if she genuinely and reasonably believed that unresolved workplace danger was serious and imminent, could not reasonably avert it and was dismissed principally because she refused to return while it persisted. The employer’s “lawful instruction” argument does not answer that statutory protection. Its best defence is evidence that the danger was not reasonably perceived as serious and imminent, had been effectively removed, could reasonably have been averted, or that dismissal was genuinely for independent misconduct. Ordinary unfair-dismissal principles remain an alternative, not a substitute for section 100. Both parties should preserve contemporaneous safety and decision-making evidence, while the employee should appeal and begin ACAS early conciliation promptly.
