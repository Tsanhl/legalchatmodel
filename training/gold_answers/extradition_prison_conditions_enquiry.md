The requested person can resist extradition if surrender would be incompatible with Convention rights, especially where current, specific evidence shows a real risk of inhuman or degrading prison conditions. A general fear or poor national record is insufficient; the evidence must connect the risk to the person, likely prison and conditions, and any reliable assurance.

### Part 1 procedure

A Part 1 case concerns a territory operating the statutory warrant procedure (Extradition Act 2003, pt 1). After arrest, the person must be brought before the appropriate judge promptly. The court checks identity, the warrant and statutory requirements, then considers bars to extradition and human rights before deciding whether to order surrender (Extradition Act 2003, pts 1 and 2). Bail, legal representation, interpretation and disclosure issues should be addressed immediately.

The defence should identify any additional bar—such as double jeopardy, passage of time, hostage-taking considerations or prior extradition—as well as proportionality where the statutory route applies. The prison issue is principally determined through section 21, which requires discharge if extradition would be incompatible with Convention rights (Extradition Act 2003, s 21).

### Article 3 prison-conditions objection

Article 3 is absolute. Extradition is prohibited where substantial grounds show a real risk that the person will suffer torture or inhuman or degrading treatment (European Convention on Human Rights, art 3; *Soering v United Kingdom* (1989) 11 EHRR 439). The court assesses the foreseeable conditions at the time of surrender, not merely past criticism.

Useful evidence includes recent inspection and monitoring reports, judgments, prison-capacity and cell-space data, sanitation, ventilation, violence, healthcare, the person’s medical vulnerability, the likely institution and expert evidence. It should distinguish remand from sentenced conditions and explain why ordinary domestic remedies would not remove the risk.

The requesting state may provide an assurance about placement or treatment. The court examines specificity, legal and practical authority, monitoring, past compliance and enforceability rather than accepting diplomatic language automatically. Fact-finding and the reliability of assurances are intensely evidence-sensitive (*Zabolotnyi v Mateszalka District Court, Hungary* [2021] UKSC 14, [2021] 1 WLR 2569).

### Decision and appeal

If the judge rejects the objections and orders extradition, a Part 1 appeal lies to the High Court with a very short statutory filing period, ordinarily seven days from the order (Extradition Act 2003, s 26). The notice and grounds should therefore be prepared before judgment. New evidence on prison conditions may require an application explaining why it was not available and why it would have affected the decision.

A further appeal to the Supreme Court is exceptional and requires certification of a point of law of general public importance and permission (Extradition Act 2003, s 32). The immediate priorities are specialist representation, bail material, a complete medical record, objective prison evidence, proposed cross-examination or expert evidence, and strict diarying of the appeal deadline.
