The passenger should treat the cancellation and the baggage loss as two distinct claims. Cancellation is principally governed by the assimilated UK air-passenger regime known as **UK261**; baggage loss on an international journey is principally governed by the Montreal Convention 1999. The itinerary, operating carrier, date, booking structure and whether the bag was checked must be confirmed before either route is applied conclusively.

### Cancelled flight: UK261

UK261 applies to flights departing from the UK and, subject to the carrier and route rules, to certain flights arriving in the UK. Current CAA guidance says that an arrival into the UK is covered where the operating carrier is a UK or EU carrier; the precise territorial condition should therefore be checked against the ticket and operating carrier (UK Civil Aviation Authority, ‘Consumer travel advice – Summer 2026’ (CAA, 2026) <https://www.caa.co.uk/newsroom/news/consumer-travel-advice-summer-2026> accessed 14 July 2026).

Where UK261 applies, cancellation ordinarily gives the passenger a choice between reimbursement, rerouting at the earliest opportunity, or rerouting later at the passenger’s convenience, subject to availability. A passenger who still wants to travel should not select a refund without considering that the choice normally ends the rerouting and care route. A passenger awaiting rerouting may also be entitled to reasonable meals, communications, accommodation and transport where the waiting period requires them (Assimilated Regulation (EC) No 261/2004 (‘UK261’), arts 5, 8 and 9).

Fixed-sum compensation is a separate question. It depends on matters including when notice was given, the timing of any replacement flight and whether the carrier proves extraordinary circumstances that could not have been avoided despite reasonable measures. Extraordinary circumstances may defeat fixed compensation but do not normally remove reimbursement, rerouting and care obligations. The passenger should therefore obtain the cancellation notice and the airline’s stated reason rather than assuming that every cancellation produces compensation (Assimilated Regulation (EC) No 261/2004 (‘UK261’), arts 5 and 7; UK Civil Aviation Authority, ‘Cancellations’ (CAA) <https://www.caa.co.uk/air-passengers/travel-problems-and-rights/flight-delays-and-cancellations/cancellations/> accessed 14 July 2026).

### Checked baggage: Montreal Convention

For international carriage within the Convention, the carrier is liable for destruction, loss or damage to checked baggage where the event occurred while the baggage was in its charge, subject to the defence concerning an inherent defect, quality or vice of the baggage. This is not an Article 17(1) passenger-accident claim, and proof of airline fault is not the basic gateway for checked baggage (Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules for International Carriage by Air (opened for signature 28 May 1999, entered into force 4 November 2003) 2242 UNTS 309 (‘Montreal Convention’), art 17(2)).

Recovery is compensatory rather than an automatic lump sum. The passenger should prove the bag’s contents and value with purchase records, photographs, baggage tags and evidence of reasonable replacement expenditure. Article 22(2)’s revised baggage limit is 1,519 Special Drawing Rights per passenger from 28 December 2024, unless a higher special declaration of interest was made and any supplementary sum paid. The limit is not a sterling amount per kilogram (International Civil Aviation Organization, ‘2024 Revised Limits of Liability under the Montreal Convention of 1999’ (2024) <https://www.icao.int/secretariat/legal/LEB%20Treaty%20Collection%20Documents/2024_Revised_Limits_of_Liability_Under_the_Montreal_Convention_of_1999_en.pdf> accessed 14 July 2026; Montreal Convention, art 22(2)).

Written notice is time-critical. Article 31 requires damage to checked baggage to be complained of in writing within seven days of receipt; delay should be complained of within 21 days from the date the baggage was placed at the passenger’s disposal. A bag is treated as lost for Article 17(3) when the carrier admits loss or it has not arrived 21 days after it ought to have arrived. Article 35 separately extinguishes the right to damages if proceedings are not brought within two years, calculated from arrival, the date the aircraft ought to have arrived, or stoppage of carriage. Those periods should not be replaced by invented one-year or two-week limits (Montreal Convention, arts 17(3), 31 and 35).

Article 33 provides alternative Convention fora, including the carrier’s domicile, principal place of business, the place of business through which the contract was made, and the destination; a further passenger-injury forum may apply in an Article 17(1) case. The principal place of business is therefore not the only possible court (Montreal Convention, art 33).

The Convention is intended to provide an exclusive damages code within its substantive and temporal scope. A passenger should not assume that an unsuccessful Convention damages claim can simply be repackaged in negligence, although rights and enforcement mechanisms outside the Convention’s damages field must be considered separately (*Sidhu v British Airways plc* [1997] AC 430 (HL); *Stott v Thomas Cook Tour Operators Ltd* [2014] UKSC 15, [2014] AC 1347).

### Evidence and next steps

The passenger should immediately retain the booking confirmation, boarding pass, cancellation message, operating-carrier details, baggage tag, property irregularity report, photographs, receipts and an itemised contents schedule. They should notify the airline in writing, make the UK261 and baggage claims as separate headings, and keep proof of submission. If the airline’s final response is unsatisfactory, the appropriate approved ADR body—or the CAA’s Passenger Advice and Complaints Team where the airline is not in an approved scheme—may assist, but the CAA cannot impose an individual award (UK Civil Aviation Authority, ‘How the CAA can help’ (CAA) <https://www.caa.co.uk/air-passengers/travel-problems-and-rights/travel-complaints/how-the-caa-can-help/> accessed 14 July 2026).

The strongest immediate position is therefore a UK261 claim for cancellation remedies, if the route and carrier are within scope, alongside a Montreal claim for proven checked-baggage loss. The passenger should act now on written notice and evidence rather than wait for the two-year litigation deadline.
