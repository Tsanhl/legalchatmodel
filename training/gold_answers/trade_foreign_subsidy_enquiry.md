The exporter must first identify where the harm occurs. A UK trade-remedies investigation protects UK industry from subsidised goods imported into the UK; it is not ordinarily a remedy for lost export sales in a foreign or third-country market. WTO proceedings are state-to-state, so the company cannot bring a WTO claim itself.

### WTO subsidy analysis

Under the WTO Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures, the core questions are whether a government or public body made a financial contribution or provided income/price support, whether this conferred a benefit, and whether the subsidy is specific (Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures, arts 1-2). Export-contingent and local-content subsidies are prohibited; other specific subsidies may be actionable if they cause the required adverse effects (Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures, arts 3, 5 and 6).

The exporter should collect the foreign measure, legislation, budget documents, recipient accounts, benchmark financing or input prices, product scope, subsidy amount, export data, lost tenders, price suppression and evidence connecting the measure to lost sales. It can submit this to the Department for Business and Trade and request government engagement or a WTO dispute, but the UK Government controls any WTO case and remedy.

### UK countervailing route

If subsidised products are entering the UK and injuring UK producers of like goods, the relevant domestic route is an application to the Trade Remedies Authority (TRA). A countervailing investigation requires evidence of a countervailable subsidy, injury to UK industry and causation; it also applies statutory economic-interest and public-interest controls under the current framework (Taxation (Cross-border Trade) Act 2018).

The application should define the product and UK industry, show supporter standing, quantify import volume and prices, demonstrate material injury through sales, market share, profit, capacity and employment, and separate harm caused by other factors. The TRA investigates and recommends; the Secretary of State takes the decision within the statutory structure. Measures may include a countervailing amount but are not compensation for historic export losses.

### Practical route

If the problem is market access abroad rather than subsidised imports into Britain, the exporter should use the Government’s trade-barrier and overseas-network routes alongside WTO evidence, contractual claims and local-law advice. If both occur, the WTO, diplomatic and TRA workstreams can proceed separately. The first task is therefore a country/product/data matrix showing subsidy recipient, destination market, UK import effects and export losses; approaching the TRA without evidence of UK import injury would select the wrong procedure.
