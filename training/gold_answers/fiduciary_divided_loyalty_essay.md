### Introduction

The proposition is substantially correct, provided “strict” is used precisely. Equity does not assume that every fiduciary is dishonest. It distrusts a decision-making structure in which a person charged to advance another’s interests is exposed to a personal interest, or a duty to somebody else, capable of deflecting judgment. The no-conflict and no-profit rules are therefore prophylactic: they intervene without requiring proof of fraud, bad faith, loss, or a decision that was demonstrably influenced. Yet strict liability is not the same as remedial or institutional inflexibility. Fiduciary status and the scope of the undertaking are context-specific; conflicts may be authorised or accepted through fully informed consent; and gain-based, proprietary and restorative remedies perform different functions. The better view is consequently that equity is strict about unauthorised divided loyalty but flexible about defining the relationship, permitting informed risk and selecting the response.

### Loyalty as the distinctive fiduciary obligation

The starting point is to separate fiduciary loyalty from the complete catalogue of trustees’ obligations. A trustee may owe duties of care, investment, accounting, information and proper administration, but breach of each is not necessarily a fiduciary breach. Millett LJ explained that the distinguishing obligation is loyalty: the fiduciary must not place himself in a position where duty and interest conflict, act for personal benefit without informed consent, or serve two principals whose interests conflict (*Bristol and West Building Society v Mothew* [1998] Ch 1 (CA)). This matters analytically. Strict conflict liability cannot be justified by describing every negligent mistake or administrative default as disloyalty.

Nor does an ordinary constituted trustee balance parallel fiduciary duties to the settlor and beneficiaries. Once property is held on the declared trusts, the trustee administers it according to the trust instrument for those beneficially entitled or for the permitted purpose. The settlor’s intentions matter because they are expressed in that instrument, not because the settlor ordinarily remains a competing fiduciary principal. “Divided loyalty” instead describes a conflict between the duty undertaken for beneficiaries and the fiduciary’s own interest, or between incompatible duties owed to different principals.

The distinction between loyalty and honesty is also important. The irreducible core of a trust requires trustees to perform the trust honestly and in good faith for beneficiaries; an exemption clause cannot reduce the arrangement to one in which trustees need not act honestly (*Armitage v Nurse* [1998] Ch 241 (CA)). But the conflict rules go further than a prohibition on fraud. An honest fiduciary may account because the legal concern is not merely blameworthy intention. It is the integrity of entrusted decision-making.

### Why the conflict rule is strict

The classic rationale is institutional rather than compensatory. A fiduciary should not be asked to determine whether self-interest has influenced a judgment that must be made for another. The law therefore removes the temptation and avoids an evidential inquiry into an internal decision-making process. The House of Lords’ insistence that a fiduciary cannot faithfully serve conflicting interests reflects the ordinary vulnerability of human judgment, not a presumption that every fiduciary will consciously cheat (*Bray v Ford* [1896] AC 44 (HL)).

That concern explains the traditional refusal to ask whether a conflicted transaction was objectively fair. In *Aberdeen Railway Co v Blaikie Brothers*, a director’s interest on the other side of a contract engaged the conflict rule notwithstanding the possibility that the terms benefited the company ((1854) 1 Macq 461 (HL)). If fairness alone were a defence, the fiduciary would retain the very power to expose the principal to disloyal decision-making that the rule seeks to control. It would also force beneficiaries to prove how an undisclosed interest affected negotiations conducted without their informed participation.

The rule is nevertheless concerned with a legally meaningful conflict, not a fanciful association. Its demanding operation is commonly expressed through the need for a real sensible possibility of conflict. That threshold reconciles prophylaxis with workable administration: equity acts before corruption or measurable loss, but it still requires a connection between the interest or competing duty and the fiduciary undertaking.

### No profit without informed authorisation

The no-profit rule complements the conflict rule. A fiduciary must not retain an unauthorised benefit obtained by reason of the fiduciary position or an opportunity encountered through it. The trustee could not retain for himself a lease connected with trust property after the lessor refused renewal for the infant beneficiary (*Keech v Sandford* (1726) Sel Cas Ch 61, 25 ER 223). The severity is deliberate. Allowing the trustee to prove that the beneficiary could not have obtained the opportunity would create incentives to manufacture or exploit evidential uncertainty.

The reach and controversy of this approach are demonstrated by a solicitor to a trust who, acting with one beneficiary, acquired shares after using knowledge and access derived from the trust relationship. The transaction benefited the trust and the defendants acted in good faith, yet an account was ordered because the opportunity and information were sufficiently connected to the fiduciary position (*Boardman v Phipps* [1967] 2 AC 46 (HL)). The result supports the proposition: equity distrusts the unauthorised structure of divided loyalty even where hindsight reveals profit rather than loss.

The same logic applies outside trusteeship. Directors who obtained profits through opportunities connected with their office were accountable without the company proving fraud or that it could itself have made the profit (*Regal (Hastings) Ltd v Gulliver* [1967] 2 AC 134 (HL)). Gain-based liability thereby prevents fiduciaries from converting entrusted access into an option: retain the gain if undiscovered, but risk only compensating provable loss if challenged. An account removes that incentive.

The strict approach also limits ordinary causation arguments. A fiduciary cannot necessarily retain profits by showing that the principal would have agreed to a transaction on different disclosed terms. The obligation was to disclose and obtain consent before exploiting the conflict, not to reconstruct a hypothetical bargain after the event (*Murad v Al-Saraj* [2005] EWCA Civ 959, [2005] WTLR 1573). This is demanding, but it protects the principal’s autonomy to decide whether the conflict should be accepted and on what conditions.

### Consent and authorisation: strictness without paternalism

Strictness does not make conflict risk incapable of lawful management. The duties are default incidents of an undertaking of loyalty, not an assertion that beneficiaries can never permit a conflicted act. The trust instrument may authorise defined transactions, legislation may regulate them, and fully informed consent by the person or persons to whom the duty is owed may remove the disloyal character of conduct. Consent must be real: disclosure should identify the nature and material extent of the conflict and benefit, and it must come from persons legally capable of consenting.

This qualification strengthens rather than contradicts the rationale. Equity distrusts the fiduciary’s unilateral assessment that the conflict is harmless; it does not distrust an informed principal’s choice to accept the risk. Authorisation relocates the decision to the person whose interests the duty protects. It also enables commercial and professional relationships in which some conflicts are foreseeable and appropriately controlled.

There are limits. A vague clause should not be treated as permission for every undisclosed profit, and consent obtained through incomplete disclosure cannot validate what the principal was not equipped to evaluate. Where beneficiaries include minors, unborn persons or persons lacking capacity, the practical route cannot simply be unanimous personal consent. The need for a valid source of authority preserves the protective function of the rule.

### Liability is strict, but remedies remain differentiated

The strongest argument against the proposition is that equity’s responses reveal considerable flexibility. That is true, but it is necessary to identify where flexibility operates. An account of profits strips gains attributable to the fiduciary breach; rescission reverses a conflicted transaction where restoration and third-party rules permit; equitable compensation responds to loss; and proprietary relief affects tracing and insolvency priority. These remedies should not be treated as interchangeable punishment.

The proprietary consequence recognised for bribes and secret commissions is especially significant. A benefit obtained by an agent through the fiduciary position is held on constructive trust for the principal, enabling tracing and priority if the fiduciary becomes insolvent (*FHR European Ventures LLP v Cedar Capital Partners LLC* [2014] UKSC 45, [2015] AC 250). That response reinforces deterrence, but it also protects the principal’s claim to value generated through an opportunity that the fiduciary was not entitled to appropriate.

Conversely, *Boardman* shows that a court may grant an allowance for work and skill that created or enlarged the profit (*Boardman v Phipps* [1967] 2 AC 46 (HL)). An allowance does not retrospectively authorise the conflict or convert loyalty into a negligence standard. It prevents the account from operating oppressively where the fiduciary’s valuable contribution is separable from the unauthorised gain. Remedial calibration can therefore coexist with a strict liability rule.

### Critical evaluation

The principal objection is over-deterrence. Capable trustees, directors and advisers may avoid beneficial opportunities, demand extensive advance permissions or increase fees to reflect uncertain exposure. A rule indifferent to good faith and benefit may appear morally crude. Moreover, broad fiduciary language can itself create uncertainty unless courts carefully identify the undertaking and the subject matter to which loyalty attaches.

Those objections justify disciplined definition and proportionate remedies, not a general fairness defence. Requiring the beneficiary to prove conscious influence, loss or an unfair price would recreate the informational disadvantage produced by non-disclosure. The fiduciary normally controls evidence about the opportunity and decision. A strict ex ante rule, combined with informed authorisation and differentiated remedies, gives the fiduciary a clear route: disclose first and obtain permission.

The proposition also needs a final qualification. Equity does not distrust multiple roles as such. Modern institutions routinely permit nominees, professional trustees and directors to hold several offices. What equity distrusts is an unauthorised conflict within the scope of a particular undertaking. Careful analysis must therefore ask: to whom was loyalty owed, concerning what matter, what interest or competing duty arose, was the conflict real, what was disclosed and authorised, and what gain or transaction should the remedy address?

### Certainty, deterrence and legitimate enterprise

Strict rules also have a rule-of-law advantage. A standard asking whether a conflicted fiduciary behaved “fairly” would be difficult to apply in advance and would invite litigation about price, motive and hypothetical alternatives. The prohibition supplies an administrable baseline: do not enter the conflict or take the gain without authority. That clarity protects conscientious fiduciaries as well as beneficiaries because disclosure and consent provide an identifiable compliance route.

The commercial objection should nevertheless influence how courts define the undertaking. A fiduciary retained for a limited transaction does not necessarily undertake loyalty across every aspect of the parties’ economic lives. Equally, information or an opportunity unconnected with the entrusted matter should not automatically be captured merely because a fiduciary relationship exists elsewhere. *Mothew* is valuable here because its focus on the obligation actually undertaken prevents “fiduciary” from becoming an unbounded label for judicial correction of hard bargains (*Bristol and West Building Society v Mothew* [1998] Ch 1 (CA)).

This boundary preserves legitimate enterprise without weakening the conflict rule once its conditions are satisfied. Narrowing the scope by reference to the undertaking is principled; excusing an established, undisclosed conflict because the transaction later appeared beneficial is not. The former identifies whether loyalty governed the opportunity at all. The latter permits the fiduciary to appropriate the principal’s decision about risk and benefit. Accordingly, contextual definition and strict enforcement are complementary rather than contradictory.

### Conclusion

Fiduciary obligations are strict because equity distrusts unauthorised divided loyalty, not because it assumes universal dishonesty or requires trustees to balance continuing duties to settlors and beneficiaries. The no-conflict and no-profit rules protect the integrity of entrusted judgment by avoiding inquiries into motive, hindsight and difficult counterfactual loss. Their apparent severity is justified by vulnerability, evidential control and the principal’s autonomy to decide whether a conflict may be accepted. Yet fiduciary law is not mechanically inflexible: the undertaking defines the duty; a real conflict must exist; informed consent and authorisation permit managed risk; and allowances, accounts, rescission, compensation and proprietary relief respond differently. Equity is therefore uncompromising about unilateral disloyalty while retaining principled flexibility around scope, permission and remedy.
