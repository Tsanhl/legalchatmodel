### Introduction

The proposition is substantially correct if “strict” is used precisely. Equity does not presume dishonesty; it distrusts a structure in which someone charged to advance another’s interests is exposed to a personal interest or competing duty capable of deflecting judgment. The no-conflict and no-profit rules are therefore prophylactic, without requiring fraud, bad faith, loss or demonstrated influence. Yet strict liability is not institutional inflexibility. The undertaking’s scope is contextual, fully informed consent can authorise risk, and personal and proprietary responses perform different functions. Equity is strict about unauthorised divided loyalty but flexible about scope, permission and consequence.

### Loyalty as the distinctive fiduciary obligation

The starting point is to separate fiduciary loyalty from the complete catalogue of trustees’ obligations. A trustee may owe duties of care, investment, accounting, information and proper administration, but breach of each is not necessarily a fiduciary breach. Millett LJ explained that the distinguishing obligation is loyalty: the fiduciary must not place himself in a position where duty and interest conflict, act for personal benefit without informed consent, or serve two principals whose interests conflict (*Bristol and West Building Society v Mothew* [1998] Ch 1 (CA)). This matters analytically. Strict conflict liability cannot be justified by describing every negligent mistake or administrative default as disloyalty.

Nor does an ordinary constituted trustee balance parallel fiduciary duties to the settlor and beneficiaries. Once property is held on the declared trusts, the trustee administers it according to the trust instrument for those beneficially entitled or for the permitted purpose. The settlor’s intentions matter because they are expressed in that instrument, not because the settlor ordinarily remains a competing fiduciary principal. “Divided loyalty” instead describes a conflict between the duty undertaken for beneficiaries and the fiduciary’s own interest, or between incompatible duties owed to different principals.

The distinction between loyalty and honesty is also important. The irreducible core of a trust requires trustees to perform the trust honestly and in good faith for beneficiaries; an exemption clause cannot reduce the arrangement to one in which trustees need not act honestly (*Armitage v Nurse* [1998] Ch 241 (CA)). But the conflict rules go further than a prohibition on fraud. An honest fiduciary may account because the legal concern is not merely blameworthy intention. It is the integrity of entrusted decision-making.

### Why the conflict rule is strict

The classic rationale is institutional rather than compensatory. A fiduciary should not be asked to determine whether self-interest has influenced a judgment that must be made for another. The law therefore removes the temptation and avoids an evidential inquiry into an internal decision-making process. The House of Lords’ insistence that a fiduciary cannot faithfully serve conflicting interests reflects the ordinary vulnerability of human judgment, not a presumption that every fiduciary will consciously cheat (*Bray v Ford* [1896] AC 44 (HL)).

That concern explains the traditional refusal to ask whether a conflicted transaction was objectively fair. In *Aberdeen Railway Co v Blaikie Brothers*, a director’s interest on the other side of a contract engaged the conflict rule notwithstanding the possibility that the terms benefited the company ((1854) 1 Macq 461 (HL)). If fairness alone were a defence, the fiduciary would retain the very power to expose the principal to disloyal decision-making that the rule seeks to control. It would also force beneficiaries to prove how an undisclosed interest affected negotiations conducted without their informed participation.

The rule is nevertheless concerned with a legally meaningful conflict, not a fanciful association. Its demanding operation is commonly expressed through the need for a real sensible possibility of conflict. That threshold reconciles prophylaxis with workable administration: equity acts before corruption or measurable loss, but it still requires a connection between the interest or competing duty and the fiduciary undertaking.

### No profit without informed authorisation

The no-profit rule complements the conflict rule. A fiduciary must not retain an unauthorised benefit obtained by reason of the fiduciary position or an opportunity encountered through it. The trustee could not retain for himself a lease connected with trust property after the lessor refused renewal for the infant beneficiary (*Keech v Sandford* (1726) Sel Cas Ch 61, 25 ER 223). The severity is deliberate. Allowing the trustee to prove that the beneficiary could not have obtained the opportunity would create incentives to manufacture or exploit evidential uncertainty.

The reach and controversy of this approach are demonstrated by a solicitor to a trust who, acting with one beneficiary, acquired shares after using knowledge and access derived from the trust relationship. The transaction benefited the trust and the defendants acted in good faith, yet an account was ordered because the opportunity and information were sufficiently connected to the fiduciary position (*Boardman v Phipps* [1967] 2 AC 46 (HL)). The result supports the proposition: equity distrusts the unauthorised structure of divided loyalty even where hindsight reveals profit rather than loss.

The same logic applies outside trusteeship. Directors who obtained profits through opportunities connected with their office were accountable without the company proving fraud or that it could itself have made the profit (*Regal (Hastings) Ltd v Gulliver* [1967] 2 AC 134 (HL)). Gain-based liability thereby prevents fiduciaries from converting entrusted access into an option: retain the gain if undiscovered, but risk only compensating provable loss if challenged. An account removes that incentive.

The current leading statement is *Rukhadze v Recovery Partners GP Ltd*. A seven-justice Supreme Court unanimously refused to introduce a defence that the fiduciary could have made the same profit through a lawful alternative. The majority treated the duty to account as arising when the profit is received and accompanying the institutional constructive trust; the concurring judgments agreed on liability while differing over whether accounting is best understood as enforcement of a primary duty or a remedy for misuse or disloyalty (*Rukhadze v Recovery Partners GP Ltd* [2025] UKSC 10, [2025] 2 WLR 529). That disagreement matters conceptually, but every approach rejects hypothetical consent or an imagined lawful route as an escape from an established unauthorised gain. *Murad* similarly protects the principal’s autonomy to set the terms of actual consent rather than reconstructing a bargain after the event (*Murad v Al-Saraj* [2005] EWCA Civ 959, [2005] WTLR 1573).

That result resolves the apparent opposition between strict obligation and flexible response. The Court did not dilute the ex ante loyalty rule. It located flexibility principally in identifying the attributable profit and granting an equitable allowance where strict disgorgement would otherwise produce disproportionate injustice, without admitting hypothetical lawful conduct as a defence.

### Consent and authorisation: strictness without paternalism

Strictness does not make conflict risk unmanageable. The trust instrument or legislation may authorise defined transactions, and fully informed consent from those owed the duty may validate conduct. Consent must be real: disclosure should identify the conflict’s nature, material extent and benefit, and legally capable principals must approve it.

This strengthens rather than contradicts the rationale. Equity distrusts the fiduciary’s unilateral assessment that a conflict is harmless, not an informed principal’s choice to accept it. Authorisation relocates the decision and enables commercial relationships with controlled, foreseeable conflicts.

A vague clause cannot authorise every undisclosed profit, and incomplete disclosure cannot validate an unevaluated risk. Minors, unborn beneficiaries and persons lacking capacity cannot simply provide unanimous personal consent. A valid source of authority remains essential.

### Liability is strict, but remedies remain differentiated

The strongest argument against the proposition is that equity’s responses reveal considerable flexibility. That is true, but it is necessary to identify where flexibility operates. Personal accounting identifies the net unauthorised gain; rescission reverses a conflicted transaction where restoration and third-party rules permit; equitable compensation responds to loss; and proprietary relief changes ownership, tracing and insolvency priority. They are not interchangeable forms of punishment. Nor, after *Rukhadze*, is it adequate to call the duty to account merely a discretionary remedy.

The proprietary consequence recognised for bribes and secret commissions is especially significant. A benefit obtained through the fiduciary position is held on constructive trust for the principal, enabling tracing and priority upon insolvency (*FHR European Ventures LLP v Cedar Capital Partners LLC* [2014] UKSC 45, [2015] AC 250). *Stevens* confirms that this is an immediate institutional trust, not a label attached only at judgment: dissipation destroys a real beneficial interest and can generate compensatory liability (*Stevens v Hotel Portfolio II UK Ltd* [2025] UKSC 28). Proprietary strictness therefore has consequences beyond deterrence.

Conversely, *Boardman* shows that a court may grant an allowance for work and skill that created or enlarged the profit (*Boardman v Phipps* [1967] 2 AC 46 (HL)). *Rukhadze* presents that discretion as equity’s answer where rigorous disgorgement would cause disproportionate injustice, while warning that its availability and amount remain fact-sensitive (*Rukhadze v Recovery Partners GP Ltd* [2025] UKSC 10, [2025] 2 WLR 529). An allowance neither authorises the conflict retrospectively nor converts loyalty into negligence; it calculates the gain fairly after liability has attached.

### Critical evaluation

The principal objection is over-deterrence: capable fiduciaries may avoid beneficial opportunities, demand extensive permissions or increase fees. A rule indifferent to good faith and benefit can seem morally crude, while broad fiduciary language creates uncertainty unless courts define the undertaking carefully.

The academic debate sharpens that objection. Conaglen defends the conflict and profit rules as protecting the proper performance of non-fiduciary duties rather than simply reversing unjust enrichment (Matthew Conaglen, ‘The Nature and Function of Fiduciary Loyalty’ (2005) 121 LQR 452). Smith instead centres the fiduciary’s undertaking to exercise judgment loyally for another, which explains both strict prophylaxis and the importance of defining the undertaking’s scope (Lionel Smith, ‘Fiduciary Relationships: Ensuring the Loyal Exercise of Judgement on Behalf of Another’ (2014) 130 LQR 608). Both approaches support strictness, but for institutionally different reasons.

Those objections justify disciplined definition and proportionate remedies, not a general fairness defence. Requiring the beneficiary to prove conscious influence, loss or an unfair price would recreate the informational disadvantage produced by non-disclosure. The fiduciary normally controls evidence about the opportunity and decision. A strict ex ante rule, combined with informed authorisation and differentiated remedies, gives the fiduciary a clear route: disclose first and obtain permission.

The proposition also needs a final qualification. Equity does not distrust multiple roles as such. Modern institutions routinely permit nominees, professional trustees and directors to hold several offices. What equity distrusts is an unauthorised conflict within the scope of a particular undertaking. Careful analysis must therefore ask: to whom was loyalty owed, concerning what matter, what interest or competing duty arose, was the conflict real, what was disclosed and authorised, and what gain or transaction should the remedy address?

### Certainty, deterrence and legitimate enterprise

Strict rules also have a rule-of-law advantage. A standard asking whether a conflicted fiduciary behaved “fairly” would invite uncertain litigation about motive, price and hypothetical alternatives. The prohibition instead gives a compliance route: disclose and obtain authority. Commercial freedom should influence the logically prior question of scope. A fiduciary retained for a limited matter does not undertake loyalty across the parties’ entire economic lives; *Mothew* prevents the label becoming an unbounded device for correcting hard bargains (*Bristol and West Building Society v Mothew* [1998] Ch 1 (CA)). *Hopcraft* likewise insists upon identifying an undertaking of single-minded loyalty before attaching the deliberately strict conflict and profit consequences (*Hopcraft v Close Brothers Ltd* [2025] UKSC 33, [2025] 3 WLR 423). Contextual definition and strict enforcement are therefore complementary: the former asks whether loyalty governed the opportunity, while the latter prevents unilateral appropriation once it did.

### Conclusion

Fiduciary obligations are strict because equity distrusts unauthorised divided loyalty, not because it assumes dishonesty or continuing duties to both settlors and beneficiaries. The rules protect entrusted judgment by avoiding inquiries into motive, hindsight and counterfactual loss. Their severity reflects vulnerability, evidential control and the principal’s autonomy. Yet the undertaking defines the duty, informed consent permits managed risk, and allowances, accounting, compensation and proprietary relief have distinct consequences. Equity is uncompromising about unilateral disloyalty while remaining principled about scope, permission and response.
