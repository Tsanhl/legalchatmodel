**Single best answer: the magistrates proceed to allocation (mode of trial), decide whether summary trial is suitable, and, if it is, the defendant may consent or elect Crown Court trial.**

After a not-guilty indication for an either-way offence, the magistrates hear representations and consider whether their sentencing powers and the nature and seriousness of the case make summary trial appropriate (Magistrates’ Courts Act 1980, s 19). If they decline jurisdiction, the case is sent to the Crown Court. If they accept jurisdiction, the defendant is asked whether to consent to summary trial and retains the right to elect jury trial.

The case does not automatically remain in the magistrates’ court merely because proceedings began there, and a not-guilty indication does not itself send every either-way offence to the Crown Court. Allocation is the required intervening decision.
