A misleading anonymous political advert is not automatically unlawful. The candidate should preserve the advert immediately, identify whether it concerns personal character or political policy, and investigate the promoter, payer, targeting and required digital imprint before selecting among police, Electoral Commission, platform, civil and election-petition routes.

### False statements about a candidate

The most specific content offence prohibits a false statement of fact about a candidate’s personal character or conduct, made or published for the purpose of affecting that candidate’s return (Representation of the People Act 1983, s 106). The provision does not generally police false opinions, policy criticism or statements about a party. The distinction between fact and political comment, the candidate’s personal character, falsity and electoral purpose is therefore decisive. A publisher has a statutory defence based on reasonable grounds for believing the statement true.

If those elements are realistically present, the candidate should obtain specialist advice and make a focused police report with the preserved evidence. Section 106 has been applied strictly because it limits political expression; the election-court litigation concerning false personal allegations against a candidate illustrates both its force and its confined scope (*R (Woolas) v Parliamentary Election Court* [2010] EWHC 3169 (Admin), [2011] 2 WLR 1362). A bare assertion that an advert is “misleading” is insufficient.

### Anonymous digital material

Electronic campaign material may have to display information identifying its promoter and the person on whose behalf it was published. The statutory digital-imprint regime distinguishes paid electronic material from certain other material published by specified political actors and contains detailed exceptions (Elections Act 2022, pt 6). The candidate should check the current Electoral Commission guidance rather than assume every anonymous post is regulated ([Electoral Commission, ‘Political campaigning online’](https://www.electoralcommission.org.uk/political-campaigning-online)).

The advert should be reported to the platform through its political-advertising and impersonation channels, with a request to preserve subscriber, payment and targeting data. The candidate can report a suspected imprint breach to the Electoral Commission or police according to the applicable enforcement allocation. The Commission decides whether to investigate and does not automatically remove the advert or compensate the candidate.

### Campaign finance, data and civil routes

If the advert was paid for, expenditure and donor/source records may engage candidate, party or third-party campaign rules. The relevant actor, election, regulated period and spending category must be established before alleging a breach (Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act 2000). Anonymous coordinated advertising may also justify checking data-protection compliance and whether personal data was unlawfully used for targeting.

Defamation may be considered if the advert identifies the candidate, conveys a defamatory factual meaning and has caused or is likely to cause serious harm (Defamation Act 2013, s 1). Available civil relief can include an urgent injunction in an appropriate case, damages and publication remedies, but courts are cautious about pre-trial restraints on speech. Truth, honest opinion and publication on a matter of public interest must be anticipated (Defamation Act 2013, ss 2-4). Platform identity disclosure may require a court-backed process.

### Election petition and evidence

An election petition is a specialist, short-deadline route after the result; it is not a general pre-election complaint mechanism. The candidate must identify a statutory basis for avoiding the election, comply with service and security requirements, and show the legally required connection between the proved illegality or irregularity and the return. Administrative irregularities can invalidate an election where they affected the result, or where the election was not conducted substantially according to law, but trivial non-compliance does not suffice (*Morgan v Simpson* [1975] QB 151). That principle should not be converted into a rule that every misleading advert must have changed individual votes.

The candidate should save the original URL, video or image, full-page screenshots, source code where lawful, date and time, impressions, targeting, ad-library entry, payment/promoter information, comments, shares, platform reports and witness accounts. They should avoid editing the only copy. Because police reporting and petition deadlines can be extremely short, local-election counsel should review the material immediately. The strongest initial route is section 106 if the advert states a provably false personal fact; otherwise the imprint, finance, platform and civil routes may be more appropriate.
