### Introduction

Law does not strike one stable balance between autonomy, certainty, fairness, accountability and power. Each value is indispensable, yet each can undermine the others: autonomy may entrench unequal bargaining power; certainty may perpetuate unfairness; fairness may reduce predictability; accountability may inhibit legitimate discretion; and public power may be necessary to secure the conditions in which autonomy is meaningful. The better view is therefore institutional and plural. Different fields allocate decision-making authority, prescribe different thresholds for intervention, and expose decisions to different forms of review. The nature of the balance depends on the relationship, the protected interest and the institution best placed to decide.

### Contract law: autonomy under conditions of fairness

Contract law begins from party autonomy. Enforcing voluntary bargains permits individuals and businesses to organise their affairs and makes planning possible. Certainty is not merely formal: predictable enforcement reduces transaction costs and enables credit, investment and long-term cooperation. Yet apparent consent may coexist with information asymmetry, standard terms or practical dependence. Unqualified autonomy can then validate private power rather than genuine self-direction.

English law responds selectively. The Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 subjects specified exclusions and limitations to statutory control, while the Consumer Rights Act 2015 imposes a broader fairness regime in consumer contracts. (Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977; Consumer Rights Act 2015.) The penalty doctrine similarly distinguishes legitimate protection of a contractual interest from disproportionate punishment. *Cavendish Square Holding BV v Makdessi* frames the inquiry in terms of a legitimate interest and detriment out of all proportion to it. (*Cavendish Square Holding BV v Talal El Makdessi; ParkingEye Ltd v Beavis* [2015] UKSC 67, [2016] AC 1172.) These controls sacrifice some ex ante certainty, but they also protect the credibility of agreement by refusing to equate signature with unlimited normative consent. Contract law therefore balances autonomy and fairness through targeted controls rather than a general judicial power to remake a bad bargain.

### Tort law: corrective justice bounded by institutional limits

Tort law makes private autonomy reciprocal: freedom of action does not include an entitlement to impose foreseeable physical harm on others. The neighbour principle in *Donoghue v Stevenson* expresses corrective justice by linking responsibility to wrongful injury. (*Donoghue v Stevenson* [1932] AC 562 (HL).) Compensation also makes power answerable, because an actor who externalises risk may be required to repair the resulting loss.

Accountability is nevertheless bounded. Unlimited liability would make ordinary activity unpredictable and could transfer distributive choices from legislatures to courts. *Robinson v Chief Constable of West Yorkshire Police* confirms that established duty categories should be applied before resorting to incremental reasoning; *Caparo Industries plc v Dickman* is not a universal formula for every negligence claim. (*Robinson v Chief Constable of West Yorkshire Police* [2018] UKSC 4, [2018] AC 736; *Caparo Industries plc v Dickman* [1990] 2 AC 605.) Duty, scope of liability, remoteness and proof of damage each confine responsibility. Tort thus combines fairness to an injured claimant with certainty for potential defendants. Its central compromise is not between responsibility and immunity, but between correcting a legally attributable wrong and declining losses outside the risk that made the conduct wrongful.

### Criminal law: legality as a restraint on coercive power

Criminal law presents the sharpest conflict because conviction authorises condemnation and punishment by the state. Fair warning and non-retroactivity protect autonomy: people must be able to know in advance what conduct attracts punishment. Article 7 of the European Convention on Human Rights embodies this legality principle. Requirements of actus reus and mens rea also connect liability to responsible agency rather than mere misfortune.

Certainty cannot, however, mean mechanical rules capable of anticipating every form of wrongdoing. Legislatures often use evaluative concepts, and courts must interpret them without retrospectively manufacturing offences. Fair labelling and proportionate sentencing make state power accountable by requiring the conviction and punishment to reflect the defendant's culpability and harm. Public protection can justify coercion, but legality insists that the justification be expressed through prospectively knowable law and procedurally fair proof. Criminal law therefore gives certainty unusual weight because the imbalance of power between state and accused is unusually severe.

### Public law: legality, accountability and institutional competence

Public law converts political power into legally limited authority. Judicial review protects legality by asking whether a decision-maker stayed within the lawful scope and purpose of the power, followed fair procedures and avoided legally irrational conclusions. *Council of Civil Service Unions v Minister for the Civil Service* recognises that prerogative powers are reviewable in principle, while also showing that subject matter such as national security can affect the intensity and practical outcome of review. (*Council of Civil Service Unions v Minister for the Civil Service* [1985] AC 374 (HL).)

The prorogation decision in *R (Miller) v Prime Minister* illustrates accountability where executive action frustrated Parliament's constitutional functions without reasonable justification. (*R (Miller) v Prime Minister* [2019] UKSC 41, [2020] AC 373.) Yet courts do not possess a general mandate to choose the best policy. Institutional competence, democratic legitimacy and statutory design restrain review. Public law's balance is consequently relational: stronger legal justification is required when power gravely impairs constitutional functions or rights, but the remedy remains directed to legality rather than judicial substitution of policy preferences.

### Human rights law: structured justification

Human rights law treats autonomy and dignity as protected interests without making them absolute. Under the Human Rights Act 1998, qualified rights permit interference for specified legitimate aims where the interference is proportionate. (Human Rights Act 1998, ss 3, 4 and 6.) Structured proportionality asks whether the measure pursues a sufficiently important aim, is rationally connected to it, uses no less intrusive measure that could achieve the aim, and strikes a fair balance. *Bank Mellat v HM Treasury (No 2)* demonstrates the discipline of that staged inquiry. (*Bank Mellat v HM Treasury (No 2)* [2013] UKSC 39, [2014] AC 700.)

Proportionality improves accountability because government must disclose and defend the relationship between its objective, evidence and chosen means. It also reduces certainty at the margins: reasonable judges may disagree about necessity or balance. That cost is justified where rigid rules would allow grave, avoidable interferences with protected interests. The framework does not simply prefer individual autonomy to collective welfare; it requires public power to justify the particular burden it places on the right-holder.

### EU law: effectiveness and the limits of national autonomy

EU law illustrates how legal orders redistribute power to secure common commitments. Direct effect in *Van Gend en Loos* allows individuals to invoke sufficiently clear and unconditional EU obligations. (*Case 26/62 Van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen* [1963] ECR 1.) Supremacy, classically expressed in *Costa v ENEL*, protects the uniform effectiveness of EU law against inconsistent national rules. (*Case 6/64 Costa v ENEL* [1964] ECR 585.) These doctrines enhance certainty within the common legal order and empower individuals, but they limit the legislative autonomy of Member States.

The balance is constitutional rather than merely technical. Uniformity prevents powerful states from treating common obligations as optional, while principles such as conferral, subsidiarity and proportionality constrain Union action. In the United Kingdom, withdrawal has altered the domestic operation of EU-derived law, but the theoretical lesson remains: certainty may require authority above the state, and legal autonomy is always autonomy within an institutional allocation of competence.

### Land law: third-party certainty and domestic fairness

Land law gives exceptional weight to formality and publicity because property rights bind third parties. Registration, written dispositions and priority rules enable purchasers and lenders to identify rights without reconstructing every private understanding. The Law of Property Act 1925 and the Land Registration Act 2002 therefore serve autonomy through secure transfer while protecting the wider market's need for certainty. (Law of Property Act 1925; Land Registration Act 2002.)

Domestic co-ownership reveals the limits of formalism. In *Stack v Dowden*, beneficial ownership was assessed in light of the parties' whole course of dealing where the legal title did not resolve their beneficial shares. (*Stack v Dowden* [2007] UKHL 17, [2007] 2 AC 432.) This responsiveness can make outcomes less predictable, yet a purely formal approach could ignore the realities of a shared home and reproduce economic inequality. Land law draws the balance by preserving strong formal rules for third-party transactions while allowing more contextual fairness within the relationship between co-owners.

### Equity and trusts: conscience disciplined by principle

Equity is often associated with fairness, but its legitimacy depends on certainty. A discretionary trust cannot operate unless the objects satisfy the “is or is not” test associated with *McPhail v Doulton*. (*McPhail v Doulton* [1971] AC 424 (HL).) This requirement protects trustees, beneficiaries and courts from an obligation too indeterminate to administer. Equity's concern with conscience is therefore channelled through identifiable doctrines rather than an unrestricted appeal to merits.

Fiduciary law supplies a particularly strong accountability mechanism. *FHR European Ventures LLP v Cedar Capital Partners LLC* confirms that a bribe or secret commission received in breach of fiduciary duty is held on constructive trust for the principal. (*FHR European Ventures LLP v Cedar Capital Partners LLC* [2014] UKSC 45, [2015] AC 250.) The proprietary response constrains the fiduciary's power and removes incentives for disloyalty. Equity may intervene where common-law formality would permit abuse, but stable tests are necessary if such intervention is to remain law rather than retrospective discretion.

### Company law: enterprise autonomy and managerial accountability

Separate legal personality under *Salomon v A Salomon & Co Ltd* enables capital aggregation, perpetual succession and limited shareholder exposure. (*Salomon v A Salomon & Co Ltd* [1897] AC 22 (HL).) It is a legal allocation of power that promotes enterprise autonomy and commercial certainty. The same separation can distance those who control a company from losses borne by creditors, employees or the public.

Company law responds primarily through governance rather than routinely disregarding personality. The Companies Act 2006 codifies directors' duties: section 174 concerns reasonable care, skill and diligence, while section 175 addresses conflicts of interest. (Companies Act 2006, ss 174–175.) Disclosure, shareholder decision-making, derivative claims and insolvency rules add other forms of accountability. The balance remains contested because shareholders, directors and stakeholders do not possess equal practical power. Nevertheless, predictable personality and limited liability are combined with duties targeted at those entrusted with managerial authority.

### Medical law: patient autonomy, capacity and professional responsibility

Medical law shows why autonomy requires more than a formal choice. *Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board* makes disclosure patient-centred: materiality depends on what a reasonable person in the patient's position would attach significance to, together with risks the doctor knows or should know that particular patient would regard as significant. (*Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board* [2015] UKSC 11, [2015] AC 1430.) The rule redistributes informational power and makes professional judgment accountable to the patient's values.

Autonomy has limits where a person lacks decision-making capacity. The Mental Capacity Act 2005 begins with a presumption of capacity, requires practicable support for decision-making and, when capacity is absent, directs decisions through best interests. (Mental Capacity Act 2005, ss 1–4.) The framework protects vulnerable persons without treating disability or an unwise decision as proof of incapacity. Medical law therefore balances welfare and autonomy through procedural safeguards, information duties and decision-specific capacity rather than a general preference for either professional power or unassisted choice.

### Comparative conclusion

Across these subjects, no value has lexical priority. Certainty is most demanding where criminal punishment or third-party property rights are at stake; contextual fairness is more prominent in domestic co-ownership and equitable relationships; autonomy is strengthened by information and capacity rules in medicine and by consent in contract; accountability intensifies where fiduciaries, company directors or public officials exercise entrusted power. The recurring legal technique is structured justification: identify the protected interest, allocate authority to an institution, demand reasons proportionate to the interference, and provide a remedy for abuse.

The proposition is therefore correct only if “balance” is understood as a disciplined allocation of power, not an intuitive compromise. Law cannot maximise autonomy, certainty, fairness and accountability simultaneously. Its distinctive task is to make the unavoidable trade-offs public, reasoned and reviewable. The quality of a legal order lies less in selecting one master value than in ensuring that the institution choosing between values acts within defined authority, on relevant evidence, and subject to an appropriate form of challenge.
