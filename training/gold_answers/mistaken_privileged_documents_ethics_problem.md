### Introduction

The solicitor must not use the documents simply because the client wants a tactical advantage. She should stop any further review or circulation, preserve the material securely, explain to the client that privilege belongs to the opposing party, and obtain urgent supervisory and procedural guidance. The decisive distinction is between loyalty and confidentiality owed to her own client, on the one hand, and the solicitor's independent duties to the court, the administration of justice and professional integrity, on the other. Those public-interest duties prevail over an instruction to exploit an obvious disclosure mistake. (Solicitors Regulation Authority, *SRA Principles* (version in force 11 April 2025) Principles 1–5 and 7.)

### Privilege and accidental disclosure

Legal professional privilege is a substantive protection belonging to the person entitled to assert it; receipt by an opponent does not automatically transfer ownership of the information or make deliberate use legitimate. The facts say that the client received “privileged documents” accidentally, so the safest analysis assumes that the other side did not intentionally waive privilege. If there is genuine doubt about privilege or waiver, that is a question for agreement or judicial determination, not unilateral exploitation by the recipient.

In civil proceedings, where a party inadvertently permits inspection of a privileged document, the inspecting party may use it only with the court's permission. (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, r 31.20.) The court can also restrain use of confidential or privileged material where the recipient knows, or ought to appreciate, that disclosure was mistaken; whether an injunction is appropriate is fact-sensitive, but obviousness of the mistake is central. (*Al Fayed v Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis* [2002] EWCA Civ 780.) The solicitor should therefore avoid copying, quoting, pleading from or deploying the documents pending consent or a court ruling.

### Confidentiality, information and the client's position

The solicitor ordinarily owes her own client a strict duty of confidentiality. She should not disclose the client's communications or intentions merely because the instruction is improper, unless disclosure is required or permitted by law. (Solicitors Regulation Authority, *SRA Code of Conduct for Solicitors, RELs, RFLs and RSLs* (version in force 11 April 2025) para 6.3.) That duty does not, however, require silence about the accidental transmission itself if procedural law, a court order or professional duty requires communication with the sender or the court.

The Code normally requires a solicitor to tell the client information material to the matter. It expressly creates an exception where the information is contained in a privileged document known to the solicitor only because it was mistakenly disclosed. (SRA Code of Conduct, para 6.4(d).) That provision is directly engaged. It confirms that the solicitor need not reveal or explain the documents' privileged contents to the client and should not facilitate further use. Since the client already received them, she should advise the client to stop reading, searching, forwarding or relying on them and to identify every copy and recipient.

Confidentiality and privilege must not be collapsed. The opponent's privilege governs protection and use of the documents; the solicitor's confidentiality duty governs what she may reveal about her own client's affairs. The proper course is tailored disclosure of what the procedure requires—normally the fact of receipt and the need to resolve use—without volunteering unrelated client confidences.

### Duties to the court and integrity

The solicitor must uphold the rule of law and proper administration of justice, act independently, honestly and with integrity, and maintain public trust. Where those Principles conflict with an individual client's desired tactic, the public-interest Principles take precedence. (SRA Principles, Principles 1–5 and 7.) She must not mislead the court or the opponent by act, omission or complicity in her client's conduct. (SRA Code of Conduct, para 1.4.) Silence becomes especially dangerous if later submissions, disclosure statements or evidence imply that the information was obtained legitimately or conceal a procedural irregularity.

The disputes provisions reinforce that conclusion. The solicitor must not misuse or tamper with evidence, may advance only properly arguable assertions, must avoid contempt and comply with court orders, and must draw material procedural irregularities to the court's attention. (SRA Code of Conduct, paras 2.1 and 2.4–2.7.) Accidentally obtained privileged material is not automatically “evidence” that may be deployed free of restraint. Using it to shape pleadings, cross-examination, settlement pressure or document requests before the issue is resolved risks misuse and may contaminate the conduct of the litigation.

### The client's instruction and whether to cease acting

A solicitor acts on authorised client instructions, but only within the law and professional rules. The instruction to “use them and not tell anyone” is not binding because it asks the solicitor to subordinate independent obligations to the client's tactical interest. She should give clear written advice: no further use; preservation rather than destruction; prompt resolution with the other side or the court; and the possible consequences of disobedience, including injunctions, adverse costs, wasted costs, professional discipline and damage to the client's position.

If the client withdraws the instruction and cooperates, the solicitor may ordinarily continue, subject to the court's directions and effective segregation of the material. If the client insists on misuse, demands a misleading statement or refuses a required notification, the solicitor must decline the act and consider terminating the retainer in a procedurally responsible way. She should check the retainer, obtain the firm's general counsel or compliance officer's advice, and seek the court's permission where withdrawal requires it. She must not reveal privileged client communications when coming off the record unless a lawful exception applies; a neutral explanation may be necessary.

### Immediate practical steps

First, the solicitor should stop reading and using the documents and instruct the client and team to do the same. Secondly, she should quarantine originals and copies, disable further circulation where possible, and preserve metadata showing how and when they arrived. Preservation matters because deletion could destroy evidence of the mistake and appear obstructive. Thirdly, she should record who accessed the documents and whether their contents influenced advice, pleadings, negotiations or evidence.

Fourthly, she should notify the supervising partner, the firm's COLP or internal ethics counsel and obtain conflict-free advice. Fifthly, she should review the relevant disclosure order, court rules, undertakings and any confidentiality regime. Sixthly, through an appropriately unexposed lawyer if necessary, she should promptly tell the sender enough to identify the accidental disclosure and agree return, deletion under a documented protocol, or an application for directions. If use is disputed, the documents should be sealed from the merits team until the court decides privilege, waiver and any restraint. (Civil Procedure Rules 1998, SI 1998/3132, r 31.20.)

The solicitor should not destroy the documents immediately: the court or opponent may require a verified account, and secure preservation is different from substantive use. Nor should she report the client automatically. Regulatory reporting depends on facts capable of amounting to a serious breach by a regulated person; the client's improper wish, without more, is not necessarily a report about a regulated person. (SRA Code of Conduct, paras 7.7–7.8.) Any reporting obligation may be channelled through the COLP where appropriate. (SRA Code of Conduct, para 7.12.)

She should also assess whether anyone on the merits team has already absorbed information that cannot realistically be forgotten. If so, simple deletion may be inadequate. A screened replacement lawyer, revised litigation team, informed agreement or tailored court order may be needed to remove forensic advantage while preserving the client's right to representation. The remedy should address actual contamination rather than punish accidental receipt.

### Professional discipline and consequences

Knowing use or concealment could breach the duties not to mislead, to protect the administration of justice, to act independently and with integrity, and to justify professional decisions. The solicitor must keep an audit trail because paragraph 7.2 requires her to be able to demonstrate compliance. (SRA Code of Conduct, para 7.2.) A deliberate attempt to exploit the mistake would be substantially more serious than an initial accidental viewing, particularly if it affected evidence, deceived the court, breached an order or continued after warning.

Potential consequences include an injunction preventing use, delivery up or deletion under supervision, adverse or wasted-costs orders, disqualification of affected lawyers, judicial criticism and an SRA investigation. The precise sanction would depend on culpability, harm, seniority, repetition, cooperation and remediation. Prompt isolation, candid procedural correction and refusal of the client's instruction materially reduce risk; concealment aggravates it.

### Conclusion

The solicitor should refuse the instruction. Her best course is to quarantine and preserve the documents, stop all use, advise the client in writing, involve the supervising partner or COLP, notify the sender or seek urgent court directions as procedure requires, and keep a complete access and decision record. She may continue only if the client accepts that lawful course and the matter can proceed without misuse or misleading conduct. If the client persists, she should cease acting where professionally and procedurally possible. This protects the opponent's privilege without unnecessarily disclosing the client's separate confidences, and it gives proper priority to the court, integrity and the administration of justice.
