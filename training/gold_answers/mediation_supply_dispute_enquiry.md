Mediation is a confidential, structured negotiation facilitated by a neutral; the mediator does not decide the merits. The companies should agree the process, protect limitation, attend with settlement authority and convert any deal into clear binding terms. Confidentiality is strong but not absolute.

### Confidentiality and without prejudice

Genuine settlement communications are ordinarily protected by the without-prejudice rule, so they cannot be used to prove admissions on the merits (*Rush & Tompkins Ltd v Greater London Council* [1989] AC 1280). The mediation agreement usually adds contractual confidentiality covering documents, discussions and the mediator.

The protection has recognised exceptions, including disputes about whether a settlement was concluded, fraud or misrepresentation and certain forms of unambiguous impropriety. It does not automatically protect pre-existing documents merely because they were shown in mediation (*Unilever plc v Procter & Gamble Co* [2000] 1 WLR 2436). The parties should mark mediation communications appropriately and agree how confidential material and personal data will be handled.

### Court powers and costs

Courts can stay or order parties to engage in a non-court dispute-resolution process where doing so is proportionate and does not impair the essence of the right to a judicial hearing (*Churchill v Merthyr Tydfil County Borough Council* [2023] EWCA Civ 1416, [2024] 1 WLR 382). An unreasonable refusal to mediate can affect costs; relevant circumstances include merits, prior attempts, cost, delay and prospects of settlement (*Halsey v Milton Keynes General NHS Trust* [2004] EWCA Civ 576, [2004] 1 WLR 3002).

The companies should not assume mediation suspends a contractual or statutory limitation period. They should agree a standstill or issue protective proceedings if needed.

### Settlement and preparation

A mediated settlement is enforceable as a contract if ordinary formation, authority, certainty and any required formality are satisfied. If proceedings exist, the parties may embody it in a consent or Tomlin order, keeping appropriate commercial terms in a confidential schedule. Terms should address payment dates, tax, releases, confidentiality, no-admission wording, future supply, defaults, interest, costs, discontinuance and enforcement.

Each company should prepare a concise position statement, key contract and loss documents, realistic alternatives, authority limits and a settlement range. The representative attending must be able to make decisions or have immediate access to a decision-maker. A term sheet should be signed before departure, with any conditions precedent stated expressly. If no settlement results, the parties return to litigation without the judge being told the protected concessions.
