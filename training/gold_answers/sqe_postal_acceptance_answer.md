**Single best answer: B’s acceptance took effect when it was posted on Tuesday, so A’s Wednesday revocation was too late, provided post was a contemplated method and the offer did not require actual receipt.**

The postal rule is an exception to the ordinary receipt rule. Where it is reasonable to use post, acceptance is complete when a properly addressed and stamped acceptance is posted, even if it arrives late or is lost (*Adams v Lindsell* (1818) 1 B & Ald 681; *Household Fire and Carriage Accident Insurance Co Ltd v Grant* (1879) 4 Ex D 216). The facts therefore point to a contract on Tuesday: B dispatched an acceptance before learning of the attempted revocation.

That conclusion is not automatic. The rule applies only where the parties expressly or impliedly contemplated post; it is more likely where the parties negotiated by post or distance made that method reasonable (*Henthorn v Fraser* [1892] 2 Ch 27). It can also be displaced by wording requiring notice or actual receipt. An option notice requiring written “notice” was construed to require communication, so posting alone was insufficient (*Holwell Securities Ltd v Hughes* [1974] 1 WLR 155). The offer’s exact language must therefore be checked before selecting the postal rule.

A’s revocation is effective only when communicated to B, not merely when dispatched. On the stated chronology, Wednesday is after Tuesday’s effective postal acceptance. The revocation cannot undo a contract already formed. If the offer required actual receipt, however, B’s acceptance would take effect only on arrival; a communicated revocation received first would then prevent formation.

The alternatives based on instantaneous communications are weaker because they apply a receipt approach to methods such as telex, not the established postal exception (*Entores Ltd v Miles Far East Corporation* [1955] 2 QB 327). The practical next step is to preserve the offer, envelope, proof of posting, revocation message, and evidence showing whether post was contemplated. The final advice turns on the offer’s wording, but absent an actual-receipt requirement, B has the stronger argument that a binding contract arose on Tuesday.
