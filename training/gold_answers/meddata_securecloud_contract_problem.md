### Introduction

MedData’s strongest routes are breach of the hosting obligation and misrepresentation; SecureCloud’s strongest route is its claim for the unpaid invoice. The decisive questions are whether the three assurances were representations or terms, whether MedData actually relied on them, whether the risk-allocation clauses survive statutory control, and whether the £100,000 late-payment charge is an unenforceable penalty.

### Misrepresentation

Each assurance must be examined separately. “Hosted entirely in the UK” is a precise statement of existing arrangements and is false because some processing occurred abroad. “Immune from ordinary cyberattacks” also conveys a verifiable security capability, although “fully NHS-grade” may be too vague unless the negotiations or an industry standard gave it definite content. A statement of present fact is actionable if it was intended to, and did, induce entry into the contract. It need not be the sole inducement, but materiality cannot replace proof that it operated on MedData’s mind. (*Edgington v Fitzmaurice* (1885) 29 Ch D 459.) MedData’s apparent opportunity to investigate would not itself defeat reliance. (*Redgrave v Hurd* (1881) 20 Ch D 1.)

The express non-reliance wording makes evidence important. MedData should preserve negotiation emails, security questionnaires, board papers and witness evidence showing that data location and security influenced its decision. If it proves actual reliance, the statements’ falsity is strong. Fraud, however, requires proof that SecureCloud lacked an honest belief, knew the statement was false, or was reckless when speaking. (*Derry v Peek* (1889) 14 App Cas 337.) Failure to patch a known vulnerability six months later supports breach, but does not alone prove SecureCloud’s state of mind during negotiations.

Absent fraud, section 2(1) of the Misrepresentation Act 1967 is MedData’s principal damages route. (Misrepresentation Act 1967, s 2(1).) Once an inducing misrepresentation is established, SecureCloud must prove that it had reasonable grounds to believe, and did believe, the statement was true until contract formation. (*Howard Marine and Dredging Co Ltd v A Ogden & Sons (Excavations) Ltd* [1978] QB 574.) Rescission is possible in principle, subject to affirmation, lapse of time, impossibility of substantial restoration and third-party rights. Damages may be more practical after six months’ performance.

The measure also matters. Fraud damages cover losses directly flowing from the transaction, subject to causation and mitigation, rather than the contractual remoteness rule. (*Smith New Court Securities Ltd v Scrimgeour Vickers (Asset Management) Ltd* [1997] AC 254.) Section 2(1) has likewise been treated as adopting the fraud measure, although MedData cannot recover twice for the same loss. (*Royscot Trust Ltd v Rogerson* [1991] 2 QB 297.) If SecureCloud proves reasonable grounds and the misrepresentation is innocent, rescission remains the primary response and the court has a discretion to award damages instead under section 2(2), rather than a free-standing section 2(1) damages claim.

### Contractual status of the assurances

The same statements may also be contractual promises. Classification is objective and turns on importance, specificity, timing, relative expertise and whether the written contract appears complete. A statement known to be important is more readily treated as a term. (*Bannerman v White* (1861) 10 CBNS 844.) SecureCloud’s specialist expertise, contrasted with MedData’s dependence on it, supports term status. (*Dick Bentley Productions Ltd v Harold Smith (Motors) Ltd* [1965] 1 WLR 623; compare *Oscar Chess Ltd v Williams* [1957] 1 WLR 370.)

The UK-hosting assurance is the clearest candidate for a term or collateral warranty because it is specific and central to confidential patient-data hosting. The absolute cyberattack assurance is also important, but a court may construe it in commercial context rather than as a literal guarantee against every attack. “NHS-grade” needs evidential definition. Their omission from the writing assists SecureCloud, but is not conclusive. If they are only representations, the Misrepresentation Act route remains; if terms, offshore processing and the security failure can found contractual damages.

Term status does not automatically permit termination. MedData must identify whether the promise is a condition or an innominate term and assess the consequences of breach. It must also elect whether to affirm or terminate, while preserving accrued damages. Continued use after discovery may evidence affirmation if MedData knows the facts and its right to terminate. Conversely, an urgent, clearly communicated transition to a replacement host is more consistent with termination and mitigation. This distinction affects SecureCloud’s invoice claim: termination discharges future primary obligations but does not ordinarily erase payment already accrued for conforming performance.

### Non-reliance clause

The clause says MedData has not relied on pre-contractual statements. In substance it seeks to prevent an essential element of misrepresentation, so section 3 of the Misrepresentation Act 1967 applies. (Misrepresentation Act 1967, s 3.) It is effective only to the extent that it satisfies the reasonableness test in section 11 of the Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977. (Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977, s 11.) A party cannot avoid that control merely by presenting non-reliance as an agreed factual basis. (*First Tower Trustees Ltd v CDS (Superstores International) Ltd* [2018] EWCA Civ 1396, [2019] 1 WLR 637.)

Reasonableness is assessed at contract formation. Relevant matters include bargaining strength, negotiation, legal advice, alternatives, knowledge of the term, insurance and whether MedData could realistically verify SecureCloud’s infrastructure. The facts do not establish these matters, so neither party should assert an automatic result. MedData’s best point is that only SecureCloud knew its patching and processing arrangements; SecureCloud will emphasise two commercial parties allocating risk. Any attempt to protect fraud would face an especially strong public-policy objection.

### Breach

Failing to patch a known vulnerability is powerful evidence that SecureCloud did not provide “reasonable commercial hosting services”. A business supplier also ordinarily owes an implied duty to exercise reasonable care and skill under section 13 of the Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982. (Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982, s 13.) The relevant standard would be informed by the known vulnerability, the sensitivity of patient data, industry practice and the cost and urgency of the patch. SecureCloud may argue that “reasonable” hosting is not attack-proof, but that does not answer the allegation that it knowingly left a remediable vulnerability open.

Processing abroad is a breach if UK-only hosting was a term or collateral warranty. If it remained only a representation, it supports rescission or misrepresentation damages instead. Whether MedData could terminate depends on classification and consequences. For an innominate term, termination requires a breach substantially depriving MedData of the contractual benefit. (*Hongkong Fir Shipping Co Ltd v Kawasaki Kisen Kaisha Ltd* [1962] 2 QB 26.) The combination of compromised patient confidentiality and undisclosed offshore processing may satisfy that test, but the scale of access, remediation and continuing security are needed before advising unequivocal termination.

### Exclusion and limitation clauses

The written clauses appear incorporated, but incorporation does not decide their reach or validity. They are construed in context to identify which liability and loss they cover. (*Photo Production Ltd v Securicor Transport Ltd* [1980] AC 827.) “Loss of data” may mean deletion or corruption; confidential data being accessed is not necessarily data being lost. “Loss of profits” is expressly listed and may therefore cover profits even when they are direct loss. “All indirect loss” ordinarily addresses the second limb of *Hadley v Baxendale*, but clear drafting and the contract as a whole control. (*Transocean Drilling UK Ltd v Providence Resources plc* [2016] EWCA Civ 372.)

Statutory control must then be applied clause by clause. For negligence causing economic or other non-death/personal-injury loss, section 2(2) of UCTA permits exclusion only if reasonable. If MedData contracted on SecureCloud’s written standard terms, section 3 also subjects exclusions for breach and substantially different performance to reasonableness. Sections 11 and Schedule 2 direct attention to bargaining power, alternatives, knowledge, practicability, resources and insurance.

The £5,000 cap limits SecureCloud’s liability. Its adequacy cannot be judged without the contract price, negotiation and insurance evidence. A very low software-services cap may be unreasonable where the supplier can insure and the customer cannot protect itself. (*St Albans City and District Council v International Computers Ltd* [1996] 4 All ER 481.) Conversely, courts give substantial weight to negotiated risk allocation between experienced commercial parties. (*Watford Electronics Ltd v Sanderson CFL Ltd* [2001] EWCA Civ 317, [2001] 1 All ER (Comm) 696.) On the present facts, £5,000 looks vulnerable given confidential patient data, but MedData must prove the factual basis for unreasonableness.

The provisions should not be collapsed into one result. The category exclusion first asks whether a claimed loss falls within “loss of data”, “loss of profits” or “indirect loss”; the cap then limits liability that otherwise survives, if its wording covers the relevant cause of action. The non-reliance clause separately addresses misrepresentation and section 3 control. MedData should argue that ambiguity is resolved by ordinary construction and that a party seeking protection for its own negligent security failure needs sufficiently clear language. SecureCloud should answer that sophisticated businesses may expressly allocate cyber and profit risk, but must still establish the facts that make the allocation reasonable.

### Causation, remoteness and quantum

MedData must prove that the unpatched vulnerability was an effective cause of the intrusion and each claimed loss. The hacker’s act is unlikely to break the chain if exploitation of such a vulnerability was the very risk SecureCloud undertook to manage. SecureCloud can nevertheless challenge technical causation, arguing that credentials, MedData’s systems or another weakness enabled access. Expert evidence and incident logs will be central.

Contract damages aim to place MedData in the position it would have occupied with proper performance. (*Robinson v Harman* (1848) 1 Ex 850.) Client and profit losses are recoverable only if they arise naturally or were within both parties’ reasonable contemplation because relevant special circumstances were communicated. (*Hadley v Baxendale* (1854) 9 Exch 341.) SecureCloud knew it hosted confidential patient data for a healthcare platform, making some client loss from a preventable breach foreseeable. MedData must still prove which clients left because of this incident, the net profits lost, duration and avoided costs. It must mitigate through prompt notification, remediation, client retention and suitable replacement hosting. (*British Westinghouse Electric and Manufacturing Co Ltd v Underground Electric Railways Co of London Ltd* [1912] AC 673.) The exclusions and cap, if valid and applicable, then restrict the recoverable sum.

### Penalty and invoice

Late payment is a breach of the payment obligation, so the £100,000 charge is a secondary obligation and the penalty doctrine is engaged. The modern question is whether SecureCloud has a legitimate interest in timely payment and whether the imposed detriment is out of all proportion to that interest; a genuine pre-estimate of loss is relevant but not the exclusive test. (*Cavendish Square Holding BV v Talal El Makdessi; ParkingEye Ltd v Beavis* [2015] UKSC 67, [2016] AC 1172.) SecureCloud has legitimate interests in cash flow, collection costs and timely performance, but a fixed £100,000 charge is vulnerable without evidence of the invoice amount, delay or exceptional commercial justification. If it is penal, the charge is unenforceable; the court does not simply reduce it to a reasonable figure.

That does not extinguish the principal invoice. SecureCloud can recover a debt earned by performance, subject to MedData showing a contractual right to withhold payment, valid termination, abatement, or an equitable set-off/counterclaim closely connected with the hosting breach. SecureCloud may also claim ordinary interest or proven loss through a lawful route. MedData should plead its damages defensively and avoid double recovery between misrepresentation and contract.

### Conclusion

MedData has a strong breach case based on the known unpatched vulnerability and a substantial argument that UK-only hosting was promised contractually. Its misrepresentation claim depends on proving actual reliance and overcoming the non-reliance clause under section 3 reasonableness. The profit exclusion and £5,000 cap create real litigation risk, but their construction and reasonableness are contestable; they are not automatically effective merely because both parties are businesses.

SecureCloud is likely entitled to any principal invoice earned, but should expect set-off and counterclaim arguments. Its £100,000 charge is likely penal unless undisclosed commercial facts supply a proportionate justification. Both parties should preserve negotiation documents, the executed terms, patch records, server-location records, incident evidence, insurance material, client communications and loss calculations. Those facts will decide reliance, term status, UCTA reasonableness, causation, termination and quantum.
