# Restitution and Unjust Enrichment

## Answer Method
- Start with the unjust-enrichment structure: enrichment, at claimant's expense, unjust factor, defences, remedy.
- Problems should separate mistake, failure of basis, duress, necessity, incapacity, illegality, change of position and proprietary consequences.
- Essays need a thesis on corrective justice, autonomy, risk allocation, unjust factors, or proprietary restitution.

## Topic Map
- Mistake, failure of basis, duress, undue influence overlap, necessity, illegality, change of position, bona fide purchase, tracing, subrogation and remedies.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Restitution answers should use the unjust-enrichment sequence before discussing fairness.
- Identify whether the claim is unjust enrichment, restitution for wrongs, subrogation, tracing or proprietary relief.
- Failure of basis and mistake often overlap with contract; explain why restitution is available despite or because of contract failure.
- Defences can decide outcome even where enrichment and unjust factor are strong.

## For Each Topic Include
- State the enrichment and expense link before unjust factor.
- Identify the precise unjust factor.
- End with personal/proprietary remedy and defences.

## Case Brief Bank
- Lipkin Gorman v Karpnale: Facts: stolen client money lost at casino. Held: restitution available subject to change of position. Reasoning: unjust enrichment and defence recognised. Answer use: unjust enrichment/change of position.
- Banque Financiere de la Cite v Parc (Battersea): Facts: refinancing and priority. Held: subrogation prevents unjust enrichment. Reasoning: remedy reflects objective benefit and intention. Answer use: subrogation.
- Deutsche Morgan Grenfell v IRC: Facts: tax paid under mistake of law. Held: mistake of law can found restitution. Reasoning: old bar removed. Answer use: mistake.
- Benedetti v Sawiris: Facts: services provided without contract. Held: enrichment valued objectively, subjective devaluation possible. Reasoning: benefit valuation discipline. Answer use: quantum meruit.
- Patel v Mirza: Facts: illegal agreement and restitution. Held: illegality defence uses policy-based trio. Reasoning: underlying purpose, public policy and proportionality. Answer use: illegality.
- Foskett v McKeown: Facts: trust money used to pay life premiums. Held: beneficiaries could trace into proceeds. Reasoning: proprietary tracing vindicates property rights. Answer use: tracing/proprietary claims.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not say "unfair" without identifying unjust factor.
- Separate enrichment value from claimant's loss where necessary.
- Always consider change of position, illegality, bona fide purchase and limitation.
- Keep proprietary tracing distinct from personal restitutionary money claims.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers run enrichment, at expense, unjust factor, defences, remedy and proprietary consequences in order.
- Strong essays distinguish unjust enrichment from contract, tort and property.
- Weaker answers treat restitution as damages or moral fairness.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Do not skip directly to fairness; unjust enrichment has a sequence.
- Change of position, illegality and bona fide purchase must be considered before remedy.
- Keep restitution for wrongs separate from unjust enrichment.

## Avoid
- Avoid treating restitution as damages.
- Avoid vague "unfair enrichment" without route and defence.
