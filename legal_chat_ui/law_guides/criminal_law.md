# Criminal Law

## Answer Method
- Problems need offence-by-offence structure: actus reus, mens rea, causation, defence, liability outcome.
- Criminal problem answers should normally go straight into offence analysis; avoid a general introduction or conclusion unless the task specifically asks for one.
- Essays need a thesis about culpability, fair labelling, autonomy, harm, prevention, or criminalisation limits.
- Keep liability separate from sentencing unless asked.
- State burden and standard where it matters.

## Topic Map
- Homicide: murder, loss of control, diminished responsibility, gross negligence manslaughter, unlawful act manslaughter.
- Non-fatal offences, consent, self-defence, intoxication, omissions.
- Property offences: theft, robbery, burglary, fraud.
- Inchoate liability, complicity, attempts, conspiracy.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Formative-style criminal answers should rank offences and defences; do not analyse every theoretical possibility at equal length.
- Defence essays need the policy fault line: autonomy/public policy for consent, culpability/control for insanity/automatism, fair labelling for homicide, or choice/pressure for duress.
- Homicide problems need causation and partial defences before final liability labels; manslaughter alternatives should be visible.
- Non-fatal offence problems should choose the charge by injury, culpability, aggravating factors, overall harm, and likely sentence; do not mechanically plead every OAPA count.
- For charging-standard style non-fatal offences, use a visible battery/ABH/GBH ladder: no or transient injury, more than transient or trifling harm, really serious harm/wounding, medical treatment evidence, weapon/use of force, and aggravating context.
- Consent-to-harm problems need express/implied consent, effectiveness of consent, public-policy category, level and type of force, risk/injury, and whether the context is recognised as lawful.
- Property offences require appropriation/property/belonging/dishonesty/intention to permanently deprive before robbery/burglary/fraud extensions.
- Transferred malice problems require correspondence between actus reus and mens rea, the same-crime limit, and a clear explanation that intention aimed at X may transfer to harm to Y without needing separate foresight of Y.

## For Each Topic Include
- Offence route: conduct element, result/circumstance element, mens rea, causation, defences.
- Defences: trigger, evidential basis, subjective/objective element, proportionality/reasonableness.
- Essay: identify exact fault line, not just “law is complex”.

## Case Brief Bank
- R v Church: Facts: victim thrown in river after assault. Held: unlawful act manslaughter needs an act all sober and reasonable people would recognise as risking some harm. Answer use: UAM objective danger test.
- DPP v Newbury and Jones: Facts: boys pushed paving stone onto train. Held: no need for defendant to foresee harm; the unlawful and dangerous act suffices. Answer use: UAM mens rea.
- R v Cheshire: Facts: negligent hospital treatment after shooting. Held: medical negligence breaks the chain only if so independent and potent as to make the original wound insignificant. Answer use: causation/medical treatment.
- R v Jordan: Facts: palpably wrong treatment after stab wound largely healed. Held: chain broken on those exceptional facts. Answer use: rare exception; contrast Smith and Cheshire.
- R v Smith (1959): Facts: dropped stretcher, poor treatment after stabbing. Held: original wound still an operating and substantial cause. Answer use: causation baseline.
- DPP v Majewski: Facts: intoxicated assaults. Held: voluntary intoxication no defence to basic-intent crimes; may negate specific intent only. Answer use: intoxication framework.
- R v Jogee: Facts: encouragement of fatal attack. Held: accessory must intend to assist or encourage the crime; foresight is only evidence of intent. Answer use: accessorial liability.
- Coroners and Justice Act 2009, ss 54-55: loss of control defence — qualifying trigger (fear of serious violence and/or things said or done of extremely grave character causing a justifiable sense of being seriously wronged), loss of self-control (need not be sudden), and the normal-person filter; considered desire for revenge excludes. Answer use: partial defence to murder.
- Homicide Act 1957, s 2 (as amended): diminished responsibility — abnormality of mental functioning from a recognised medical condition, substantial impairment of ability to understand, form rational judgment or exercise self-control, providing an explanation for the killing. Answer use: partial defence to murder.
- R v Adomako: Facts: anaesthetist failed to notice disconnected tube. Held: gross negligence manslaughter requires duty, breach, causation of death, and negligence so bad as to be criminal. Answer use: GNM test.
- Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008, s 76: self-defence — honest belief in need for force (even if mistaken), reasonable force judged on the facts believed; disproportionate force fails (householder cases partly excepted). Answer use: self-defence structure.
- R v Cunningham: Facts: gas-meter interference. Held: recklessness requires foresight of risk. Reasoning: subjective culpability matters. Answer use: mens rea.
- R v Woollin: Facts: child thrown causing death. Held: virtual certainty direction for oblique intent. Reasoning: jury may infer intent. Answer use: murder intent.
- R v G and R: Facts: children started fire. Held: subjective recklessness. Reasoning: liability should track defendant’s foresight. Answer use: recklessness.
- R v Brown: Facts: consensual sadomasochistic injury. Held: consent not generally defence to serious injury. Reasoning: public policy limits autonomy. Answer use: consent to harm.
- R v Jogee: Facts: secondary liability. Held: foresight is evidence, not mens rea. Reasoning: accessory must intend assistance/encouragement. Answer use: complicity.
- R v Shivpuri: Facts: impossible drug importation attempt. Held: impossible attempts can be criminal. Reasoning: culpable intent suffices under statute. Answer use: attempts.

## Feedback Rules
- Keep offence selection visible before analysing defences.
- Do not add repetitive jury-assessment sections; integrate facts into each element.
- Rank liability outcomes: strong, arguable, weak.
- Problem answers should apply theory to facts, not rehearse essay debates. Save broader criminalisation critique for essay questions.
- Where charging standards are relevant, explain why the facts justify the selected offence rather than merely stating the statutory maximum or injury label.
- Sports/consent problems need the context-specific scope of consent, level of injury, nature of the conduct, rules of the game, and public-policy limits. Do not treat every foul as either automatically criminal or automatically consented to.
- Where transferred malice, accident, or unintended victim facts arise, identify the intended offence and whether the mens rea transfers to the actual harm.
- Defence answers should separate evidential trigger from legal burden/standard and then apply the facts to each limb.
- Sports violence answers should apply Barnes-style factors: sport type, level of play, nature of act, degree of force, risk of injury, rule breach, defendant's state of mind and whether prosecution is reserved for grave conduct.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band problem answers choose the most realistic charge first, then run actus reus, mens rea, causation, defences and final liability.
- Top-band consent answers distinguish express/implied consent, recognised exceptions, public-policy limits and the level/type of harm.
- Top-band homicide/serious violence answers separate murder intent, s.18/s.20/s.47 levels, manslaughter alternatives, causation and partial defences.
- Weaker answers usually analyse every possible offence equally, repeat scenario facts, or bolt on defences without testing their legal triggers.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Select and rank charges before detailed analysis. A strong answer explains why one offence is stronger than another rather than listing all offences neutrally.
- Keep conduct, circumstance/result, mens rea, causation and defences separate for each offence. Do not let policy or moral blame replace statutory elements.
- Homicide and serious violence need causation, intervening acts, intent/recklessness level and manslaughter or partial-defence alternatives before the final label.
- Consent, self-defence, intoxication, duress and loss of control require legal triggers, evidential basis and burden/standard. Do not bolt them on after liability.
- Complicity, attempts and conspiracy need their own mental element after Jogee/attempts doctrine; foresight, preparation and intention must not be merged.

## Avoid
- Avoid treating every possible offence as equally plausible.
- Avoid moral conclusions without statutory elements.

## Anonymized Quality Upgrade
- Fitness-to-plead and insanity essays should place the historical test against modern psychiatry and engage attributed academic and law-reform criticism rather than relying on unsupported policy assertion.
- State a qualified thesis immediately, test reform against concrete cases, confront victim-rights and public-protection rationales, and verify the current status of reports or Bills before concluding.
