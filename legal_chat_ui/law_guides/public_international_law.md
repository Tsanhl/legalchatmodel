# Public International Law

## Answer Method
- Start with source: treaty, custom, general principles, Security Council act, domestic implementing statute, or soft law.
- Problems should separate jurisdiction, attribution, breach, responsibility, immunity, remedies, and enforcement.
- Essays need a thesis on consent, sovereignty, legitimacy, fragmentation, enforcement, or institutional limits.
- Keep international law separate from private international law.

## Topic Map
- Sources, treaty interpretation, state responsibility, attribution, due diligence, use of force, self-defence.
- Immunities, universal jurisdiction, international criminal law, ICC cooperation.
- International humanitarian law, human rights, statehood/recognition, dispute settlement.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Public-international-law answers need source discipline: treaty, custom, general principle, Security Council act, domestic implementation, or soft law.
- State-responsibility problems should run attribution, breach, circumstances precluding wrongfulness, invocation, consequences, and remedies.
- Use-of-force answers need evidence, Article 2(4), self-defence/Security Council route, necessity, proportionality, imminence, and collective-security limits.
- IHL answers need conflict classification, status, targeting rule, proportionality, precautions, command/criminal responsibility, and enforcement limits.

## For Each Topic Include
- ARSIWA: attribution -> breach -> circumstances precluding wrongfulness -> consequences.
- Use of force: Article 2(4) prohibition -> Security Council/self-defence exception -> necessity/proportionality.
- Immunity: jurisdiction first, immunity second, exception/waiver third.
- IHL: classification -> status -> targeting rule -> proportionality/precautions -> accountability.

## Case Brief Bank
- Nicaragua: Facts: support for rebels and use of force. Held: effective control for attribution; customary rules on force. Reasoning: control over specific operations matters. Answer use: attribution/use of force.
- Bosnia Genocide: Facts: genocide attribution. Held: effective control reaffirmed. Reasoning: high threshold for state responsibility. Answer use: attribution.
- Corfu Channel: Facts: mines in territorial waters. Held: due diligence/no knowingly allowing harm. Reasoning: state knowledge and prevention duties. Answer use: due diligence.
- Oil Platforms: Facts: self-defence justification. Held: necessity/proportionality not satisfied. Reasoning: evidential burden and strict test. Answer use: self-defence.
- Arrest Warrant: Facts: ministerial immunity. Held: sitting foreign minister immune from foreign criminal jurisdiction. Reasoning: functional performance of office. Answer use: personal immunity.
- Pinochet: Facts: former head of state and torture. Held: functional immunity limited for international crimes under implementing regime. Reasoning: treaty/criminal accountability. Answer use: ratione materiae limits.

## Feedback Rules
- Separate jurisdiction from immunity.
- Do not treat jus cogens as automatically removing all procedural immunity.
- For universal jurisdiction, identify domestic statutory hook.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Source discipline comes first: treaty, custom, general principle, Security Council act, domestic implementation and soft law have different authority and enforcement consequences.
- State responsibility answers must run attribution, breach, circumstances precluding wrongfulness, invocation, reparation/countermeasures and enforcement separately.
- Use-of-force answers require Article 2(4), Security Council or self-defence route, evidence, necessity, proportionality, imminence and collective-security limits.
- Immunity answers should separate adjudicative jurisdiction, personal immunity, functional immunity, waiver, treaty/statutory exceptions and enforcement immunity.
- IHL answers need conflict classification, status of person/object, targeting rule, proportionality, precautions, command/criminal responsibility and enforcement limits.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Sources and treaty interpretation: Statute of the International Court of Justice, art 38(1); Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties 1969, arts 31-32; *North Sea Continental Shelf Cases (Federal Republic of Germany/Denmark; Federal Republic of Germany/Netherlands)* [1969] ICJ Rep 3.
- Use of force/self-defence: UN Charter, arts 2(4), 39-42 and 51; *Military and Paramilitary Activities in and against Nicaragua (Nicaragua v United States of America) (Merits)* [1986] ICJ Rep 14; *Oil Platforms (Islamic Republic of Iran v United States of America)* [2003] ICJ Rep 161.
- Responsibility and remedies: International Law Commission, Articles on Responsibility of States for Internationally Wrongful Acts (2001), arts 1-2, 4-11, 31, 34-37 and 49-54; *Gabcikovo-Nagymaros Project (Hungary/Slovakia)* [1997] ICJ Rep 7.
- Immunity and international criminal law: State Immunity Act 1978, ss 1-3; *Jurisdictional Immunities of the State (Germany v Italy: Greece intervening)* [2012] ICJ Rep 99; *Arrest Warrant of 11 April 2000 (Democratic Republic of the Congo v Belgium)* [2002] ICJ Rep 3; Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, arts 5, 17 and 27.

## Avoid
- Avoid private-international-law concepts unless the prompt asks.
- Avoid soft-law instruments as if binding without explanation.
