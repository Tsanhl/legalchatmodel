# Legal Ethics and Professional Regulation

## Answer Method
- Start with the professional role, client, retainer, regulator, conduct rule, conflict type, confidentiality issue, client-money issue, or professional-duty breach.
- Problems should separate duties to client, court/administration of justice, third parties, regulator, and public interest.
- End with practical action: decline/cease acting, informed consent where allowed, information barrier, disclosure, report, remediation, or disciplinary risk.

## Topic Map
- Conflicts, confidentiality, privilege, undertakings, client money, competence, independence, misleading the court, referrals, complaints, discipline and sanctions.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Ethics problems should identify the professional role, client identity, retainer scope and duty conflict before suggesting action.
- Duties to the court/administration of justice override client instructions where they conflict.
- Confidentiality, privilege and disclosure/reporting duties are related but not identical.
- Client-money and undertaking issues require immediate practical remediation, record correction and regulator notification where appropriate.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the client and duty-holder precisely.
- Distinguish own-interest conflict, client-client conflict, former-client confidentiality, and duty to the court.
- State whether consent can cure the problem or whether acting is prohibited.

## Case Brief Bank
- Bolkiah v KPMG: Facts: former-client confidential information and conflict. Held: firm must show no real risk of disclosure. Reasoning: strict protection of confidentiality. Answer use: former-client conflict/information barriers.
- Hilton v Barker Booth & Eastwood: Facts: solicitor acted despite conflict and dishonesty risk. Held: breach of duty to client. Reasoning: loyalty and disclosure duties. Answer use: conflicts.
- Three Rivers (No 5): Facts: legal advice privilege scope. Held: privilege attaches within defined client-lawyer communications. Reasoning: client identification matters. Answer use: privilege.
- R v Farooqi: Facts: advocates and misleading court. Held: duties to court and proper administration of justice are central. Reasoning: advocacy has ethical limits. Answer use: court duty.
- SRA Principles and Codes: Use current rules for independence, honesty, integrity, client interest, service, confidentiality, conflicts, undertakings and client money. Answer use: live rule source.

## Current SRA Rule Bank — verified 14 July 2026
- Use the SRA Principles and Code version in force from 11 April 2025. Principles 1–5 require respect for the rule of law and proper administration of justice, public trust, independence, honesty and integrity; Principle 7 protects each client's best interests. Where they conflict, the public-interest Principles and duties to the court take priority over an individual client's interests. (Solicitors Regulation Authority, *SRA Principles* (version in force 11 April 2025) Principles 1–5 and 7.)
- A solicitor must not mislead or attempt to mislead a client, the court or others by act or omission, or allow or be complicit in a client's misleading conduct. (Solicitors Regulation Authority, *SRA Code of Conduct for Solicitors, RELs, RFLs and RSLs* (version in force 11 April 2025) para 1.4.)
- In proceedings, do not misuse or tamper with evidence; advance only properly arguable assertions; avoid contempt; comply with court orders; and draw material authorities or procedural irregularities to the court's attention. (SRA Code of Conduct, paras 2.1, 2.4–2.7.)
- Confidentiality is the default, but paragraph 6.4(d) expressly permits a solicitor not to tell the client material information contained in a privileged document known only because it was mistakenly disclosed. That exception defeats an instruction to exploit the accident; it is not authority to destroy the document or conceal misconduct. (SRA Code of Conduct, paras 6.3–6.4(d).)
- For an accidentally received privileged document: stop reviewing and circulating it; segregate it and preserve the transmission/audit trail; advise the client that it cannot properly be used; notify the supervising partner/compliance function; assess the applicable court order, procedural rule and common-law restraint; communicate with the sender or seek urgent court directions where required. Do not automatically disclose the client's confidential admissions or report the client to the regulator without identifying a lawful/regulatory basis.
- If the client persists, do not follow the improper instruction. Explain the conflict and consequences in writing, avoid misleading the court or opponent, and consider ceasing to act in accordance with the retainer and procedural duties. Record the reasons so the decision can be justified under paragraph 7.2. (SRA Code of Conduct, paras 1.4, 2.1–2.7 and 7.2.)
- Primary live sources: `https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/principles/` and `https://www.sra.org.uk/solicitors/standards-regulations/code-conduct-solicitors/`. Recheck the version banner and paragraph numbering at answer time.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not treat client consent as a universal cure.
- State the required practical step: disclose, decline, cease acting, report, correct, obtain consent, protect privilege, or return money.
- Keep legal privilege separate from professional confidentiality and AML/reporting obligations.
- If rule numbers are needed, verify current SRA/BSB rules before final output.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers identify duty-holder, client, conflict type, rule source, whether consent is available, immediate action and sanction risk.
- Strong essays balance client loyalty, court duty, independence, confidentiality and public trust.
- Weaker answers give moral advice without professional rule or practical step.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current SRA/BSB rules and guidance are update-sensitive; verify live rule numbers before final output.
- Do not solve conflicts only by disclosure; some conflicts require refusal or ceasing to act.
- Keep legal privilege/confidentiality separate from regulatory reporting obligations.

## Avoid
- Avoid vague "professionalism" language without rule, duty and action.
- Avoid assuming client consent cures all conflicts.
