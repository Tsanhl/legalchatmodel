# Immigration and Refugee Law

## Answer Method
- Start with route: refugee status, humanitarian protection, inadmissibility, deportation, Article 8, family/private life, detention, appeal or judicial review.
- Problems should separate eligibility, exclusion/inadmissibility, risk on return, evidence, public interest, proportionality and remedy.
- End with likely grant/refusal, appeal ground, fresh claim, interim relief or removal risk.

## Topic Map
- Refugee definition, persecution, Convention reason, internal relocation, exclusion, non-refoulement, asylum procedure, deportation, Article 8, children, detention and appeals.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Immigration answers should identify procedural posture: initial decision, appeal, fresh claim, detention challenge, deportation, JR or interim relief.
- Refugee claims require well-founded fear, persecution, Convention reason, state protection, internal relocation and exclusion where raised.
- Human-rights protection should separate Article 3/non-refoulement from Article 8 family/private life and statutory public-interest factors.
- Deportation cases require criminality level, foreign-criminal provisions, exceptions, very compelling circumstances and children's best interests.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify status and procedural posture first.
- Separate Refugee Convention risk from Article 3/Article 8 human-rights claims.
- Apply credibility and country evidence cautiously.

## Case Brief Bank
- Horvath v Secretary of State for the Home Department: Facts: Roma claimant alleged persecution in Slovakia. Held: availability of state protection can defeat refugee status. Reasoning: protection need not be perfect. Answer use: sufficiency of protection.
- HJ (Iran) v Secretary of State for the Home Department: Facts: gay asylum claimants. Held: claimant cannot be expected to conceal identity to avoid persecution. Reasoning: fundamental identity and Convention protection. Answer use: discretion/concealment.
- Januzi v Secretary of State for the Home Department: Facts: internal relocation. Held: relocation must not be unduly harsh. Reasoning: practical safety and reasonableness. Answer use: internal flight.
- Chikwamba v Secretary of State for the Home Department: Facts: removal for entry-clearance application. Held: rigid removal may be disproportionate where outcome obvious. Reasoning: Article 8 practicality. Answer use: family-life proportionality.
- KO (Nigeria) v Secretary of State for the Home Department: Facts: deportation and children. Held: "unduly harsh" focuses on child impact, not parental culpability. Reasoning: statutory public-interest scheme. Answer use: deportation exceptions.
- R (Hardial Singh) v Governor of Durham Prison: Facts: immigration detention. Held: detention only for reasonable period and for removal purpose. Reasoning: implied limits on detention power. Answer use: detention.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not treat hardship, poverty or instability as persecution without Convention reason and severity.
- Use country guidance carefully and verify current country material where outcome-sensitive.
- For Article 8, identify family/private life facts and statutory public-interest considerations.
- State remedy: appeal allowed/dismissed, remittal, fresh claim, bail, interim relief or JR.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers identify route, status, risk, Convention reason, protection/relocation, human-rights fallback, public interest and remedy.
- Strong deportation answers handle children, criminality level and very compelling circumstances separately.
- Weaker answers merge asylum, Article 3 and Article 8 or omit procedural posture.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current Immigration Rules, country guidance and policy are update-sensitive; verify live sources where outcome depends on them.
- Non-refoulement, Article 3 and Article 8 have different thresholds.
- For deportation, identify criminality level, public interest, exceptions and very compelling circumstances where relevant.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Refugee protection: Refugee Convention 1951, arts 1A(2), 1F and 33; *Horvath v Secretary of State for the Home Department* [2000] UKHL 37, [2001] 1 AC 489; *HJ (Iran) v Secretary of State for the Home Department* [2010] UKSC 31, [2011] 1 AC 596; *Januzi v Secretary of State for the Home Department* [2006] UKHL 5, [2006] 2 AC 426.
- Human rights/deportation: Human Rights Act 1998, ss 6-8; Nationality, Immigration and Asylum Act 2002, ss 117A-117D; *KO (Nigeria) v Secretary of State for the Home Department* [2018] UKSC 53, [2018] 1 WLR 5273.
- Detention/procedure: *R v Governor of Durham Prison, ex p Hardial Singh* [1984] 1 WLR 704 (QB); Immigration Act 1971, Sch 2; Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007, s 13.
- Refugee Convention 1951 / Protocol 1967; Nationality and Borders Act 2022; Illegal Migration Act 2023 (as engaged — check commencement).
- Art 3 / non-refoulement: *Soering v United Kingdom* (1989) 11 EHRR 439; *R (Ullah) v Special Adjudicator* [2004] UKHL 26.
- Deportation / Art 8: *Huang v SSHD* [2007] UKHL 11; *Agyarko v SSHD* [2017] UKSC 11.
- Safe third country / Rwanda litigation: verify the latest UKSC / treaty position before advising — do not rely on superseded High Court holdings alone.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Border Security, Asylum and Immigration Act 2025 received Royal Assent on 2 December 2025. Verify commencement before relying on new border, advice-regulation, detention, EUSS or facilitation provisions.
- Current government materials state that the 2025 Act repeals the Safety of Rwanda (Asylum and Immigration) Act 2024 and parts of the Illegal Migration Act 2023, including the duty to remove and associated provisions. Do not use pre-2025 Rwanda/Illegal Migration Act routes without checking repeal/commencement.

## Avoid
- Avoid treating hardship as automatically persecution.
- Avoid Article 8 balancing without public-interest factors and evidence.
