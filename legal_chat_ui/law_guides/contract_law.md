# Contract Law

## Answer Method
- Identify the contractual route before remedies: formation, terms, vitiating factor, breach, exclusion, unfairness, frustration, or damages.
- Problems should follow chronology: negotiations -> formation -> terms -> breach/vitiation -> defences/exclusions -> remedies.
- Essays need a thesis on autonomy, certainty, fairness, reliance, risk allocation, or piecemeal control.
- Essay answers should break the question into its actual sub-questions and return to the wording throughout; if the question is divided into numbered or lettered parts, mirror that structure.
- Distinguish representation, term, collateral warranty, and mere opinion.

## Topic Map
- Offer/acceptance, consideration, intention, certainty; incorporation and interpretation of terms.
- Misrepresentation, mistake, duress, undue influence, unconscionability/unfairness controls.
- Breach, damages, remoteness, mitigation, specific performance, termination.
- Exclusion clauses, UCTA, CRA, entire-agreement and non-reliance clauses.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Problem answers should separate contract formation, content, vitiation, breach, termination, and remedies; avoid mixing all arguments into one enforceability section.
- Terms/exclusion answers need incorporation, construction, statutory control, breach type, causation, and remedy.
- Vitiating-factor answers need representation/pressure/mistake, inducement or causation, claimant conduct, bars to rescission, and damages.
- Mistake essays should test whether the modern law genuinely clarifies earlier authority or merely narrows equitable flexibility; compare doctrinal certainty with fairness rather than summarising cases.
- Damages answers require expectation/reliance choice, remoteness, mitigation, causation, agreed damages, and equitable remedies where relevant.

## For Each Topic Include
- Formation: agreement, consideration, intention, certainty.
- Terms: incorporation, construction, implied terms, classification, breach.
- Misrepresentation: actionable statement, inducement, category, remedy, exclusion/non-reliance control.
- Damages: causation, remoteness, mitigation, measure, loss proof.

## Case Brief Bank
- Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball: Facts: advertised reward. Held: unilateral offer accepted by performance. Reasoning: intention and consideration shown by deposit/performance. Answer use: formation.
- Williams v Roffey: Facts: promise to pay more for performance. Held: practical benefit can support consideration. Reasoning: commercial reality and absence of duress. Answer use: consideration.
- Hadley v Baxendale: Facts: late mill shaft delivery. Held: damages limited by contemplation. Reasoning: ordinary and special communicated losses. Answer use: remoteness.
- Hong Kong Fir: Facts: breach classification dispute. Held: innominate terms require effects analysis. Reasoning: remedy depends on deprivation of benefit. Answer use: termination.
- Photo Production v Securicor: Facts: exclusion clause after fundamental breach. Held: construction controls exclusion. Reasoning: freedom of contract and clause wording. Answer use: exclusion clauses.
- Misrepresentation Act 1967, s 2(1): pre-contract false statement inducing the contract gives damages as if fraudulent unless the maker proves reasonable grounds to believe it true (Howard Marine). Answer use: misrepresentation remedies.
- Misrepresentation Act 1967, s 3 (as amended by UCTA 1977): a term excluding or restricting misrepresentation liability (including non-reliance/entire-agreement clauses raising contractual estoppel) is effective only so far as it satisfies the UCTA reasonableness test (First Tower Trustees v CDS). Answer use: non-reliance clauses.
- UCTA 1977, ss 3, 11 and Sch 2: in business-to-business contracts on written standard terms, exclusion of liability for breach must be reasonable, judged at contract time by bargaining strength, availability of alternatives, insurance. Answer use: exclusion clauses.
- Smith New Court v Scrimgeour Vickers: Facts: fraudulent misrepresentation share sale. Held: fraud damages cover all direct losses, no remoteness cap. Answer use: fraud vs s 2(1) damages.
- Cavendish v Makdessi: Facts: penalty clause challenge. Held: penalty test asks legitimate interest and proportionality. Reasoning: not limited to genuine pre-estimate. Answer use: agreed damages.
- First Tower Trustees Ltd v CDS (Superstores International) Ltd: a non-reliance clause that prevents proof of reliance can fall within Misrepresentation Act 1967, s 3 and must satisfy UCTA reasonableness; do not let the label "basis clause" decide the substance.
- St Albans City and District Council v International Computers Ltd: a very low software-contract liability cap on standard terms may be unreasonable, with bargaining strength, insurance and available alternatives relevant. Answer use: technology-contract caps under UCTA.
- Watford Electronics Ltd v Sanderson CFL Ltd: negotiated risk allocation between experienced commercial parties is given substantial weight, but it does not create automatic enforceability. Answer use: reasonableness and bargaining context.
- Transocean Drilling UK Ltd v Providence Resources plc: clear wording can allocate specified categories of loss, and the label "consequential" must be read in its contractual context. Answer use: distinguish expressly listed direct loss from Hadley's second-limb indirect/consequential loss.
- Bannerman v White: a statement's importance to the recipient can support classification as a term. Answer use: specific pre-contract assurances.
- Dick Bentley Productions Ltd v Harold Smith (Motors) Ltd and Oscar Chess Ltd v Williams: relative expertise helps distinguish a contractual term from a representation. Answer use: a specialist host's security/location assurances.
- Derry v Peek: fraud requires knowledge of falsity, absence of honest belief, or recklessness; do not infer fraud merely because the statement later proves false.
- Edgington v Fitzmaurice and Redgrave v Hurd: an actionable misrepresentation must operate on the claimant's mind and induce entry; it need not be the only reason, and an opportunity to verify does not by itself defeat reliance.
- British Westinghouse Electric v Underground Electric Railways: the claimant must take reasonable steps to mitigate post-breach loss. Answer use: client-loss and remediation evidence.

## Feedback Rules
- Push contractual-term arguments where statements are specific, important, and made by a party with superior expertise.
- Do not treat unfairness as one doctrine; identify the control device.
- Analyse exclusion/non-reliance clauses by construction first, then statutory control.
- A contract essay must defend an answer to the exact proposition. Avoid writing a general survey of formation, mistake, frustration, or damages because the topic is familiar.
- When the question asks whether a case or doctrine is significant, analyse what changed in later law and what practical problem remains.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Classification before remedy: term, representation, collateral warranty, misrepresentation, breach, mistake, duress, undue influence, frustration and unfairness controls have different gateways and remedies.
- Exclusion and non-reliance clauses need four stages: incorporation, construction, statutory or misrepresentation control, and practical effect on damages/rescission.
- Termination needs breach classification and election discipline: condition, innominate term, repudiation, affirmation, waiver, accrued rights and restitution after termination where relevant.
- Damages must separate expectation/reliance, causation, remoteness, mitigation, loss of bargain, agreed damages/penalty and equitable remedies. Do not treat remoteness as the same as causation.
- Technology-hosting problems need a clause-by-clause matrix: the promised location/security standard; the actual patching/processing breach; each exclusion and cap; the loss category; the applicable UCTA route; then causation, remoteness, mitigation and proof. Do not assume "loss of profits" is indirect merely because it is listed beside indirect loss.
- A late-payment sum engages the penalty doctrine only if it is a secondary obligation triggered by breach. Identify the innocent party's legitimate interest and compare the stipulated detriment with that interest; a commercially extravagant fixed sum is vulnerable even if labelled a charge.
- Non-reliance analysis must ask what the clause does in substance. If it excludes liability for or prevents proof of a pre-contractual misrepresentation, apply Misrepresentation Act 1967, s 3 and UCTA reasonableness before treating it as contractual estoppel.
- Factual integrity is non-negotiable: do not copy a clause number, quoted wording, party fact or procedural finding from a retrieved authority into the hypothetical. The question is the only source of problem facts.
- Essays should define the central tension, such as certainty versus fairness or autonomy versus control, then use cases to prove that thesis rather than narrating doctrine chronologically.
- Consideration essays must distinguish three different settings. *Stilk v Myrick* is the orthodox existing-duty rule; *Williams v Roffey* allows a factual practical benefit to support a promise to pay more where there is no duress or fraud; *Foakes v Beer* preserves the rule that part-payment of an undisputed debt is not consideration for surrendering the balance. Never attribute practical-benefit reasoning to *Foakes*.
- *Williams v Roffey* did not overrule *Foakes v Beer*. *Rock Advertising Ltd v MWB Business Exchange Centres Ltd* left the practical-benefit/part-payment issue unresolved because the no-oral-modification clause disposed of the appeal. The resulting asymmetry is a central criticism, not an exception to ignore.
- Promissory estoppel is equitable, not statutory and not a product of the Misrepresentation Act 1967. Use *High Trees* for the modern principle, *Combe v Combe* for the ordinary shield-not-sword limit, *Collier* cautiously on part-payment, and *Rock Advertising* for modern restraint. Analyse clear promise, reliance/alteration of position, inequity, and whether relief suspends or extinguishes rights.
- A first-class reform conclusion should compare abolition with replacement by intention, reliance or a statutory modification regime. Do not equate doctrinal flexibility with fairness; address bargain, evidentiary and cautionary functions as well as nominal consideration, existing duties and debt composition.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Verify the current text and application of the Sale of Goods Act 1979, Misrepresentation Act 1967 and Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 against legislation.gov.uk before relying on a section or amendment.
- For consumer facts, check whether the Consumer Rights Act 2015 displaces or supplements the business-to-business analysis; do not assume the parties' status.
- For a recent or specialist transaction, search current official judgments for any later treatment of the leading authority before presenting the older case as exhaustive.

## Avoid
- Avoid remedy discussion before liability route.
- Avoid treating consumer and business controls as interchangeable.
