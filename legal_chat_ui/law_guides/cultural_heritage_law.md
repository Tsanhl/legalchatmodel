# Cultural Heritage Law

## Answer Method
- Start with the object, origin, date of removal, title chain, applicable domestic law, international convention, limitation, good faith and recovery route.
- Problems should separate ownership/title, export controls, private international law, criminal/regulatory route, restitution diplomacy and practical enforcement.
- Essays need a thesis on property, cultural identity, market certainty, colonial context, public interest or restitution justice.

## Topic Map
- Illicit trafficking, export controls, stolen objects, limitation, lex situs, good-faith purchase, museum duties, UNESCO/UNIDROIT-style frameworks, restitution and repatriation.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Cultural heritage problems require a title-and-time map: origin, removal date, export law, acquisition chain, current possessor, forum and limitation.
- Separate legal recovery from ethical restitution; moral strength does not automatically prove title or a private-law remedy.
- Conflict-of-laws analysis is often decisive because title may depend on the lex situs at each transfer.
- Public museum, sanctions, export-control and criminal routes may create leverage even where private title recovery is weak.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the time and place of removal/acquisition.
- Separate moral claim from legal title/recovery route.
- End with strongest legal route and non-legal restitution pressure.

## Case Brief Bank
- Sale of Goods Act 1979, s 21: under English law a non-owner normally cannot pass a better title than they possess, subject to defined exceptions.
- Limitation Act 1980, s 4: actions concerning stolen property have a special postponement structure; do not apply a simple six-year period from export or museum acquisition.
- Dealing in Cultural Objects (Offences) Act 2003, s 1: dishonest dealing in a cultural object known or believed to be tainted is a criminal offence.
- Winkworth v Christie Manson & Woods: Facts: stolen Japanese works sold abroad then returned to England. Held: foreign transfer law could determine title. Reasoning: lex situs governs transfer of movable property. Answer use: conflict of laws/title.
- Attorney-General of New Zealand v Ortiz: Facts: carved panels exported contrary to New Zealand law. Held: foreign public/export laws were not directly enforceable as property claims. Reasoning: limits on enforcing foreign public laws. Answer use: export controls/private recovery.
- Iran v Barakat Galleries: Facts: state claimed antiquities removed from Iran. Held: foreign patrimony law could create proprietary rights recognisable in England. Reasoning: property claim distinguished from enforcing penal/public law. Answer use: state ownership statutes.
- City of Gotha v Sotheby's: Facts: stolen painting sold through market. Held: limitation and title issues required close analysis. Reasoning: recovery depends on title chain and limitation, not morality alone. Answer use: stolen art claims.
- Government of the Islamic Republic of Iran v The Barakat Galleries Ltd: Answer use: use the full title where cited and explain patrimony-law route carefully.

## Feedback Rules
- Always distinguish state ownership/patrimony law from export prohibition.
- Do not assume UNESCO or UNIDROIT instruments apply retrospectively or directly without domestic implementation.
- If limitation is raised, identify whether the claim is conversion, recovery of goods, restitution, or public-law/export enforcement.
- Include practical settlement/repatriation pressure only after legal routes are analysed.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers map object, title chain, situs, limitation, treaty/domestic route, public-law leverage and remedy.
- Strong essays evaluate market certainty, cultural identity, colonial injustice and rule-of-law constraints without collapsing them.
- Weaker answers treat cultural significance as ownership or rely on soft law as if binding.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current treaty implementation and sanctions/export rules are update-sensitive.
- Do not assume international conventions apply retrospectively.
- Check title, limitation and conflict-of-laws issues before policy.

## Avoid
- Avoid treating cultural significance as itself proof of legal ownership.
- Avoid relying on soft-law restitution norms as binding without explanation.
