# Jurisprudence

## Answer Method
- Start with the theoretical dispute: positivism, natural law, rule of law, legal certainty, discretion, interpretation, legitimacy, authority or justice.
- Essays need a thesis, defined concepts, named theorists, concrete legal examples and evaluative conclusion.
- Do not write a neutral textbook survey where the question asks whether one account is convincing.

## Topic Map
- Hart, Austin and Raz; legal positivism, separability, rule of recognition, sources thesis and authority.
- Aquinas, Fuller and Finnis; natural law, practical reason, legality and the moral defect of unjust law.
- Dworkin; principles, rights, constructive interpretation and law as integrity.
- Holmes, Llewellyn and Frank; prediction, law in action, judicial behaviour and factual indeterminacy.
- Feminist jurisprudence; public/private power, dominance, care, embodiment and intersectionality.
- Critical race theory; structural racism, interest convergence, rights critique and intersectionality.
- Marxist theory; class, ideology, material relations, commodity form and relative autonomy.
- Postcolonial theory and TWAIL; empire, sovereignty, legal pluralism, racial hierarchy and epistemic power.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Jurisprudence essays should define the contested concept, identify rival theories, use legal examples and defend a final position.
- Positivism answers must distinguish separability, sources thesis, validity, obligation and moral evaluation.
- Rule-of-law answers should separate formal legality, procedural legality and substantive justice.
- Interpretation answers should connect theory to actual judicial reasoning rather than abstract preference.
- Multi-theory essays must ask what dimension each theory explains: validity, authority, interpretation, prediction, legitimacy, political economy, race, gender or empire. Do not treat incompatible theories as interchangeable.

## For Each Topic Include
- Define the contested concept before evaluation.
- Pair theory with legal examples.
- State why one account better explains law or legal reasoning.
- Give each critical theory a distinct mechanism of legal power; feminism, critical race theory, Marxism and postcolonial theory are not one generic fairness critique.

## Case Brief Bank
- Hart: Use The Concept of Law for rule of recognition, primary/secondary rules, internal point of view and separability. Answer use: positivism and legal system structure.
- Fuller: Use The Morality of Law for internal morality, legality principles and the Grudge Informer debate. Answer use: natural law and legality.
- Dworkin: Use Taking Rights Seriously and Law's Empire for rights thesis, principles and law as integrity. Answer use: hard cases and adjudication.
- Raz: Use authority/service conception and sources thesis. Answer use: authority and exclusive positivism.
- Finnis: Use natural-law account of basic goods and practical reasonableness. Answer use: modern natural law.
- Holmes, Llewellyn and Frank: use prediction, law in action, situation-sense and fact scepticism to test the gap between appellate doctrine and institutional behaviour. Answer use: realism.
- MacKinnon and Smart: use dominance and law's production of gendered subjects while distinguishing liberal, radical, relational and intersectional feminist approaches. Answer use: feminist jurisprudence.
- Crenshaw and Bell: use intersectionality, structural racial power and interest convergence; distinguish critique of rights from strategic rights claims. Answer use: critical race theory.
- Marx and Pashukanis: use material relations, ideology and commodity-form analysis; test reductionism against law's relative autonomy and capacity to constrain dominant interests. Answer use: Marxist theory.
- Anghie and TWAIL: use the colonial formation of sovereignty and international law, legal pluralism and continuing hierarchy. Answer use: postcolonial theory.
- R (UNISON) v Lord Chancellor: Facts: tribunal fees restricted access. Held: access to justice is constitutional. Answer use: concrete rule-of-law example.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not name theorists without stating their argument and applying it.
- Avoid "both sides are valid" endings; choose the better explanation for the question.
- Use legal examples sparingly but concretely: hard cases, statutory interpretation, unjust laws, access to justice or rights adjudication.
- Keep descriptive, conceptual and normative claims separate.
- A defensible pluralist thesis does not say every theory is equally true. Rank them by explanatory task and identify conflicts: positivism can explain source-based validity while critical theories explain whose power those sources institutionalise.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band essays define terms, compare theories, use concrete examples, evaluate objections and defend a thesis.
- Strong answers show why a theory explains legal practice better or worse, not merely what the theory says.
- Weaker answers are theorist summaries or personal political views without jurisprudential structure.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Keep conceptual claim, descriptive claim and normative claim distinct.
- For Hart/Fuller questions, identify whether the disagreement is about validity, morality, efficacy or fidelity to law.
- For certainty/justice questions, explain the trade-off rather than treating certainty as always good or always bad.
- Do not reduce positivism to "law is whatever the state commands"; distinguish Austin from Hart and Raz.
- Do not claim natural law simply denies that wicked enactments are law; distinguish legal validity, focal meaning, moral obligation and legality.
- Do not present realism as saying rules never matter, feminism as a single voice, CRT as only individual prejudice, Marxism as crude economic determination, or postcolonial theory as confined to past empire.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Essays must separate descriptive UK constitutional practice from normative theory.
- Cite cases for legal propositions; cite theorists as secondary literature only.

## Avoid
- Avoid name-dropping theorists without applying their argument.
- Avoid reducing jurisprudence to personal political opinion.
