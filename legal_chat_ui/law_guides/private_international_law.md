# Private International Law

## Answer Method
- Keep jurisdiction, applicable law, recognition/enforcement, and anti-suit/freezing relief separate.
- Problems need a forum-by-forum map and practical litigation advice.
- Essays need a thesis on certainty, party autonomy, comity, access to justice, or post-Brexit coherence.
- Always identify the governing instrument or common-law regime before applying cases.

## Topic Map
- Jurisdiction: service, gateways, forum conveniens, exclusive jurisdiction clauses, parallel proceedings.
- Applicable law: Rome I, Rome II, common-law residual issues, mandatory rules, foreign law proof.
- Recognition/enforcement: judgments, arbitral awards, public policy, natural justice.
- Interim relief: freezing injunctions, anti-suit injunctions, arbitration-related relief.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Conflict answers must be sequenced: jurisdiction first, then applicable law, then recognition/enforcement, then interim relief if needed.
- Cross-border commercial problems should be mapped around the three backbone questions: which court can hear the case, which law governs the dispute, and whether any judgment or award can be recognised/enforced.
- For essays about incentives to sue in England, compare the front-end jurisdiction gateway with the back-end recognition/enforcement gateway. English law may be relatively open at the service/forum stage while remaining stricter when recognising a foreign judgment later.
- Hague 2019 answers must state status, scope, exclusions, commencement/entry-into-force limits, and contracting-state reciprocity before treating it as a full replacement for Brussels/Lugano-style circulation. Hague 2019 is a partial repair of recognition/enforcement, not a complete jurisdiction code.
- Freezing-injunction questions need the jurisdictional basis, domestic/international reach, ancillary nature, asset risk, undertaking/cross-undertaking, and enforcement practicality.
- Post-Brexit questions must identify whether the relevant regime is common law, retained/assimilated EU law, Hague, Lugano-related, Rome I/Rome II, arbitration, or residual doctrine.
- Post-Brexit civil/commercial jurisdiction answers often become structurally asymmetrical: English jurisdiction may be common-law/CPR-based while recognition abroad depends on the foreign forum, Hague, domestic law or residual rules. State that asymmetry instead of forcing a single Brussels-style framework.
- Service-out analysis should use CPR 6.36/6.37 and PD6B gateway discipline: serious issue to be tried, good arguable gateway, and England as the proper place/forum. The gateway is not itself the forum conveniens analysis.
- Presence/submission/agreement should be separated from service-out permission. A company is not automatically present in England merely because a group company or subsidiary is present.
- Cryptocurrency/cross-border asset questions need situs/characterisation discipline before remedies.
- Foreign mandatory-rule problems require performance location, unlawfulness/illegality abroad, policy strength, party expectations, and whether the forum should give effect to the foreign rule.

## For Each Topic Include
- Jurisdiction: basis of jurisdiction -> gateway/permission -> serious issue -> forum conveniens -> case-management result.
- Choice of law: characterise issue, identify connecting factor, test exceptions/mandatory rules.
- Enforcement: instrument/common-law route, finality, jurisdictional competence, presence/submission/agreement, natural justice/fraud/public policy defences, and asset practicality.

## Case Brief Bank
- Spiliada: Facts: competing forums. Held: stay depends on clearly more appropriate forum. Reasoning: connecting factors and justice to parties matter. Answer use: forum non conveniens.
- Owusu: Facts: EU domicile and non-EU forum. Held: Brussels regime limited forum non conveniens. Reasoning: certainty and mandatory jurisdiction. Answer use: retained/EU contrast.
- Fiona Trust: Facts: arbitration clause scope. Held: presumption of one-stop adjudication. Reasoning: commercial parties intend disputes to be resolved in one forum. Answer use: arbitration clause construction.
- Enka v Chubb: Facts: governing law of arbitration agreement. Held: structured choice-law approach. Reasoning: express/implied choice and closest connection. Answer use: arbitration and applicable law.
- Vedanta v Lungowe: Facts: parent company and foreign harm. Held: jurisdiction and duty issues can justify English proceedings. Reasoning: access to justice and real issue analysis. Answer use: cross-border corporate litigation.
- Brownlie II: Facts: tort gateway and foreign accident litigation. Held: gateway analysis turns on properly characterised damage and a good arguable case. Reasoning: service-out gateways are threshold filters, not merits adjudication. Answer use: tort gateway discipline.
- Adams v Cape: Facts: foreign judgment enforcement and corporate presence. Held: presence/submission/recognised jurisdictional bases are required at common law. Reasoning: corporate group links do not by themselves equal presence. Answer use: recognition/enforcement and company presence.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not merge merits and jurisdiction.
- Do not treat “England is convenient” as enough; weigh connecting factors and justice.
- State litigation sequence: claim form/service, challenge/stay, interim relief, enforcement.
- Separate the front-end jurisdiction question from the back-end recognition/enforcement question. The rules that let England hear a claim are not identical to the rules that make a foreign judgment enforceable in England.
- When arguing that England is attractive, identify the exact incentive: wider service-out gateway, claimant-friendly forum analysis, access-to-justice factor, interim relief, quality of commercial courts, or easier domestic enforcement. Do not leave the incentive as a vague reputation point.
- Recognition/enforcement sections must identify whose judgment is being enforced, in which forum, against which assets, and through which regime. Without that practical map the legal rule is under-applied.
- Keep the introduction separate from the substantive parts. End the introduction with the thesis, the governing regimes, and the reason the order of issues matters.
- Define technical labels on first use: gateway, forum conveniens, applicable law, mandatory rules, recognition, enforcement, anti-suit relief, and arbitral-seat concepts.
- If an issue is excluded, such as choice of law or enforcement, say why it is outside the question instead of silently omitting it.
- Do not present cases as detached snippets. For each authority, state the governing principle, the exception or limit, and the practical consequence for forum strategy.
- For essay structure, prefer governing principle then exception then implication. Avoid unexplained “this-but-however” chains.
- Comparative points must have a purpose: certainty, party autonomy, comity, access to justice, enforcement realism, or post-Brexit coherence.
- End each major section with the current position and litigation implication, not just a neutral summary of the authorities.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Map forum and issue first: jurisdiction, service, stay/forum conveniens, applicable law, interim relief, recognition and enforcement must not be merged.
- Post-Brexit problems need instrument status before cases: common law, Hague, Lugano-related issues, Rome I/Rome II, arbitration, retained/assimilated EU law or residual doctrine.
- For post-Brexit essays, test the asymmetry thesis: the front-end question is whether England may hear the claim; the back-end question is whether a foreign judgment can later be recognised and enforced in England. Explain how that asymmetry affects litigation strategy.
- Jurisdiction/service answers need gateway, serious issue, good arguable case, proper forum, permission/service mechanics and challenge timing where relevant.
- Choice-of-law answers must characterise the issue, identify the connecting factor, then test exception, mandatory rule, illegality and public policy.
- Recognition/enforcement answers require finality, jurisdictional competence, natural justice, fraud/public policy and practical assets/enforcement sequencing.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic international-law discussion.
- Avoid using EU instruments without checking retained/post-Brexit status.

## Anonymized Output Architecture
- A post-exit jurisdiction and enforcement essay should test front-end service and forum rules separately from back-end recognition and execution, verify the current treaty position, and explain how that asymmetry changes forum and asset strategy.
- End each major section with both the current legal position and its litigation consequence; abstract claims about comity or attractiveness are insufficient without the legal mechanism.
