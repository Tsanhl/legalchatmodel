# Aviation Law

## Answer Method
- Start with the governing regime: Montreal Convention, domestic carriage contract, consumer route, negligence fallback, or regulatory complaint.
- Passenger-injury problems need route discipline: international carriage -> Article 17 accident/bodily injury -> Article 21 liability tier -> defences/limits -> damages and limitation.
- Do not treat ordinary turbulence, delay, baggage, and injury as the same legal route.

## Topic Map
- International carriage, carrier liability, passenger injury, baggage, delay, jurisdiction, limitation, damages, exclusivity, and consumer/regulatory remedies.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Passenger injury answers should start with the Warsaw/Montreal regime and only then consider domestic contract, tort or consumer routes.
- Cargo and baggage answers need documentary chronology: ticket/air waybill, checked baggage, delay notice, damage report, limitation period and recoverable loss.
- Regulatory complaints should be kept distinct from private damages claims; compensation schemes may assist but do not replace the Convention analysis.
- Jurisdiction and limitation points often decide aviation problems before liability merits.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the journey and whether the convention applies.
- Separate accident, injury, causation, liability limits, defences, jurisdiction and remedies.
- End with the strongest route and any practical evidence needed.

## Case Brief Bank
- Air France v Saks: Facts: passenger suffered hearing loss after normal cabin-pressure operation. Held: Article 17 accident requires an unexpected or unusual external event. Reasoning: injury alone is not enough. Answer use: accident threshold.
- Morris v KLM: Facts: sexual assault by another passenger during international carriage. Held: carrier liability could arise where the event was an accident within Article 17. Reasoning: focus is on the unusual external event, not carrier fault. Answer use: passenger assault and accident.
- Sidhu v British Airways: Facts: passengers detained during Gulf War stopover. Held: Convention route is exclusive where it applies. Reasoning: uniform international regime displaces parallel domestic claims. Answer use: exclusivity.
- Stott v Thomas Cook: Facts: disabled passenger alleged breach of EU disability rules during flight. Held: damages claim was barred by Convention exclusivity. Reasoning: domestic/EU rights could not bypass the Convention damages regime. Answer use: regulatory rights versus damages.
- Deep Vein Thrombosis litigation: Facts: passengers alleged DVT from flying conditions. Held: ordinary flight conditions were not an Article 17 accident. Reasoning: no unexpected external event. Answer use: ordinary risk versus accident.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not begin with negligence merely because the facts mention injury; ask first whether international carriage and Article 17 apply.
- Distinguish bodily injury, psychiatric harm, baggage loss, delay and denied boarding because each route has different limits and remedies.
- Where damages are limited, explain the practical effect of limitation rather than only stating liability.
- If the passenger relies on disability, consumer or regulatory protections, explain whether those routes create enforcement rights, compensation rights, or both.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers identify the carriage regime, Article 17/19 route, limitation period, exclusivity, liability tier, jurisdiction and remedy.
- Strong problem answers give a practical result: whether the airline pays, whether damages are capped, and what evidence is needed.
- Weaker answers treat all passenger complaints as negligence, omit exclusivity, or ignore limitation and jurisdiction.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Check the applicable convention and limitation period before common-law analysis.
- Keep Article 17 accident/injury distinct from Article 19 delay and baggage rules.
- Do not overstate strict liability; identify the relevant liability tier and any available defence.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank

- *Air France v Saks* 470 US 392 (1985).
- *Morris v KLM Royal Dutch Airlines* [2002] UKHL 7, [2002] 2 AC 628.
- *Sidhu v British Airways plc* [1997] AC 430 (HL).
- *Stott v Thomas Cook Tour Operators Ltd* [2014] UKSC 15, [2014] AC 1347.
- *In re Deep Vein Thrombosis and Air Travel Group Litigation* [2005] UKHL 72, [2006] 1 AC 495.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- The Montreal Convention is the 1999 convention, not a 1929 instrument. The Warsaw Convention was signed in 1929 and belongs to the predecessor regime.
- Separate standardised cancellation/long-delay rights under the assimilated UK air-passenger regime from proof of recoverable delay damage under Montreal Convention art 19. Never say every delay over three hours is compensable under art 19.
- Checked-baggage destruction, loss or damage is addressed by art 17(2); delay by art 19; complaint notice requirements by art 31; and the two-year extinguishment period by art 35.
- Convention exclusivity prevents a parallel domestic damages route where the claim falls within its substantive and temporal scope. It does not erase every regulatory or declaratory right.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic negligence analysis before checking the carriage regime.
- Avoid assuming all in-flight injury is an Article 17 accident.
