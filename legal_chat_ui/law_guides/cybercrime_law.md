# Cybercrime Law

## Answer Method
- Start with conduct: unauthorised access, impairment, fraud, blackmail, harassment, malicious communications, data misuse, jurisdiction or extradition.
- Problems should separate each offence: actus reus, mens rea, jurisdiction, evidence, defences and charging outcome.
- Essays need a thesis on overbreadth, harm, deterrence, enforcement, anonymity, cross-border cooperation or reform.

## Topic Map
- Computer misuse, ransomware, unauthorised access, impairment, fraud, communications offences, harassment, cyberstalking, data offences, jurisdiction, evidence and mutual legal assistance.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Cybercrime problem answers should translate facts into conduct elements: access, authorisation boundary, impairment, intent, gain/loss, threat, communication and evidence.
- Keep Computer Misuse Act offences separate from Fraud Act, blackmail, harassment, malicious communications, data-protection and proceeds-of-crime routes.
- Cross-border facts require jurisdiction, extradition/mutual assistance and evidential practicality rather than an assumption of easy prosecution.
- Sentencing and charging advice should identify harm, culpability, scale, planning, vulnerability and public-interest factors.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the system, access permission, conduct and intended/actual harm.
- Keep cyber-specific offences separate from fraud/blackmail/harassment overlays.
- For platform problems, separate individual criminal liability from Online Safety Act provider duties, Ofcom compliance/enforcement, data disclosure and privacy.
- End with strongest charges or duties, evidential gaps, sentencing/remedy exposure and any current-law checks.

## Case Brief Bank
- DPP v Bignell: Facts: police officers accessed information for unauthorised purposes. Held: access with permission to the system may not be unauthorised merely because purpose is improper. Reasoning: authorisation boundary matters. Answer use: Computer Misuse Act access.
- R v Bow Street Magistrates' Court, ex p Allison: Facts: employee used authorised access dishonestly. Held: exceeding authority can amount to unauthorised access depending on scope. Reasoning: permission is fact-sensitive. Answer use: insider access.
- R v Gold and Schifreen: Facts: unauthorised use of Prestel credentials. Held: old forgery law was inadequate, prompting computer-misuse reform. Reasoning: cyber-specific criminalisation needed. Answer use: reform/background.
- Collins v DPP: Facts: message sent by electronic communications network. Held: "grossly offensive" communication is judged by ordinary standards. Reasoning: criminal communication threshold. Answer use: online abuse.
- Chambers v DPP: Facts: joke threat on Twitter. Held: context matters when assessing menacing character. Reasoning: not every foolish online message is criminal. Answer use: communications offences and context.

## Feedback Rules
- Avoid "hacking" as a conclusion; identify access, authorisation and mens rea.
- Do not rely only on online harm language; match each offence to statutory elements.
- For ransomware, analyse unauthorised impairment, blackmail/fraud, money laundering and evidential tracing separately.
- If data misuse is alleged, separate criminal offences from civil/regulatory UK GDPR consequences.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers classify each cyber act into offence, AR/MR, jurisdiction, evidence, defence and likely charge.
- Strong essays evaluate overbreadth, authorisation, harm and enforcement limits with concrete statutory examples.
- Weaker answers list statutes without matching conduct and mental element.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current cybercrime statutes, sentencing and online-safety/communications rules are update-sensitive.
- Do not treat terms-of-service breach as automatically criminal access without analysing authorisation.
- For cross-border facts, address jurisdiction and enforcement practicality.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Computer misuse and fraud: Computer Misuse Act 1990, ss 1, 2, 3, 3A, 3ZA, 4 and 5; Fraud Act 2006, ss 1-4 and 6-7; Theft Act 1968, s 21; Proceeds of Crime Act 2002, ss 327-329.
- Communications, threats and intimate images: Online Safety Act 2023, Pt 10, especially ss 179-184; Online Safety Act 2023, ss 187-188 inserting Sexual Offences Act 2003, ss 66A-66B; Communications Act 2003, s 127 where still applicable; Malicious Communications Act 1988, s 1 where still applicable; Protection from Harassment Act 1997, ss 1-3.
- Authorisation and online speech: *R v Bow Street Magistrates' Court, ex p Allison* [2000] 2 AC 216 (HL); *DPP v Bignell* [1998] 1 Cr App R 1 (DC); *R v Gold and Schifreen* [1988] AC 1063 (HL); *Chambers v DPP* [2012] EWHC 2157 (Admin), [2013] 1 WLR 1833; *Collins v DPP* [2006] UKHL 40, [2006] 1 WLR 2223.
- Platform duties and enforcement: Online Safety Act 2023, Pts 3-4 and 7; Ofcom illegal-content risk-assessment, safety-duties, record-keeping, review and enforcement guidance should be checked for current operational duties.
- Evidence, seizure and disclosure: Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 powers and Codes; Criminal Justice and Police Act 2001 seizure/filtering powers where mixed electronic material is seized; Investigatory Powers Act 2016 and communications-data routes where platform-held data is sought; Article 8 ECHR and Data Protection Act 2018/UK GDPR for privacy controls.
- Cross-border route: Convention on Cybercrime (Budapest Convention), arts 22, 24, 25, 29, 31 and 32; Computer Misuse Act 1990, ss 4-5 for jurisdiction/significant-link analysis.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Online Safety Act 2023 imposes platform duties separate from criminal liability. Ofcom's 2026 illegal-content duties material requires in-scope services to carry out illegal-content risk assessment, put protections in place, and comply with record-keeping/review duties.
- Part 10 communications offences came into force on 31 January 2024. Do not analyse online abuse only through Communications Act 2003, s 127 if Online Safety Act 2023 offence routes are engaged.
- For false or threatening communications, check Online Safety Act 2023, ss 179-184; for intimate-image or cyberflashing facts, check the Sexual Offences Act 2003 amendments and current commencement.

## Problem Route: Stolen Credentials, Ransomware Tools And Platform Reports
- Teenager: start with Computer Misuse Act 1990, s 1 for access using stolen credentials, then consider s 2 if access was with intent to commit or facilitate further offences, s 3 if files/systems were impaired, and s 3A if ransomware tools were made, supplied, obtained or adapted for CMA offending.
- Exam files: consider fraud, dishonesty, data/confidentiality, school disciplinary/public-interest issues and evidence of copying; do not assume theft of information under Theft Act 1968 unless a recognised property route is present.
- Group chat: separate possession/obtaining of tools, encouragement/assistance, conspiracy or participation evidence from mere presence in a chat.
- Platform: identify whether the service is in scope, whether reported threats/intimate images are illegal content, whether risk assessment and safety duties were met, and whether delay creates Ofcom enforcement exposure rather than private criminal liability for the provider.
- Investigators: check lawful device seizure, digital-material filtering, platform disclosure route, Article 8/privacy proportionality and continuity/admissibility of digital evidence.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic "hacking" labels.
- Avoid listing offences without matching each to conduct and mens rea.
- Avoid treating online-safety regulatory duties as a strict guarantee that all illegal user content must instantly disappear.
