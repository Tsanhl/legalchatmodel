# Human Rights Law

## Answer Method
- Start with right, jurisdiction, state responsibility/public authority, interference, legality, legitimate aim, necessity/proportionality, remedy and margin/discretion.
- Problems should separate each Convention or human-rights route rather than giving one global fairness answer.
- Essays need a thesis on institutional competence, proportionality, rights protection, democratic legitimacy, or remedial effectiveness.

## Topic Map
- Article 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 11, 14, Protocol rights, derogation, extraterritoriality, proportionality, positive obligations, remedies and declarations.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Human-rights answers should classify the right as absolute, limited or qualified before justification.
- HRA problem answers need public authority/victim status, act/omission, compatibility, interpretation/declaration/remedy and any damages route.
- Qualified-right problems should apply legality, legitimate aim, suitability/rational connection, necessity and fair balance distinctly.
- Positive-obligation and operational-duty claims require threshold, knowledge/risk and reasonable measures, not hindsight.

## For Each Topic Include
- Establish jurisdiction and engagement before justification.
- Separate absolute, limited and qualified rights.
- End with likely breach and remedy.

## Case Brief Bank
- Bank Mellat v HM Treasury: Facts: financial restrictions challenged. Held: structured proportionality analysis required. Reasoning: legitimate aim, rational connection, necessity and fair balance. Answer use: proportionality.
- R (Nicklinson) v Ministry of Justice: Facts: assisted dying and Article 8. Held: courts recognised engagement but showed institutional caution. Reasoning: separation of powers and margin/discretion. Answer use: Article 8 and institutional competence.
- Osman v United Kingdom: Facts: police failure to prevent violence. Held: Article 2 operational duty arises for real and immediate risk known or ought to be known. Reasoning: reasonable measures standard. Answer use: positive obligations.
- Al-Skeini v United Kingdom: Facts: deaths in Iraq. Held: Convention jurisdiction can apply extraterritorially in exceptional circumstances. Reasoning: authority/control can establish jurisdiction. Answer use: extraterritoriality.
- Animal Defenders International v United Kingdom: Facts: political advertising ban. Held: broad ban compatible with Article 10. Reasoning: fair balance and democratic process. Answer use: expression/proportionality.
- R (Ullah) v Special Adjudicator: Facts: domestic Convention interpretation. Held: domestic courts generally keep pace with Strasbourg. Reasoning: no more, no less approach. Answer use: HRA method with later qualifications.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not use Article 8 as a general fairness provision; identify private/family life, home or correspondence and interference.
- Keep engagement, interference and justification in separate paragraphs.
- Where Article 14 is raised, identify status, comparator, ambit and justification.
- Remedies need HRA route: declaration of incompatibility, interpretation, quashing/declaration, damages only where just satisfaction requires.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers identify right, jurisdiction, victim/public authority, engagement, interference, justification intensity and remedy.
- Strong essays evaluate institutional competence and proportionality without losing doctrinal sequence.
- Weaker answers skip jurisdiction/engagement or use proportionality as a conclusion.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Derogation requires a distinct threshold and does not apply to non-derogable rights.
- Extraterritoriality must be established before breach analysis.
- Proportionality needs legitimate aim, suitability/rational connection, necessity and fair balance.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Protest and assembly answers should verify the Public Order Act 2023 alongside Public Order Act 1986, ss 12 and 14, because serious disruption, protest offences and protest conditions may control the statutory context before Articles 10 and 11 proportionality.
- Do not assume a free-standing damages remedy under the HRA. Check Human Rights Act 1998, s 8 and the overlap with judicial review relief.

## Avoid
- Avoid using Article 8 or proportionality as a generic fairness label.
- Avoid skipping jurisdiction or engagement.
