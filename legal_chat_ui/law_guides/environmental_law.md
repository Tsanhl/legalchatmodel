# Environmental Law

## Answer Method
- Start by identifying the legal route: private nuisance, negligence, statutory nuisance, environmental permitting, judicial review, or broad environmental-principles essay.
- Do not present environmental law as one unified moral project. Separate environmental principles, regulatory techniques, enforcement reality, and remedies.
- Problem answers should rank practical routes, because the strongest claim often differs for amenity harm, personal injury, chemical run-off, regulatory breach, and public-law inaction.
- Essays need a thesis on whether environmental law balances protection and development, manages compromise, or under-enforces environmental values.

## Source Modes
- Current-law triggers include environmental permitting rules, regulator guidance, climate legislation, statutory nuisance procedure, and post-Brexit retained/assimilated environmental law. Verify current status before relying on them.
- Use official statute, regulator guidance, and current policy first for live compliance points; use cases and commentary for doctrinal and evaluative propositions.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Environmental nuisance problems should use this sequence: standing/proprietary interest -> private nuisance amenity harm -> negligence for personal injury and causation -> statutory nuisance procedure -> regulatory or judicial-review route -> ranked remedies.
- Permits and substantial compliance are relevant evidence, but not automatically a defence to private nuisance unless the statutory scheme clearly excludes common-law rights.
- Odour, night noise and loss of enjoyment usually fit nuisance or statutory nuisance more naturally than negligence.
- Respiratory symptoms or personal injury require a separate negligence analysis with breach, factual causation, medical evidence, remoteness, and proof of loss.
- Chemical run-off needs evidence of source, pathway, contamination, causation, and whether a regulatory or common-law remedy is realistically stronger.
- Judicial review of regulator inaction is usually harder than nuisance/statutory nuisance because the claimant must identify a public-law defect rather than merely show environmental harm.
- For statutory nuisance, state the practical route: local authority investigation, abatement notice, possible appeal or defence, and resident magistrates' court proceedings if the authority does not act.

## Topic Map
- Environmental principles: sustainable development, precaution, polluter pays, prevention, integration, public participation, and intergenerational equity.
- Regulatory techniques: permitting, standards, impact assessment, monitoring, enforcement notices, remediation, civil sanctions, criminal offences, and regulator discretion.
- Civil claims: private nuisance, negligence, Rylands-type arguments where relevant, causation, injunctions, damages, and expert evidence.
- Public-law routes: standing, illegality, irrationality, procedural fairness, reasons, legitimate expectation, human-rights arguments, and remedies.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the legal route and decision-maker before discussing policy.
- State what evidence proves environmental harm, causation, breach, and remedy.
- Separate legal test from environmental value language; do not let "polluter pays" replace the statutory or common-law route.
- In mixed waste/planning problems, separate planning permission legality, EIA, habitats assessment, permit conditions, nuisance/statutory nuisance, regulatory enforcement and JR time limits.
- End with practical ranking: strongest route, weaker fallback, urgent evidence needed, interim relief and likely remedy.

## Case Brief Bank
- Coventry v Lawrence: Facts: nuisance from motorsport/stadium noise. Held: planning permission does not by itself authorise nuisance. Reasoning: statutory context matters but does not automatically remove private rights. Answer use: permits/planning and private nuisance.
- Fearn v Tate Gallery: Facts: visual intrusion from viewing gallery. Held: nuisance can protect ordinary use and enjoyment of land. Reasoning: substantial interference and locality remain central. Answer use: amenity interference.
- Cambridge Water: Facts: chemical contamination and escape. Held: remoteness/foreseeability limited liability. Reasoning: environmental harm still needs orthodox causation and foreseeability. Answer use: contamination and remoteness.
- Marcic v Thames Water: Facts: sewer flooding and statutory undertaker regulation. Held: statutory scheme may affect private-law routes. Reasoning: courts should not bypass a detailed regulatory framework. Answer use: regulator/statutory scheme interaction.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not discuss sustainable development as a slogan without explaining how precaution, polluter pays, enforcement, and development trade-offs interact.
- Do not merge amenity nuisance with personal injury; respiratory or health harms usually need separate negligence and causation analysis.
- Do not treat permits, licences or regulator satisfaction as conclusive answers to private-law claims.
- Do not present judicial review as a merits appeal against the regulator's environmental judgment.
- Use expert evidence and practical remedy discussion: monitoring, abatement, injunction, damages, enforcement pressure, complaint route, or public-law remedy.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers separate civil, regulatory and public-law routes, then rank them practically.
- Top-band essays test institutional effectiveness and remedial force rather than restating broad environmental values.
- Weaker answers overuse principles, ignore evidence/causation, or treat regulatory compliance as ending the analysis.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Confirm whether the answer is about private harm, regulatory compliance, public-law inaction, or broad policy evaluation.
- Separate nuisance, negligence, statutory nuisance, permitting and judicial review in distinct analytical boxes.
- Check current statutes, regulator guidance and environmental policy where the prompt turns on live regulation.
- End with a clear practical outcome: best claimant route, likely defence, evidence gap, remedy and fallback.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Statutory nuisance and civil routes: Environmental Protection Act 1990, ss 79-82; *Barr v Biffa Waste Services Ltd* [2012] EWCA Civ 312, [2013] QB 455; *Coventry v Lawrence* [2014] UKSC 13, [2014] AC 822; *Fearn v Board of Trustees of the Tate Gallery* [2023] UKSC 4, [2024] AC 1; *Cambridge Water Co v Eastern Counties Leather plc* [1994] 2 AC 264 (HL); *Manchester Ship Canal Co Ltd v United Utilities Water Ltd* [2024] UKSC 22.
- Environmental permitting and enforcement: Environmental Permitting (England and Wales) Regulations 2016, regs 12 and 38; Environmental Permitting (England and Wales) Regulations 2016, Sch 9 definitions of pollution where relevant; Environment Act 2021; Environment Agency odour-management, enforcement and offence-response guidance should be checked where waste-site compliance or criminal/regulatory consequences are live.
- Planning, EIA and habitats: Town and Country Planning Act 1990, s 70; Planning and Compulsory Purchase Act 2004, s 38(6); Town and Country Planning (Environmental Impact Assessment) Regulations 2017, regs 4, 18 and 25 and Sch 4; Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017, regs 9 and 63; *Berkeley v Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions* [2001] 2 AC 603 (HL); *R (Champion) v North Norfolk District Council* [2015] UKSC 52, [2015] 1 WLR 3710; *R (Friends of the Earth Ltd) v Heathrow Airport Ltd* [2020] UKSC 52, [2021] PTSR 190; *R (Finch) v Surrey County Council* [2024] UKSC 20.
- Public participation and principles: Aarhus Convention 1998; Rio Declaration 1992, principles 10, 15 and 16; Environment Act 2021 environmental principles policy statement where current-law/public-law prompts require it.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- For planning EIA, verify the Town and Country Planning (Environmental Impact Assessment) Regulations 2017 and any current government guidance, including transitional or replacement environmental assessment reforms.
- For protected habitats, verify the Conservation of Habitats and Species Regulations 2017 and current appropriate-assessment guidance before advising on permission or permit legality.
- For permits, verify Environmental Permitting (England and Wales) Regulations 2016, Environment Agency current enforcement guidance and whether the issue is permit compliance, statutory nuisance, private nuisance or judicial review.
- For waste facilities, verify current Environment Agency odour and appropriate-measures guidance, because permit conditions often require an approved odour management plan, risk assessment and prevention or minimisation of serious pollution.

## Problem Route: Waste Facility, EIA, Habitats And Nuisance
- Permission route: identify the decision-maker, development plan, material considerations, reasons and whether understated odour/traffic information made the consultation or planning judgment legally defective rather than merely controversial.
- EIA route: ask whether an EIA was required; whether the environmental statement contained the information required by regulation 18 and Schedule 4; whether cumulative emissions and reasonable alternatives were adequately addressed; and whether further information/consultation was required.
- Habitats route: if a protected habitat may be affected, ask whether the authority carried out screening and, where necessary, an appropriate assessment before concluding no adverse effect on site integrity under the Habitats Regulations.
- Permit route: separate permit grant/variation/compliance from planning permission; test odour, dust, noise, monitoring, management-plan and enforcement conditions under the Environmental Permitting Regulations and current Environment Agency guidance.
- Private-law route: run statutory nuisance under Environmental Protection Act 1990, ss 79-82 and private nuisance separately; permits and planning permission are relevant to locality and statutory context but are not automatic defences.
- Remedies: for residents, rank prompt JR/statutory-review/interim relief if in time, regulatory complaint/enforcement pressure, abatement notice or magistrates' court route, private nuisance injunction/damages, and expert evidence for causation.

## Avoid
- Avoid slogan-level environmental principles.
- Avoid assuming environmental harm automatically creates a private-law claim.
- Avoid treating judicial review as a substitute for proving nuisance, negligence or statutory nuisance.
