# Family Law

## Answer Method
- Start with the type of dispute: children, financial remedies, domestic abuse, divorce/nullity, parenthood, or public/private child law.
- Problems need order-specific advice and practical outcomes.
- Essays need a thesis on welfare, autonomy, vulnerability, equality, state intervention, or discretion.
- Keep statutory welfare tests central.

## Topic Map
- Children Act welfare principle, welfare checklist, parental responsibility, child arrangements, prohibited steps, specific issue orders.
- Financial remedies: needs, sharing, compensation, conduct, nuptial agreements.
- Domestic abuse/protective orders; public child protection threshold; parenthood and assisted reproduction where relevant.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Children answers should identify the order sought before applying welfare; do not write a generic welfare essay.
- Financial-remedy answers need asset schedule, needs, sharing, compensation, conduct only if exceptional, and final order.
- Public-child-law answers must separate threshold from welfare/proportionality and remedy.
- Domestic abuse answers should connect protective order, evidence, duration, undertakings, enforcement, and child/contact consequences.

## For Each Topic Include
- Children: jurisdiction/order sought -> welfare paramountcy -> checklist -> facts -> order.
- Finance: assets/resources -> needs -> sharing/compensation -> departure factors -> order.
- Public law: threshold -> welfare -> proportionality -> care/supervision/adoption outcome.

## Case Brief Bank
- J v C: Facts: custody dispute. Held: welfare is paramount. Reasoning: child’s interests override adult claims. Answer use: welfare principle.
- Re G (Children): Facts: same-sex parents and care dispute. Held: welfare, not biological status alone, controls. Reasoning: parenthood factors are relevant but not decisive. Answer use: parental status.
- Payne v Payne / Re F: Facts: relocation disputes. Held: welfare assessment is holistic. Reasoning: no rigid presumption. Answer use: relocation.
- White v White: Facts: financial remedy after long marriage. Held: yardstick of equality. Reasoning: discrimination between breadwinner/homemaker rejected. Answer use: sharing.
- Miller; McFarlane: Facts: financial remedy principles. Held: needs, compensation, sharing. Reasoning: fairness has strands. Answer use: finance structure.
- Radmacher v Granatino: Facts: pre-nuptial agreement. Held: weight given if freely entered and fair. Reasoning: autonomy balanced with fairness. Answer use: nuptial agreements.

## Feedback Rules
- End with specific orders, not abstract welfare language.
- Do not let adult rights displace child welfare.
- In finance, separate needs, sharing, and compensation.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Identify the order sought before the statutory test. Child arrangements, prohibited steps, specific issue, non-molestation, occupation, financial remedy and care/adoption orders need different gateways.
- Children answers must apply welfare paramountcy and the checklist to concrete facts, then give safeguards such as handover, supervision, indirect contact, undertakings, duration and review.
- Public child-law answers must prove threshold separately before welfare and proportionality. Do not assume serious concern automatically justifies care or adoption.
- Financial remedy answers need an asset schedule, resources, needs, sharing, compensation, nuptial-agreement weight, conduct only if exceptional, and a precise final order.
- Domestic abuse answers should connect evidence, protective order, duration, power of arrest/undertakings, enforcement and child/contact consequences.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Children: Children Act 1989, ss 1 and 8; *Re G (Children) (Residence: Same-Sex Partner)* [2006] UKHL 43, [2006] 1 WLR 2305; *Re B (A Child) (Care Proceedings: Threshold Criteria)* [2013] UKSC 33, [2013] 1 WLR 1911.
- Financial remedy: Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, ss 23-25A; *White v White* [2000] UKHL 54, [2001] 1 AC 596; *Miller v Miller; McFarlane v McFarlane* [2006] UKHL 24, [2006] 2 AC 618; *Radmacher v Granatino* [2010] UKSC 42, [2011] 1 AC 534.
- Domestic abuse/protection: Family Law Act 1996, ss 33 and 42; *Yemshaw v Hounslow London Borough Council* [2011] UKSC 3, [2011] 1 WLR 433.
- Jurisdiction/abduction: Child Abduction and Custody Act 1985; *A v A (Children: Habitual Residence)* [2013] UKSC 60, [2014] AC 1.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic fairness without statutory factors.
- Avoid assuming one parent “wins”; give order structure and safeguards.
