# First-Class (70+) Writing Standards — anonymized synthesis of UK law-school feedback

These rules are a fresh, non-identifying synthesis of marking guidance, writing checklists
and differently graded legal work. Raw submissions, distinctive wording, module codes,
institutional identifiers, marks, filenames and author details are deliberately excluded.
Marking criteria: Knowledge, Analysis (incl. problem solving), Communication, Research.

## A. Authority and citation (the biggest mark-loser)
1. OSCOLA by default. Case names italicised in text and footnotes, NOT in the table of cases.
2. Statutes: "Pensions Act 1995, s 34" — no full stop after "s". Regulations: "…Regulations 2005, reg 4".
3. E&W judgment pinpoints in [square brackets]; ECJ as "para 123". Footnotes end with a full stop.
4. Quotes: single quotation marks; quotations over 3 lines are indented without quote marks.
5. Pinpoint every proposition to the exact paragraph (or page where no paragraphs) — but ONLY
   pinpoints actually verified against the source. No pinpoint at all is bad (caps marks below
   the 60s); an INVENTED or unchecked pinpoint is worse (serious loss). Never copy pinpoints
   from another work's footnotes unchecked. In this app: cite name-only unless the ledger
   verifies the pinpoint, and say what needs verification.
6. Authority batting order: UK Supreme Court > Court of Appeal > High Court > (for pensions)
   Pensions Ombudsman. Cite the decision at its highest level (Edge at CA, not HC). Always
   check the decision has not been overturned/superseded; between conflicting same-level
   decisions, pick one and explain why.
7. Pensions Ombudsman citation: Case Name [Case Reference] Pensions Ombudsman Determination
   (Date) — italicise the name only.
8. Bibliography (only when requested or coursework register): Table of cases (not italicised),
   Table of legislation, Other sources — in that order.
9. Citations do not replace argument; no citation strings; avoid citing the same source in
   consecutive footnotes; only cite what was actually used.
10. No academic titles: "Miller argues…", never "Professor Miller of Harvard argues…".
11. Use the latest editions/most recent authority; scan backwards from the current year;
    beware overruled case law.
12. Treat authority verification as a release blocker. A plausible-looking but nonexistent
    case, report citation, neutral citation or pinpoint is a catastrophic reliability error,
    not a cosmetic OSCOLA mistake. If an authority cannot be verified in the indexed ledger
    or a current official source, omit the unsupported detail and identify the point requiring
    verification.

## B. Structure
1. Essays: entry point at the highest level of abstraction, then structure DOWN the tree to
   lower levels; subheadings help the reader follow the argument.
2. Never open with "This essay will…" / "I will now explore…" — write like an academic
   article. Do not repeat or rephrase the question in the text.
3. Advice/problem register: Part I / A. / 1. / 1.1 / (a) numbering (judges', barristers' and
   solicitors' house style). Academic essays: headings yes, numbering only if permitted.
4. Problem questions: roadmap sentence listing every decision; issue-headed sections; exact
   test; application to named facts; conditional branching; counterargument; ranked outcome;
   practical next steps. An explicit numbered Assumptions section for factual gaps earns credit.
5. Essays: qualified thesis first; competing arguments grouped by camp (not "X says, Y
   states…" across opposite camps without positioning); doctrinal limits; balanced conclusion
   that takes a position.
6. SQE-style: single best answer stated first, then concise IRAC justification and why the
   alternatives are wrong; no long headings.
7. General legal enquiries: plain-language direct answer first, then the authority basis,
   practical next steps, and what needs official verification.

## C. Writing mechanics
1. Paragraphs ≤ 6 lines; sentences ≤ 2 lines as a rule of thumb.
2. Defined-term shorthand saves words: Eligible Adult Dependant ("EAD") — bold the defined
   term at first use; never "hereinafter".
3. SPaG errors always lose marks. Consistent formatting throughout.
4. Words defined in legislation do not automatically carry that meaning in deeds/contracts —
   meaning is fact- and context-sensitive; a party's rejected argument is not the holding.

## D. Analysis (what separates 70+ from 60s)
1. Anchor every conclusion to a concrete fact from the question.
2. Spot missing/ambiguous facts and show how each gap changes the analysis.
3. Where law is unsettled, present the competing tests with their sources, then take a
   reasoned position; note when a range of answers is valid.
4. Characterise the request/facts against the legal test BEFORE applying the test.
5. Engage precisely with quoted wording (e.g. 'risk of significant detriment' vs 'significant
   risk of some detriment' are different limbs).
6. Calibrated conclusions ('likely', 'strong argument', 'court-dependent'); no absolutes
   unless binding authority compels them.
7. Research effort shows: current data, recent decisions, official reports — beyond the
   handout list.
8. Stay on the exact evaluative proposition from the introduction onward. A descriptive
   account of doctrine, scheme types or cases does not become analysis merely because it is
   accurate; every section must show how its material proves, qualifies or defeats the thesis.
9. Develop fewer points in greater depth. Merge thin sections; use topic sentences and
   transitions to show how each part advances the central argument. Sustained essay prose is
   the default; lists belong in advice/briefing formats or genuinely functional roadmaps.
10. Engage with the strongest contrary authority or academic interpretation before taking a
    position. Do not omit a central case, dissent, statutory limb or course debate that goes to
    the heart of the question.
11. In problem questions, spend words according to outcome sensitivity. Cover material
    alternatives, but do not devote a long section to a route that is obviously unlikely while
    omitting the best claim, decisive statutory rule or fact-specific defence.
12. Support empirical, policy, economic and technical claims as carefully as black-letter
    propositions. Explain why the source matters rather than dropping it into a citation string.

## E. Register and depth controls distilled from the complete marked-work set
1. A 70+ problem answer combines accurate issue spotting with precise application. State the
   best route first, distinguish close alternatives, and correct any result-changing statutory
   misconception before polishing style.
2. A 70+ essay is not one-sided. Counterarguments, minority/dissenting reasoning and the
   rationale behind the doctrine are part of the main analysis, not a late paragraph added for
   balance.
3. Broad or comparative work must use each comparison for a defined analytical function.
   Avoid moving rapidly across many regimes without demonstrating their connection to the
   thesis.
4. OSCOLA References must contain only authorities actually used in the answer. Inline
   authority belongs immediately after the proposition it supports; never invent a neutral
   citation or pinpoint.
5. Marker feedback and student scripts are writing evidence, not legal authority. Legal rules
   must be verified against the indexed authority ledger or current official sources.
