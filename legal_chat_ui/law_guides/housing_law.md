# Housing Law

## Answer Method
- Start with status: tenant, licensee, secure/assured tenancy, homelessness applicant, occupier, landlord, local authority or mortgagee.
- Problems should separate possession route, notice validity, deposit/repair issues, unlawful eviction, homelessness duties, equality/human-rights points and remedies.
- End with the practical housing outcome: possession likely, adjournment, defence, injunction, damages, review, or appeal.

## Topic Map
- Tenancy status, possession, notices, rent arrears, repairs, deposits, unlawful eviction, homelessness, allocation, anti-social behaviour, public-law defences and human rights.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Housing problems should start with status and statutory route: assured, assured shorthold, secure, licence, excluded occupier, homelessness applicant or public-law claimant.
- Possession answers need notice validity, ground, mandatory/discretionary status, proportionality/public-law defence, suspension/adjournment and costs.
- Disrepair and deposit issues should be separated from possession unless they affect defence, counterclaim or set-off.
- Homelessness answers require eligibility, homelessness, priority need, intentionality, local connection, review and appeal sequence.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the tenancy/status and statutory route first.
- Check notice, ground, discretion, proportionality and procedural fairness.
- Separate private landlord claims from local-authority/public-law duties.

## Case Brief Bank
- Manchester City Council v Pinnock: Facts: possession by public authority. Held: Article 8 proportionality defence may be raised. Reasoning: court must assess proportionality where properly raised. Answer use: public-sector possession.
- Hounslow LBC v Powell: Facts: Article 8 possession cases. Held: proportionality review is available but usually concise. Reasoning: property management interests carry weight. Answer use: Article 8 limits.
- Akerman-Livingstone v Aster Communities: Facts: disability discrimination defence to possession. Held: Equality Act defence may require structured discrimination analysis. Reasoning: not the same as Article 8 summary proportionality. Answer use: equality defence.
- Street v Mountford: Facts: licence labelled agreement gave exclusive possession. Held: substance, not label, determines tenancy. Reasoning: exclusive possession for term at rent usually tenancy. Answer use: status.
- Birmingham CC v Ali: Facts: homelessness accommodation and duties. Held: statutory homelessness sequence controls obligations. Reasoning: local authority duty depends on statutory criteria. Answer use: homelessness route.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not advise on possession without identifying tenancy status and notice route.
- Keep Article 8, Equality Act and statutory discretion separate.
- If homelessness is raised, follow the statutory decision/review/appeal path rather than generic public-law fairness.
- Include practical outcome: possession order, suspension, adjournment, injunction, review, appeal or damages.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers identify status, notice, ground, discretion, proportionality/equality/public-law defence, counterclaim and order.
- Strong homelessness answers state each statutory criterion and procedural next step.
- Weaker answers assume all occupiers have the same security or omit notice validity.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current notice periods, homelessness guidance and housing procedure can change; verify live law where timing matters.
- Do not treat occupation as ownership; status controls route and remedy.
- For public bodies, consider proportionality and equality/public-law issues separately from the possession ground.

- Public-sector possession and equality: *Manchester City Council v Pinnock* [2010] UKSC 45, [2011] 2 AC 104; *Hounslow London Borough Council v Powell* [2011] UKSC 8, [2011] 2 AC 186; *Akerman-Livingstone v Aster Communities Ltd* [2015] UKSC 15, [2015] AC 1399.
- Homelessness/repairs/deposits: Housing Act 1996, Pt VII; Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, ss 11 and 9A; Housing Act 2004, ss 213-215.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Renters' Rights Act 2025 received Royal Assent and the government roadmap states the first phase for the private rented sector comes into force on 1 May 2026. Verify commencement, transitional rules and whether the tenancy is PRS/social/lodger before applying section 21 or assured-periodic tenancy analysis.
- From Phase 1 the roadmap identifies abolition of section 21 no-fault eviction for PRS, introduction of assured periodic tenancies, possession-ground reform, rent-increase controls, rental-bidding limits, anti-discrimination measures, pet-request duties and stronger enforcement/rent repayment orders. Treat timing and tenancy type as decisive.

## Avoid
- Avoid assuming every occupier has the same security.
- Avoid giving possession advice without notice, ground, discretion and remedy.
