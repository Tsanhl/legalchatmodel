# Competition Law

## Answer Method
- Start with the legal route: Chapter I / Article 101, Chapter II / Article 102, merger control, market investigation, or digital markets regulation.
- Define the market only as far as needed; do not spend the answer on market definition if dominance/agreement is obvious.
- Essays need a thesis about error-cost, consumer welfare, market integration, administrability, or digital-market power.
- Essay answers should state which goal of competition law is doing the work: consumer welfare, effective competition, fairness, market integration, innovation, administrability, or pluralist public-interest control.
- Problems must end with infringement risk, exemption/objective-justification prospects, enforcement route, and likely remedy/penalty.

## Topic Map
- Market definition and market power; agreements, concerted practices, object/effect restrictions, exemption.
- Dominance and abuse: exclusionary abuse, exploitative abuse, refusal to supply, tying, self-preferencing, margin squeeze, excessive pricing.
- Digital competition: data advantage, platforms, ecosystems, DMA-style ex ante duties, interoperability, privacy/data overlap.
- Private enforcement and damages; merger control; CMA/EU institutional enforcement.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Digital/platform questions should not become policy essays: identify the abuse theory, data or ranking mechanism, foreclosure/exploitation evidence, and administrability problem.
- Article 102 answers should keep dominance, abuse, effects, counterfactual evidence, and objective justification in separate paragraphs.
- Article 102 problem answers should use this spine: undertaking -> market definition -> dominance -> abuse theory -> foreclosure/exploitation evidence -> effect on trade/competition -> objective justification/efficiencies -> enforcement/remedy.
- UK-only dominance problems should expressly take the Chapter II / section 18 Competition Act 1998 route before borrowing EU Article 102 reasoning by analogy. Do not silently switch to EU law if the facts and enforcement setting are domestic.
- Smart-device or voice-assistant problems should begin with the undertaking and market-power source, not with the branded product as if the product itself were the legal person. Treat the product/ecosystem as the channel through which the undertaking may hold or leverage dominance.
- In voice-assistant / smart-home facts, the strongest route is often tying or technological tying through compulsory pre-installation, defaults, firmware, system permissions, API limits, interoperability limits, or practical non-removability. Self-preferencing is weaker unless the facts show ranking, demotion, unequal display, privileged access, or a comparable non-equivalent advantage.
- Essays need a thesis on whether consumer-welfare tools can handle privacy, data concentration, ecosystems, or ex ante regulation.
- Contemporary-issues questions should connect doctrine to recent scholarship or enforcement practice, but the legal test still has to control the answer.
- Use US/comparative antitrust only as a labelled comparator; do not let it replace EU/UK doctrine.
- If an essay says Article 102 is "effects-based", define the term. Distinguish actual effects, likely effects, capability to foreclose, presumptions of harm, as-efficient-competitor analysis, consumer welfare, and protection of the competitive process.
- Digital-platform analysis needs economic vocabulary tied to facts: network effects, data-driven entry barriers, economies of scale/scope, defaults, multi-sided markets, traffic diversion, foreclosure of efficient rivals, and non-price harms such as quality, innovation, privacy and choice.
- Smart-device, app-store, marketplace and cloud-platform facts should test defaults/pre-installation, tying or technical integration, data access or retention, unfair trading conditions, and non-price quality/privacy harm before reaching for self-preferencing.
- Data/privacy allegations can support competition analysis, but keep the regimes distinct: use the data practice as evidence of exploitation, quality degradation, lock-in, entry barriers or unfair terms, not as a free-standing GDPR answer.
- Criminal misuse, cybersecurity incidents, or third-party hacking do not by themselves establish an abuse. Connect the incident to dominance-linked terms, inadequate quality/security as a non-price degradation, exploitative risk allocation, or objective-justification evidence.
- Proposed security-driven price increases or discounts should be handled cautiously: check whether the price has been imposed, whether cost/margin/comparator evidence exists, and whether the price point is better treated as justification, exploitative unfairness, excessive pricing, or merely contextual evidence.
- Current-law answers must verify the Commission's Article 102 exclusionary-abuse Guidelines process. Do not present the 2009 Guidance as the final modern framework where a current/digital question requires the draft/forthcoming Guidelines context.

## For Each Topic Include
- Article 101/Chapter I: agreement/undertaking, object/effect, appreciability, exemption under Article 101(3).
- Article 102/Chapter II: undertaking, dominance, abuse, effect on trade/competition, objective justification/efficiencies.
- Digital cases need mechanism: how data, default status, ranking, tying, or access terms foreclose rivals.
- Always separate legal test from economic evidence.
- Self-preferencing: not per se abusive. Identify the precise market context, traffic dependence, unequal ranking/display or access condition, potential foreclosure, causal link, and remedy.
- Refusal to supply/access: decide whether Bronner/essential facilities truly applies. Do not apply Bronner merely because a platform design or ranking case affects access.
- DMA overlap: label the DMA as ex ante gatekeeper regulation and Article 102 as ex post enforcement. Explain overlap and why one does not simply replace the other.

## Case Brief Bank
- United Brands: Facts: banana pricing and market-power dispute. Held: dominance can be inferred from economic strength. Reasoning: market position, barriers, and independence matter. Answer use: dominance and market definition.
- Hoffmann-La Roche: Facts: loyalty rebates by dominant undertaking. Held: loyalty-inducing rebates can abuse dominance. Reasoning: foreclosure and dependency distort competition. Answer use: rebates and exclusion.
- Intel: Facts: exclusivity rebates and economic effects. Held: effects analysis may be required when evidence contests foreclosure. Reasoning: form and capability matter. Answer use: modern rebate analysis.
- Google Shopping: Facts: preferential treatment of comparison-shopping service. Held: self-preferencing can be abusive on specific facts. Reasoning: leveraging and traffic effects matter. Answer use: platform self-preferencing.
- Meta Platforms v Bundeskartellamt: Facts: data processing and competition enforcement overlap. Held: data-protection compliance can inform competition assessment. Reasoning: exploitative terms may reflect dominance. Answer use: data/competition intersection.
- Sainsbury's v Visa/Mastercard: Facts: interchange fees. Held: restriction and exemption analysis require structured economic proof. Reasoning: counterfactual and pass-on matter. Answer use: payment systems and damages.

## Feedback Rules
- In UK domestic advice, write "section 18 / Chapter II" first and then use EU Article 102 case law as persuasive/interpretive support. Do not make the answer look like EU enforcement unless the prompt asks for EU law.
- Reduce dominance and market-definition preliminaries when the problem gives a high market share and the exam asks mainly about abuse categories; spend the word budget on tying, technological tying, exclusivity, exploitative data terms, objective justification, and remedy.
- Do not treat objective justification as a label; identify aim, evidence, proportionality, and less restrictive alternatives.
- Do not over-plead self-preferencing or discrimination without explaining the exact foreclosure mechanism: ranking, default placement, interoperability, API/access terms, demotion, tying, data access, or comparable unequal treatment.
- Use economic concepts only where they change the legal outcome.
- If a core point has already appeared, keep it once where it does real analytical work, usually as the topic sentence for the abuse/market/evidence paragraph.
- Self-preferencing must be tied to a concrete theory of harm; if the facts instead fit tying, defaults, refusal to supply, exploitative terms, or unequal access, use the stronger abuse route.
- Excessive-pricing or discriminatory-pricing analysis needs cost, margin, comparator, equivalent-transaction, and competitive-disadvantage evidence. If those facts are thin, treat pricing as contextual support rather than a standalone abuse.
- Market definition must serve the abuse theory. Explain why the chosen market boundary changes dominance, foreclosure, exploitation, or remedy.
- Every competition-law conclusion needs a short evidence verdict: what fact proves the route, what fact is missing, and how that affects enforcement risk.
- For default-placement or compulsory-preinstallation facts, ask whether the dominant undertaking is leveraging power through tying, technical integration or default architecture. Self-preferencing is weaker unless there is a ranking, demotion, display or access-discrimination mechanism.
- Broad data-use, voice-recording, retention or one-sided access terms may be framed as exploitative abuse under section 18(2)(a)/Article 102(a) where dominance removes meaningful user choice. Explain the non-price harm and distinguish it from a data-protection breach.
- A cybersecurity incident or privacy scandal is usually evidence of consumer harm, quality degradation, lock-in or weak competitive constraint, not automatically an abuse without a dominance-linked trading condition or exclusionary mechanism.
- Proposed price increases, excessive pricing and discriminatory pricing are evidence-intensive. State whether the price has actually been imposed and whether cost, margin, comparator, profitability or economic-value evidence is available.
- Google Shopping analysis should state that the abuse depended on leveraging dominance in general search into comparison shopping through unequal ranking/display and potential foreclosure effects. It should also explain why the Court did not reduce the case to a Bronner refusal-to-supply problem.
- The Bronner distinction should be central in platform cases: true compulsory access/refusal cases are different from non-price platform architecture, self-preferencing, ranking, and discrimination cases.
- Take the interventionism critique seriously: ask whether the rule protects competition or competitors, whether "competition on the merits" gives enough legal certainty, and whether Article 102 is being used as indirect platform regulation.
- End Article 102 essays with a position on the correct balance between administrability, economic effects, legal certainty, error costs, and digital-market contestability.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Define the theory of harm before economics. Dominance, abuse, effects, objective justification and remedy must be distinct.
- Article 102 essays using "effects-based" must define actual effects, likely effects, capability, presumptions, AEC analysis, consumer welfare and competitive-process protection.
- Platform answers need the economic mechanism: network effects, data, defaults, ranking, interoperability, switching costs, traffic diversion, scale/scope and foreclosure of efficient rivals.
- Google Shopping/Bronner discipline is essential: self-preferencing is not per se unlawful and Bronner indispensability is not automatically the test for non-refusal platform design.
- Smart-device answers should classify compulsory pre-installation before self-preferencing: contractual tying, technological tying, exclusivity/default foreclosure, exploitative data terms, unfair risk allocation, and pricing must be separately tested.
- Objective-justification analysis must deal with security/compliance arguments at evidence level: legitimate aim, necessity, whether the term or design actually addresses the risk, proportionality, less restrictive alternatives, and whether efficiencies are passed to consumers.
- Current-law answers should flag the DMA as ex ante gatekeeper regulation and verify the Article 102 Guidelines process before treating the 2009 Guidance as final.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Resale price maintenance ordinarily concerns a supplier requiring a retailer not to resell below a fixed or minimum price. A genuinely non-binding recommended price is not itself RPM; pressure, incentives, monitoring or agreement may change the analysis.
- Chapter I remedies are not US antitrust remedies. A private claimant seeks compensatory damages or other available relief; never award treble damages.
- Distinguish CMA public enforcement (directions and financial penalties, subject to statute) from private standalone/follow-on claims in the CAT or courts. The CMA does not ordinarily award a retailer its private damages.
- Section 47A provides a CAT route; section 58A addresses the binding effect of final infringement decisions. Plead infringement, causation and loss and address pass-on where relevant.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic “big tech bad” analysis.
- Avoid conflating Article 101 collective conduct with Article 102 unilateral conduct.
- Avoid using "effects-based" as a vague synonym for modern or sensible.
- Avoid treating the Digital Markets Act as if it has displaced Article 102.

## Anonymized Output Architecture
- For a platform-abuse problem, give the enforcement-risk view first, then address undertaking and jurisdiction, only the market definition needed for power, dominance, the strongest abuse theory, the mechanism of harm, objective justification and proportionate remedy.
- Keep dominance, abuse and effects separate. Privacy, security and innovation support justification only where evidence shows necessity, proportionality and no less restrictive design.
