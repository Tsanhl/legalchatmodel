# Construction Law

## Answer Method
- Start with contract form, scope, time machinery, payment route, defects, variations, notices, adjudication or dispute-resolution route.
- Problems should separate delay, extension of time, liquidated damages, defects, payment, termination, adjudication and remedies.
- End with practical project outcome: entitlement, notice risk, adjudication prospects, damages, rectification, or settlement leverage.

## Topic Map
- JCT/NEC-style contracts, delay, extensions of time, liquidated damages, variations, defects, payment notices, adjudication, professional negligence, limitation and dispute resolution.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Construction answers should be contract-machinery first: identify the relevant clause, notice requirement, valuation method and dispute route before using general breach language.
- Delay answers need critical path, causation, concurrency, extension of time, loss and expense, liquidated damages and prevention principle issues separated.
- Payment answers should use the statutory payment-notice/pay-less-notice route and the adjudication consequence where relevant.
- Defects answers need specification, workmanship/design responsibility, causation, cost of cure, diminution and reasonableness.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the contract clause/rule before fairness.
- Check notice, causation, concurrency, mitigation, valuation and remedy.
- Separate employer, contractor, consultant and subcontractor positions.

## Case Brief Bank
- Housing Grants, Construction and Regeneration Act 1996, s 108: a party to a qualifying construction contract may refer a dispute to adjudication at any time; the compliant procedure must secure appointment/referral within seven days, a decision within 28 days subject to permitted extension, and temporary binding effect.
- Scheme for Construction Contracts (England and Wales) Regulations 1998, SI 1998/649, sch 1 paras 1-23: default adjudication procedure where the contract does not comply with s 108.
- Macob Civil Engineering Ltd v Morrison Construction Ltd [1999] EWHC Technology 254, [1999] BLR 93: adjudication decisions are intended to be enforced promptly despite errors, subject to narrow jurisdiction/natural-justice controls.
- Carillion Construction Ltd v Devonport Royal Dockyard Ltd [2005] EWCA Civ 1358, [2006] BLR 15: courts should respect the intended temporary finality and enforce adjudicators' decisions, with challenges kept to narrow grounds.
- Bresco Electrical Services Ltd v Michael J Lonsdale (Electrical) Ltd [2020] UKSC 25, [2020] 1 WLR 2434: adjudication remains a distinct statutory dispute-resolution right; insolvency does not remove jurisdiction, although enforcement consequences require separate attention.
- Henry Boot Construction v Malmaison Hotel: Facts: concurrent delay under building contract. Held: contractor may receive extension where a relevant event causes delay despite contractor delay. Reasoning: contractual allocation of time risk. Answer use: concurrency/EOT.
- Multiplex v Honeywell: Facts: delay and prevention principle. Held: employer prevention may affect liquidated damages unless contract machinery grants time. Reasoning: employer cannot insist on completion date it prevented. Answer use: prevention principle.
- Grove Developments v S&T: Facts: payment notices and adjudication. Held: employer may pursue true valuation after paying notified sum. Reasoning: payment regime and valuation rights are distinct. Answer use: smash-and-grab adjudication.
- Cavendish Square Holding v Makdessi: Facts: penalty doctrine reform. Held: legitimate interest and proportionality guide penalties. Reasoning: clause may protect performance interest beyond pre-estimate. Answer use: liquidated damages.
- Ruxley Electronics v Forsyth: Facts: defective swimming-pool depth. Held: unreasonable cost of cure not recoverable; loss of amenity awarded. Reasoning: damages must be proportionate. Answer use: defects/remedial cost.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not analyse delay without identifying the critical path and contractual notice machinery.
- Keep adjudication's temporarily binding result separate from final valuation or litigation/arbitration outcome.
- Explain whose design or workmanship risk the defect falls under.
- For liquidated damages, analyse enforceability and entitlement separately.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers identify the contract form/clause, notice compliance, causation, valuation, adjudication route and practical remedy.
- Strong problem answers give party-specific advice for employer, contractor, subcontractor and consultant where relevant.
- Weaker answers use generic breach/damages analysis without time, payment and notice machinery.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current adjudication/payment rules and contract wording control the answer.
- Delay analysis needs critical path, causation, notice and contractual machinery.
- Defect claims need breach, causation, remedial cost, diminution and reasonableness.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic breach analysis without contract machinery.
- Avoid giving delay conclusions without causation and notices.
