# Succession, Wills and Administration of Estates

## Answer Method
- Start with the exact route: will validity, construction, revocation, intestacy, estate administration, personal representatives, or Inheritance Act 1975 family provision.
- Validity problems need a strict probate sequence: formal validity/due execution -> testamentary capacity -> knowledge and approval -> undue influence or fraud -> effect of invalidity -> any Inheritance Act 1975 claim.
- Keep probate validity separate from fairness. A disappointing disposition is not invalid merely because it appears harsh.
- End with the estate consequence: later will valid, earlier will revived/operative, intestacy, partial invalidity, personal representative duty, or family provision remedy.

## Source Modes
- Current-law triggers include procedural probate rules, Inheritance Act 1975 thresholds, intestacy figures, tax/estate administration rules, and professional-practice guidance. Verify current status before relying on them.
- Use statute and leading probate authority for tests; use practice material for administration steps and evidence.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Formal validity should address Wills Act 1837 requirements before moving to capacity or suspicious circumstances.
- Testamentary capacity should use Banks v Goodfellow as the core route and tie capacity to the time of execution.
- Lucid-interval facts matter: good-and-bad-day medical evidence does not automatically remove capacity, but it raises proof and timing issues.
- Knowledge and approval is central where suspicious circumstances surround a carer, beneficiary, interpreter, drafter, major gift, isolation, or departure from an earlier will.
- Suspicious circumstances can shift or intensify the propounder's evidential burden on knowledge and approval; they do not automatically prove undue influence.
- Probate undue influence is a demanding route. Opportunity, dependence and benefit are not enough without coercion, pressure, isolation, threats, or fraudulent calumny.
- The golden rule is evidential best practice for elderly or seriously ill testators, not a standalone validity requirement.
- Inheritance Act 1975 analysis comes after validity unless the question is purely family provision. Identify applicant category and whether the standard is maintenance-only before discussing fairness.

## Topic Map
- Will formalities: writing, signature, intention, attestation, witnesses, and effect of non-compliance.
- Capacity and intention: Banks v Goodfellow capacity, delusions, lucid intervals, medical evidence, solicitor evidence, and timing.
- Knowledge and approval: suspicious circumstances, preparation of the will, reading/understanding, major departures, and evidential burden.
- Undue influence/fraud: coercion, fraudulent calumny, pressure, isolation, proof difficulty, and remedy.
- Estate outcomes: invalidity consequence, earlier will, intestacy, personal representatives, grants, administration duties, and claims against the estate.
- Family provision: applicant category, reasonable financial provision, maintenance standard, estate size, needs/resources, obligations, disability, conduct, and remedy.

## For Each Topic Include
- State who bears the burden and whether it shifts or intensifies on the facts.
- Tie each legal route to specific evidence: medical notes, solicitor attendance notes, witnesses, instructions, dependency, isolation, benefit, and prior testamentary pattern.
- Keep capacity, knowledge and approval, and undue influence analytically separate.
- State the practical probate consequence clearly.

## Case Brief Bank
- Banks v Goodfellow: Facts: will challenged for mental disorder. Held: capacity requires understanding the act/effect, property, claims, and absence of disorder poisoning the disposition. Reasoning: capacity is decision-specific. Answer use: core testamentary capacity test.
- Key v Key: Facts: elderly testator made will after bereavement. Held: capacity failed on the evidence. Reasoning: emotional vulnerability and timing can undermine capacity where understanding is impaired. Answer use: fragile testators and capacity evidence.
- Gill v Woodall: Facts: major departure and suspicious circumstances. Held: knowledge and approval needed careful proof. Reasoning: execution alone may not suffice where circumstances are suspicious. Answer use: knowledge and approval.
- Re Edwards: Facts: allegation of pressure/fraudulent conduct in will-making. Held: probate undue influence requires proof of coercion. Reasoning: persuasion or opportunity is insufficient. Answer use: undue influence threshold.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not import inter vivos undue-influence doctrine wholesale into wills disputes without recognising the stricter probate approach.
- Do not treat capacity, knowledge and approval, suspicious circumstances and undue influence as interchangeable routes.
- Do not treat the golden rule as a validity requirement.
- Do not jump to an Inheritance Act 1975 fairness discussion before deciding whether the will is valid.
- For adult-child or carer claims under the 1975 Act, focus on statutory eligibility, maintenance, financial need and estate resources rather than disappointment alone.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers use the probate sequence, state burdens expressly, and give a clear estate consequence.
- Top-band answers handle suspicious circumstances as knowledge-and-approval evidence first and undue influence only where coercion evidence supports it.
- Weaker answers merge validity routes into a general fairness discussion or omit what happens if the challenged will fails.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Confirm formal validity first, then capacity, knowledge and approval, undue influence/fraud, invalidity consequence, and any 1975 Act claim.
- Check who propounds the will and what evidence supports execution, capacity, instructions and understanding.
- Treat lucid intervals and medical evidence as timing questions, not generic incapacity labels.
- End with the likely operative testamentary instrument or intestacy outcome and any separate family-provision risk.

## Avoid
- Avoid broad moral unfairness analysis without a probate route.
- Avoid saying suspicious circumstances prove undue influence without evidence of coercion.
- Avoid leaving the estate distribution consequence unstated.
