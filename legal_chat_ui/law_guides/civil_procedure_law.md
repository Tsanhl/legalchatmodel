# Civil Procedure and Access to Justice

## Answer Method
- Start with the procedural route: claim form/service, limitation, jurisdiction, interim relief, disclosure, costs, sanctions, ADR, appeal, or access-to-justice challenge.
- Problem answers should separate procedural validity, case-management discretion, prejudice, remedy and costs.
- Essays need a thesis on whether procedure promotes fair resolution, efficiency, equality of arms, settlement, or constitutional access to court.

## Topic Map
- Overriding objective, service, limitation, statements of case, disclosure, evidence, interim injunctions, summary judgment, strike out, sanctions, costs, ADR, appeals, and access to justice.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Procedural problem answers should identify the current stage of litigation before applying rules; pre-issue, service, allocation, interim relief, trial and appeal questions have different consequences.
- Relief from sanctions answers need the Denton sequence and must separate breach seriousness from all-the-circumstances discretion.
- Interim injunction answers need the American Cyanamid route, cross-undertaking and practical balance of convenience.
- Access-to-justice essays should connect cost, delay, legal aid and court fees to constitutional principle, not only administrative efficiency.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the procedural rule and stage first.
- Apply discretion factors: seriousness, prejudice, promptness, proportionality, alternatives and costs.
- End with the order the court is likely to make.

## Case Brief Bank
- American Cyanamid v Ethicon: Facts: interim injunction sought in patent dispute. Held: serious issue, adequacy of damages and balance of convenience guide relief. Reasoning: court avoids mini-trials at interim stage. Answer use: interim injunctions.
- Denton v TH White: Facts: relief from sanctions appeals. Held: apply seriousness/significance, reason for default, and all circumstances. Reasoning: disciplined case management and justice. Answer use: sanctions.
- Mitchell v News Group Newspapers: Facts: costs-budget default. Held: strict compliance culture after Jackson reforms. Reasoning: efficiency and proportionality. Answer use: sanctions background, now read with Denton.
- Halsey v Milton Keynes General NHS Trust: Facts: costs consequences of refusing ADR. Held: unreasonable refusal of ADR may affect costs. Reasoning: parties cannot be compelled in a way that denies access to court, but costs encourage ADR. Answer use: ADR and costs.
- R (UNISON) v Lord Chancellor: Facts: employment tribunal fees restricted claims. Held: fees unlawful. Reasoning: effective access to court is constitutional. Answer use: access to justice.

## Feedback Rules
- Name the procedural rule or doctrine before saying the court has discretion.
- Distinguish strike out, summary judgment, unless orders, relief from sanctions and wasted costs; they are not interchangeable.
- Costs consequences should follow the procedural outcome and party conduct.
- Where a limitation or service point is fatal, say so before spending space on merits.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band civil procedure answers state the stage, governing rule, discretion factors, prejudice, likely order and costs.
- Strong access-to-justice essays balance procedural efficiency against effective court access and equality of arms.
- Weaker answers say "unfair" or "proportionate" without rule, threshold, discretion and remedy.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Keep merits and procedure separate.
- For access-to-justice essays, connect cost, delay, legal aid, representation and effective remedy to the constitutional principle.
- For sanctions, ask what rule was breached, why, how serious it is, whether relief is prompt, and what order is proportionate.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Strike out under CPR r 3.4 and summary judgment under current CPR r 24.3 are distinct. Following the October 2023 Part 24 rewrite, r 24.2 concerns the types of proceedings in which summary judgment is available; r 24.3 contains the no-real-prospect/no-other-compelling-reason grounds.
- Do not present Halsey as establishing an absolute current prohibition on compelling non-court dispute resolution. Read it with Churchill and address proportionality and effective access to court.
- Identify the application route, evidence, opportunity to respond, order sought and costs; do not promise either disposal order merely because the pleaded case appears weak.

## Avoid
- Avoid treating procedure as technical background only.
- Avoid asserting unfairness without naming the procedural rule, discretion and remedy.
