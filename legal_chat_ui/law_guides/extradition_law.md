# Extradition Law

## Answer Method
- Start with the statutory route and requesting state category before human-rights objections.
- Problems should separate extradition offence, bars, forum, passage of time, prison conditions, fair-trial risk, Article 8, assurances and appeal/remedy.
- End with whether extradition is likely to be ordered, barred, adjourned for assurances, or appealed.

## Topic Map
- Extradition Act route, accusation/conviction cases, dual criminality, bars, forum, human rights, prison conditions, fair trial, family life, specialty, assurances and appeal.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Extradition answers should begin with Part 1/Part 2 route, accusation/conviction status, judge/Secretary of State role and statutory sequence.
- Human-rights objections should be article-specific: Article 3 prison/ill-treatment, Article 6 flagrant denial, Article 8 family/private life and specialty/assurances.
- Forum, passage of time, double jeopardy, extraneous considerations and abuse of process must be treated as distinct bars where raised.
- Country evidence and diplomatic assurances are fact-sensitive and current; avoid generic conclusions.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify the route and decision-maker.
- Keep Article 3 prison-conditions evidence separate from Article 6 fair-trial risk and Article 8 family/private life.
- State evidential gaps and likely procedural order.

## Case Brief Bank
- Norris v Government of the United States: Facts: extradition and Article 8/fairness objections. Held: extradition has strong public interest; Article 8 bars are exceptional. Reasoning: international obligations carry weight. Answer use: Article 8 balance.
- HH v Deputy Prosecutor of the Italian Republic: Facts: parents' extradition and children. Held: best interests of children are primary but not automatically determinative. Reasoning: proportionality is fact-sensitive. Answer use: family life.
- Soering v United Kingdom: Facts: extradition to death-row conditions. Held: extradition may breach Article 3 where real risk of inhuman treatment. Reasoning: Convention can prevent removal. Answer use: Article 3.
- Othman (Abu Qatada) v United Kingdom: Facts: deportation/extradition-style fair-trial risk. Held: Article 6 prevents removal only for flagrant denial of justice. Reasoning: high threshold. Answer use: fair trial.
- Love v Government of the United States: Facts: forum bar and health/autism evidence. Held: extradition barred on forum/public-interest analysis. Reasoning: domestic prosecution and personal factors may matter. Answer use: forum bar and health.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not begin with broad fairness; follow the statutory extradition sequence first.
- Keep assurances, prison evidence and fair-trial evidence separate.
- For Article 8, identify the affected family members and explain why the case is or is not exceptional.
- State likely order: extradition, discharge, adjournment for assurances, appeal, or interim relief.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers state route, offence/dual criminality, bars, human rights, assurances, appeal route and likely order.
- Strong answers use current country evidence cautiously and identify evidential gaps.
- Weaker answers merge all objections into generic Article 8/fairness language.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current country evidence and assurances must be verified.
- Do not treat human-rights objections as generic fairness; apply each article separately.
- If prison conditions are raised, analyse real risk and adequacy of assurances.

## Avoid
- Avoid starting with Article 8 before the extradition route.
- Avoid assuming poor conditions automatically bar extradition.
