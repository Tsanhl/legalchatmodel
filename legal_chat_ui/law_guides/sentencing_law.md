# Sentencing Law

## Answer Method
- Start with offence, statutory maximum/minimum, guideline category, culpability, harm, aggravation, mitigation, guilty plea and ancillary orders.
- Appeal problems should identify whether the sentence is wrong in principle or manifestly excessive/unduly lenient.
- Keep conviction liability and sentencing discretion separate.

## Topic Map
- Sentencing purposes, offence guidelines, culpability, harm, aggravating/mitigating factors, dangerousness, custody threshold, community orders, fines, compensation, ancillary orders and appeals.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Sentencing answers should apply the guideline sequence visibly: offence category, culpability/harm, starting point, range, aggravation, mitigation, plea, totality and ancillary orders.
- Appeal answers must state the appellate threshold before re-arguing mitigation.
- Dangerousness, mandatory minimums, custodial threshold and community-order suitability should be kept distinct.
- Where current guideline ranges matter, verify Sentencing Council guidance rather than inventing figures.

## For Each Topic Include
- Apply the guideline sequence visibly.
- State the likely range and why movement within the range is justified.
- End with likely sentence or appeal outcome.

## Case Brief Bank
- R v Goodyear: Facts: indication of likely sentence. Held: judge may give sentence indication under controlled procedure. Reasoning: protects fairness and plea decisions. Answer use: plea/sentence indication.
- R v Caley: Facts: custody threshold and suspended sentences. Held: custody threshold and suspension require separate reasoning. Reasoning: custodial term not automatically immediate. Answer use: suspension.
- R v Sargeant: Facts: manslaughter sentencing factors. Held: culpability and harm require structured assessment. Reasoning: sentencing must match blame and consequence. Answer use: guideline method.
- R v Petherick: Facts: deportation/family impact in sentence. Held: family impact may mitigate but punishment/public interest remain. Reasoning: proportionality and mercy. Answer use: personal mitigation.
- Attorney General's Reference cases: Use for unduly lenient sentence threshold and appellate correction. Answer use: prosecution appeal.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not mix liability defences with mitigation.
- Identify statutory maximum/minimum and guideline category before giving outcome.
- Explain movement within the guideline range rather than merely listing factors.
- Ancillary orders, compensation, disqualification, restraining orders and surcharge should be included where facts raise them.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers show guideline category, starting point, range, aggravation, mitigation, plea credit, totality, ancillary orders and appeal threshold.
- Strong appeals distinguish wrong principle, manifest excess and undue leniency.
- Weaker answers call a sentence harsh/lenient without statutory maximum, guideline or threshold.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Current Sentencing Council guidelines are update-sensitive; verify where sentence range matters.
- Do not rely on moral blame alone; locate culpability and harm.
- Guilty plea, previous convictions, remorse, vulnerability and risk must be treated as separate factors.

## Avoid
- Avoid saying a sentence is "harsh" without appeal threshold.
- Avoid mixing sentencing mitigation with liability defences.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Sentencing Act 2020 (consolidating framework); Criminal Justice Act 2003 legacy provisions only where still relevant.
- *R v Blackshaw* [2011] EWCA Crim 2312 (guidelines / deterrence context — use carefully); guideline authorities must be matched to the current Sentencing Council guideline in force on the sentencing date.
- Totality / guilty plea: apply current Sentencing Council guidelines rather than outdated case headlines alone.
- Custodial thresholds / community orders: Sentencing Act 2020, Parts 2–10 as engaged.
- Dangerous offenders / extended sentences: SA 2020 provisions replacing CJA 2003 chapters — verify current sections.
- Appeal against sentence: Criminal Appeal Act 1968; undue leniency references under CJA 1988, s 36.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Cite the guideline and statutory maximum for the offence dated to the sentence; do not invent tariff numbers.
