# Public Law, Constitutional Law and Judicial Review

## Answer Method
- Start with constitutional/statutory source of power, claimant standing, decision-maker, ground of challenge, remedy, and time limit.
- Problems need issue-by-issue judicial-review grounds, not a generic fairness essay.
- Essays need a thesis on legality, accountability, separation of powers, parliamentary sovereignty, rights protection, or institutional competence.
- Planning-law prompts need development-plan status, material considerations, reasons/conditions, discretion, and statutory/JR route.

## Topic Map
- Parliamentary sovereignty, rule of law, prerogative, devolution, rights under HRA, constitutional statutes.
- Judicial review: illegality, relevant/irrelevant considerations, legitimate expectation, procedural fairness, reasons, irrationality, proportionality.
- Planning law: TCPA 1990 s 70(2), PCPA 2004 s 38(6), NPPF, planning conditions, local authority discretion, statutory review.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Public-law answers should identify the decision-maker, power source, claimant standing, ground of review, intensity of review, remedy, and discretion.
- Legitimate-expectation questions need representation/practice, clarity, reliance, fairness, overriding public interest, and remedy.
- Human-rights questions need public authority status, engaged right, interference, legality, legitimate aim, necessity/proportionality, and remedy.
- Planning prompts need development plan, material considerations, national policy, reasons/conditions, consultation, and judicial-review limits.

## For Each Topic Include
- JR: public body/amenability -> standing/time -> ground -> intensity of review -> remedy/discretion.
- Legitimate expectation: representation/practice -> clarity -> reliance/fairness -> overriding public interest -> remedy.
- Planning: development plan first, material considerations second, discretion third, reasons/conditions/review last.

## Case Brief Bank
- Factortame (No 2): Facts: UK statute conflicted with EU law. Held: courts disapplied the Merchant Shipping Act while EU law applied. Reasoning: EU supremacy accepted through the ECA 1972 as a domestic gateway. Answer use: sovereignty under EU membership.
- Jackson v Attorney General: Facts: challenge to Hunting Act via Parliament Acts. Held: Acts valid. Reasoning: obiter doubts whether sovereignty is absolute; rule of law as controlling factor (Lord Steyn, Lord Hope). Answer use: qualified sovereignty debate.
- Miller (No 1): Facts: could ministers trigger Article 50 by prerogative? Held: no — statutory authority required. Reasoning: prerogative cannot remove statutory rights; sovereignty of Parliament over the executive. Answer use: prerogative limits.
- Miller (No 2)/Cherry: Facts: prorogation of Parliament. Held: prorogation unlawful, justiciable. Reasoning: parliamentary sovereignty and accountability as constitutional principles limiting prerogative. Answer use: judicial enforcement of constitutional principle.
- Privacy International: Facts: ouster of IPT decisions from review. Held: ouster clause read not to exclude review for error of law. Reasoning: rule of law requires access to courts; echoes Anisminic. Answer use: ouster clauses.
- Anisminic v FCC: Facts: statutory ouster of commission determinations. Held: nullities not protected by ouster. Reasoning: errors of law take decision-maker outside jurisdiction. Answer use: foundation of ouster doctrine.
- Thoburn v Sunderland CC: Facts: metric martyrs. Held: constitutional statutes not impliedly repealable. Reasoning: hierarchy of statutes protecting fundamental rules. Answer use: constitutional statutes.
- AXA General Insurance v Lord Advocate: Facts: challenge to Scottish Parliament Act. Held: Acts of devolved legislatures not reviewable on common-law grounds save rule-of-law fundamentals. Answer use: devolution and rule of law.
- GCHQ: Facts: trade union ban by prerogative. Held: prerogative powers can be reviewable. Reasoning: subject matter, not source, controls reviewability. Answer use: JR foundations.
- Padfield: Facts: minister refused inquiry. Held: discretion must promote statutory purpose. Reasoning: power cannot frustrate Act. Answer use: improper purpose.
- Anisminic: Facts: tribunal error. Held: legal error can render decision null. Reasoning: jurisdictional error broadened. Answer use: error of law.
- Coughlan: Facts: promise of home for life. Held: substantive legitimate expectation protected. Reasoning: fairness can require promise kept absent overriding interest. Answer use: legitimate expectation.
- UNISON: Facts: employment tribunal fees. Held: fees unlawful. Reasoning: access to justice is constitutional. Answer use: rule of law.
- Tesco Stores v Dundee: Facts: planning policy interpretation. Held: policy meaning is legal question; application for authority. Reasoning: interpretive discipline and discretion. Answer use: planning.

## Feedback Rules
- Identify the exact ground of review; do not write generic “unfair/irrational”.
- Planning answers must not become generic JR answers; include statutory hooks.
- Remedies are discretionary: quashing, declaration, mandatory/prohibiting order, damages only where separate route.
- Legitimate-expectation answers should not stop at "promise made". Test clarity, qualification, reliance or fairness, frustration of expectation, overriding public interest, and remedy.
- Fettering/improper-purpose answers should connect the challenged conduct to the statutory purpose or policy discretion; do not treat all bad administration as illegality.
- Human-rights problem answers must keep engagement, interference, legality, legitimate aim, necessity/proportionality and remedy in separate steps.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band JR answers identify decision-maker, power source, amenability, standing/time, ground of review, intensity, remedy and discretion.
- Top-band legitimate-expectation answers distinguish procedural from substantive expectation and explain why the remedy is or is not keeping the promise.
- Top-band planning answers use the development plan, material considerations, national policy, reasons/conditions and statutory review route before broader JR points.
- Weaker answers usually write political commentary, cite proportionality without a rights context, or omit the discretionary nature of remedies.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Start with power source, amenability, standing, time limit and alternative remedy before merits. Judicial review is not a general appeal.
- Grounds must be exact: illegality, relevant/irrelevant considerations, improper purpose, fettering, procedural fairness, reasons, legitimate expectation, irrationality or proportionality.
- Legitimate expectation requires promise/policy, clarity, reliance or practice, procedural/substantive content, fairness, overriding public interest and remedial discretion.
- Rights/proportionality answers must justify intensity of review and apply legitimate aim, suitability, necessity and fair balance rather than generic rights language.
- Remedies need discretion: quashing, mandatory, prohibiting, declaration, injunction, damages only where separate basis exists, interim relief, remittal and practical consequence.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Protest and assembly questions should verify Public Order Act 1986, ss 12 and 14 as amended, Police, Crime, Sentencing and Courts Act 2022 and Public Order Act 2023 before applying Articles 10 and 11 or common-law proportionality.
- Public-law algorithm/risk-score questions should identify power source, transparency/reasons, relevant considerations, equality/data protection, and whether human rights require more intense review.

## Avoid
- Avoid political commentary without legal standard.
- Avoid proportionality unless the right/context justifies it.

## Anonymized Output Architecture
- A legitimate-expectation problem should open with claimant, decision, legal power, time and desired relief; distinguish procedural from substantive expectation, then test representation or practice, qualification, fairness, frustration and overriding interest before other illegality grounds.
- A rights problem should keep engagement, interference, legality, legitimate aim, necessity, less intrusive means, fair balance and remedy separate. Explain the selected review intensity rather than importing proportionality into every dispute.
