# AI and Data Protection Law

## Answer Method
- Identify the exact issue first: data protection compliance, automated decision-making, AI training inputs, AI-generated outputs, authorship/inventorship, platform/intermediary liability, surveillance/biometrics, or product/tort liability for autonomous systems.
- Keep the legal regimes separate: UK GDPR + Data Protection Act 2018; the EU GDPR and EU AI Act; sectoral rules (Online Safety Act 2023, equality, IP); and common-law misuse of private information.
- Problems need: applicable regime and territorial scope, lawful basis, data subject rights, controller/processor roles, breach/enforcement, and remedy.
- Essays need a thesis on innovation versus rights, accountability of opaque systems, the limits of consent, or whether existing law can absorb AI without new statute.
- Always flag currency: this is fast-moving law; say where official online verification of the latest instrument or case is needed.

## Topic Map
- Data protection: personal data, special category data, lawful bases, consent, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimisation.
- Data subject rights: access, rectification, erasure ("right to be forgotten"), portability, objection, and Article 22 rights on automated decisions.
- Accountability: controller/processor roles, DPIAs, international transfers, breach notification, ICO enforcement and fines.
- AI inputs: text and data mining, copyright in training data, database right, lawful basis for training on personal data.
- AI outputs: authorship/ownership of generated works, patent inventorship, liability for harmful or infringing output.
- Governance/risk: EU AI Act risk tiers, prohibited practices, biometric and facial recognition, public-sector equality and human-rights duties.
- Liability: product liability, negligence, and intermediary/platform liability for autonomous or algorithmic systems.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Data protection problems should start by confirming personal data and the applicable regime, then lawful basis, then rights and enforcement; do not jump to fines first.
- Automated-decision questions must separate Article 22 (solely automated, legal/significant effect), the safeguards, and the transparency duties; do not merge with general consent.
- AI-training questions must separate the input layer (copyright/database/personal data in the training set) from the output layer (who owns or is liable for what the model produces).
- Surveillance/biometric questions need Article 8 ECHR, the data protection regime, the public sector equality duty, and any specific statutory basis, kept distinct.
- Liability questions should identify the defendant and route (controller, developer, deployer, manufacturer, platform) before remedy.

## For Each Topic Include
- Data protection compliance: personal data -> regime/scope -> lawful basis -> transparency/purpose -> rights -> breach/enforcement -> remedy.
- Automated decisions: solely automated? -> legal or similarly significant effect? -> exception/consent/contract/authorised? -> safeguards and explanation -> objection.
- AI training inputs: protected subject matter -> who owns it -> exception (TDM/fair dealing) or lawful basis -> licence -> infringement/remedy.
- AI outputs: type of output -> authorship/inventorship rules -> ownership/assignment -> liability for harm or infringement.
- Biometric/facial recognition: Article 8 engagement -> in accordance with law -> necessity/proportionality -> data protection compliance -> equality duty.

## Case Brief Bank
- Lloyd v Google [2021] UKSC 50: Facts: representative claim for loss of control of browser data. Held: no uniform "loss of control" damages without proof of damage or distress per claimant. Reasoning: statutory damage requires individualised assessment. Answer use: data damages/representative actions.
- Vidal-Hall v Google [2015] EWCA Civ 311: Facts: tracking via Safari cookies. Held: misuse of private information is a tort; distress damages available without pecuniary loss. Reasoning: separates privacy tort from data protection claim. Answer use: privacy remedies.
- Durant v FSA [2003] EWCA Civ 1746: Facts: subject access request scope. Held: "personal data" is information that is biographical and has the individual as its focus. Reasoning: narrows the concept. Answer use: meaning of personal data.
- Google Spain (C-131/12): Facts: delisting request to search engine. Held: a right to erasure/delisting can arise against search engines. Reasoning: data protection rights over outdated/irrelevant results. Answer use: right to be forgotten (EU).
- R (Bridges) v South Wales Police [2020] EWCA Civ 1058: Facts: live automated facial recognition. Held: deployment unlawful — insufficient legal framework, deficient DPIA, breach of the public sector equality duty. Reasoning: Article 8 "in accordance with law" and equality duty not met. Answer use: biometrics/state surveillance.
- Schrems II (C-311/18): Facts: EU-US transfer mechanisms. Held: Privacy Shield invalid; standard contractual clauses require case-by-case adequacy assessment. Reasoning: third-country surveillance undermines adequacy. Answer use: international transfers.
- Thaler v Comptroller-General [2023] UKSC 49: Facts: AI named as patent inventor. Held: an inventor must be a natural person under the Patents Act 1977. Reasoning: statutory language controls; no entitlement flows from a machine. Answer use: AI inventorship.
- Getty Images v Stability AI: Facts: claim over training a generative model on copyright images. Held: proceeding through the UK courts; treat as unsettled. Reasoning: tests training-data infringement and TDM limits. Answer use: AI training inputs (current/unsettled — verify latest).

## Feedback Rules
- State the regime (UK GDPR/DPA 2018, EU GDPR, EU AI Act, common-law privacy) and territorial scope before analysing.
- Separate the privacy tort (misuse of private information) from the statutory data protection claim; they have different elements and remedies.
- For AI, name the exact layer: training input, model, output, or deployment. "AI law" alone is not an issue.
- Treat consent as one lawful basis among several; do not assume consent is required or sufficient.
- Flag where the instrument or case may have changed and online verification is needed.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Confirm the data is personal (and whether special category) and identify controller versus processor before duties and liability.
- Lawful basis is a closed list: do not default to consent; assess legitimate interests, contract, legal obligation, vital/public interest as appropriate, and add an Article 9 condition for special category data.
- Article 22 applies only to solely automated decisions producing legal or similarly significant effects; identify the exception relied on and the safeguards (meaningful information, human intervention, contest).
- Keep the AI input and output layers distinct: training-data copyright/database/personal-data questions are separate from authorship, inventorship, and liability for outputs.
- For surveillance and biometrics, run Article 8 (engagement, in accordance with law, necessity, proportionality), the data protection regime, and the public sector equality duty as separate gateways.
- International transfers need an adequacy decision, appropriate safeguards (SCCs/BCRs) with a transfer risk assessment, or a derogation — not a bare consent.

## Avoid
- Avoid discussing fines or enforcement before establishing personal data, lawful basis, and breach.
- Avoid merging the privacy tort and the data protection claim into one cause of action.
- Avoid treating "AI copyright" as a single issue without naming the right, the act, and the input/output layer.
- Avoid asserting current statutory detail without flagging that this area changes rapidly and needs verification.
