# Tort Law

## Answer Method
- Problems should use duty, breach, causation, remoteness, defences, and remedy unless the tort has a different structure.
- Essays need a thesis about corrective justice, deterrence, loss allocation, insurance, autonomy, or institutional competence.
- Identify the tort before applying general negligence language.
- Separate factual causation, scope of duty, remoteness, and loss quantification.

## Topic Map
- Negligence, omissions, psychiatric harm, pure economic loss, negligent misstatement, occupiers’ liability.
- Nuisance, Rylands, vicarious liability, defamation/privacy, product liability.
- Clinical negligence where expressly requested: breach, Bolam/Bolitho, Montgomery, causation/loss of chance.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Negligence answers must keep duty, breach, factual causation, scope of duty, remoteness, defences, and loss distinct.
- Duty essays should identify whether the case falls into an established category before using incremental/Caparo-style reasoning.
- Omission questions should start from the no-general-duty-to-act baseline, then test assumption of responsibility, creation/control of danger, third-party control, statutory context, and recognised relationships.
- Public-authority duty questions require institutional-competence discipline: separate operational negligence from failure to confer a public benefit, and test whether imposing a private-law duty would distort the statutory function.
- Psychiatric-harm and pure-economic-loss questions require category discipline; do not reason from general foreseeability alone.
- Nuisance/Rylands/privacy/defamation answers should state the protected interest first before balancing or remedy.
- Defamation answers should sequence meaning, reference, publication, serious harm, defence, and remedy; keep fact/opinion distinction visible before honest-opinion analysis.

## For Each Topic Include
- Negligence: duty category -> breach -> causation -> remoteness -> defences -> damages.
- Economic loss: exclusionary baseline -> assumption of responsibility -> reliance -> scope limits.
- Psychiatric harm: primary/secondary victim, control mechanisms, proximity.

## Case Brief Bank
- Donoghue v Stevenson: Facts: contaminated drink. Held: manufacturer owed duty to consumer. Reasoning: neighbour principle. Answer use: duty foundation.
- Caparo v Dickman: Facts: audited accounts relied on by investors. Held: no duty to investors for takeover losses. Reasoning: proximity, fairness, and purpose limit duty. Answer use: duty/scope.
- Robinson v Chief Constable: Facts: police arrest injured passer-by. Held: use established categories before Caparo. Reasoning: incrementalism not universal checklist. Answer use: modern duty method.
- Bolam / Bolitho: Facts: medical professional standards. Held: responsible body test subject to logical scrutiny. Reasoning: expertise respected but not conclusive. Answer use: clinical breach.
- Montgomery: Facts: failure to warn of material risk. Held: patient-centred disclosure duty. Reasoning: autonomy in risk information. Answer use: consent/negligence where asked.
- Michael v Chief Constable of South Wales: Facts: 999 call before fatal attack. Held: no police duty to protect from third-party harm. Reasoning: omissions principle; no assumption of responsibility. Answer use: public authorities/omissions.
- Poole BC v GN: Facts: council failed to protect children from neighbours. Held: no duty from statutory functions alone. Reasoning: public bodies judged by ordinary omissions principles. Answer use: public authority liability.
- Alcock v Chief Constable of South Yorkshire: Facts: Hillsborough psychiatric harm claims. Held: secondary victims need close ties, proximity in time/space, direct perception. Reasoning: policy control mechanisms. Answer use: psychiatric injury.
- Page v Smith: Facts: minor crash, chronic fatigue relapse. Held: primary victims need only foreseeable personal injury. Reasoning: physical/psychiatric harm not distinguished for primary victims. Answer use: primary/secondary distinction.
- Paul v Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust: Facts: relatives witnessed collapse from earlier negligence. Held: no secondary-victim claim for witnessing medical-negligence consequences. Reasoning: accident requirement retained. Answer use: current psychiatric-injury limits.
- Murphy v Brentwood DC: Facts: defective house foundations. Held: pure economic loss not recoverable in negligence. Reasoning: contract/tort boundary; floodgates. Answer use: economic loss rule.
- Spartan Steel v Martin: Facts: cut power cable, lost profits. Held: economic loss from damage to another's property irrecoverable. Reasoning: line-drawing for liability. Answer use: consequential vs pure economic loss.
- Hedley Byrne: Facts: negligent financial reference. Held: assumption of responsibility can found duty for statements. Reasoning: reliance and responsibility. Answer use: economic loss.
- Nettleship v Weston: Facts: learner driver injured an instructor. Held: drivers are judged by the objective standard of the reasonably competent driver. Answer use: established driver duty and breach without a fresh Caparo inquiry.
- Barnett v Chelsea and Kensington Hospital Management Committee: Facts: negligent failure to treat did not cause death because treatment would not have saved the claimant. Held: ordinary factual causation uses the but-for test. Answer use: distinguish breach from proof that it caused the injury or aggravation.
- The Wagon Mound (No 1): Facts: oil spill caused an unforeseeable kind of fire damage. Held: the kind of damage must be reasonably foreseeable. Answer use: tort remoteness; the precise sequence or extent need not be foreseen.
- Froom v Butcher: Facts: a claimant's failure to wear a seat belt worsened injury. Held: it may reduce personal-injury damages to the extent it contributed to the injury, without causing the collision. Answer use: contributory negligence and causal apportionment.
- McKew v Holland & Hannen & Cubitts: Facts: unreasonable conduct by an injured claimant caused a later accident. Held: a sufficiently unreasonable, independent act may break the chain. Answer use: novus actus analysis; later negligence does not automatically break causation.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not treat Caparo as the whole law after Robinson.
- Causation must not become a “loss of chance” workaround in personal injury unless law permits it.
- Explain why policy limits the duty, not just that it does.
- For defamation, do not assume reputational impact. Explain why the pleaded meaning meets the serious-harm threshold and why the statement is fact, opinion, or public-interest publication.
- For omissions and third-party harm, identify the specific duty-generating feature rather than relying on moral blame alone.
- Psychiatric-harm answers must start by distinguishing mere distress from recognised psychiatric illness, then primary victim from secondary victim, before applying the control mechanisms.
- Vicarious-liability answers must run all three hurdles: relationship, tort, and close connection/course of employment. Do not assume the relationship hurdle after Barclays if the tortfeasor may be in business on their own account.
- Public-body duty answers should separate direct positive acts from pure omissions, operational negligence from policy/resource allocation, and ambulance/fire/police lines of authority.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band negligence answers use the modern duty method: established category first, incremental/Caparo reasoning only where needed, and policy explained rather than asserted.
- Top-band psychiatric-harm answers apply ordinary fortitude and Alcock controls for secondary victims, and do not let foreseeability alone do all the work.
- Top-band vicarious-liability answers separate employment/akin relationship from close connection, and test independent-contractor limits expressly.
- Top-band defamation answers sequence meaning, reference, publication, serious harm or serious financial loss, defence, and remedy.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Identify the tort and protected interest before using negligence language. Nuisance, defamation, privacy, product liability and vicarious liability do not use the same test.
- Duty answers should begin with recognised category or no-duty baseline, then only use incremental reasoning where genuinely needed.
- Psychiatric harm needs recognised illness, primary/secondary victim classification, control mechanisms and policy limits; distress alone is not enough.
- Public authority/omission problems require direct act versus omission, operational negligence versus policy/resource allocation, assumption of responsibility and statutory-function distortion.
- Remedies must follow causation and scope of duty: loss type, remoteness, mitigation, contributory negligence, injunction/declaration and damages should be stated with practical outcome.
- In road-traffic negligence, do not use Caparo as a universal checklist, do not treat non-use of a seat belt as causing the collision, and do not categorise earnings or business loss consequential on personal injury/property damage as automatically irrecoverable pure economic loss.
- For delayed treatment or a later collision/obstruction, prove what extra harm the later event caused. Analyse concurrent causes, indivisible versus distinct aggravation, contribution, and whether the later act was sufficiently independent and potent to break the chain.
- Clinical negligence must separate diagnosis/treatment from disclosure. *Bolam*, qualified by *Bolitho*, governs professional diagnostic or treatment performance; *Montgomery* governs disclosure of material risks and reasonable alternatives and is not a general misdiagnosis test.
- For delayed diagnosis, require expert evidence on the counterfactual treatment and outcome. Ordinary personal-injury causation remains balance-of-probabilities; do not silently convert a sub-50% lost chance of recovery into damages contrary to *Gregg v Scott*.
- A relative who witnesses a later medical crisis must satisfy the secondary-victim controls in *Alcock* and the restrictive current analysis in *Paul v Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust*. A disease's later external manifestation is not automatically an accident caused by the clinician.
- An employer's contract loss caused by injury to its employee is ordinarily relational pure economic loss. Without assumption of responsibility or another recognised route, foreseeability does not create a duty to protect the employer's profits.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Negligence foundation: *Donoghue v Stevenson* [1932] AC 562 (HL); *Caparo Industries plc v Dickman* [1990] 2 AC 605 (HL); *Robinson v Chief Constable of West Yorkshire Police* [2018] UKSC 4, [2018] AC 736.
- Breach/professional standard: *Bolam v Friern Hospital Management Committee* [1957] 1 WLR 582 (QB); *Bolitho v City and Hackney Health Authority* [1998] AC 232 (HL); *Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board* [2015] UKSC 11, [2015] AC 1430.
- Causation/remoteness: *Barnett v Chelsea and Kensington Hospital Management Committee* [1969] 1 QB 428 (QB); *Fairchild v Glenhaven Funeral Services Ltd* [2002] UKHL 22, [2003] 1 AC 32; *Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v Morts Dock and Engineering Co Ltd (The Wagon Mound No 1)* [1961] AC 388 (PC).
- Road negligence/defences: *Nettleship v Weston* [1971] 2 QB 691 (CA); *Froom v Butcher* [1976] QB 286 (CA); *McKew v Holland & Hannen & Cubitts (Scotland) Ltd* [1969] 3 All ER 1621 (HL); Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act 1945, s 1; Civil Liability (Contribution) Act 1978, s 1.
- Psychiatric harm/economic loss: *Alcock v Chief Constable of South Yorkshire Police* [1992] 1 AC 310 (HL); *Paul v Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust* [2024] UKSC 1, [2024] 2 WLR 417; *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* [1964] AC 465 (HL); *Spartan Steel & Alloys Ltd v Martin & Co (Contractors) Ltd* [1973] QB 27 (CA).
- Medical causation: *Barnett v Chelsea and Kensington Hospital Management Committee* [1969] 1 QB 428 (QB); *Gregg v Scott* [2005] UKHL 2, [2005] 2 AC 176.
- Statutory tort routes: Occupiers' Liability Act 1957, s 2; Consumer Protection Act 1987, s 2; Defamation Act 2013, s 1.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic negligence where nuisance/defamation/product liability is the real route.
- Avoid damages before causation.
