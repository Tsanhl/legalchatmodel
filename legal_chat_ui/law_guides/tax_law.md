# Tax Law

## Answer Method
- Start with tax type and charging provision: income tax, corporation tax, capital gains tax, VAT, inheritance tax, or anti-avoidance.
- Problems need taxpayer, transaction, charge, computation assumption, relief/exemption, anti-avoidance, and compliance consequence.
- Essays need a thesis on certainty, equity, efficiency, avoidance, administrability, or fairness.
- State assumptions for dates, residence, amounts, connected persons, and statutory thresholds.
- For avoidance essays, build the answer chronologically: formal entitlement, Ramsay purposive construction, limits of purposive construction, statutory GAAR/TAARs, then administration and penalties.
- For problem questions, do not jump to GAAR before applying the ordinary charging code, relief conditions, timing rules, and any targeted anti-avoidance rule.

## Topic Map
- Residence/domicile, employment/self-employment, trading income, capital/revenue distinction, CGT disposals.
- VAT supply classification, exemptions, input tax; corporation tax basics.
- Avoidance: Ramsay, GAAR, targeted anti-avoidance, disclosure and penalties.
- International/treaty issues: residence tie-breakers, permanent establishment, treaty interpretation, withholding/dividend treatment, and double-tax relief.
- Administration and dispute control: self-assessment, discovery, closure notices, follower notices, penalties, legitimate expectation, judicial review, and statutory appeals.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Tax answers must be charge-led: taxpayer, taxable event, source of charge, timing, computation, relief, anti-avoidance, compliance.
- Avoidance essays should compare legality, certainty, administrability, fairness, revenue protection, and parliamentary design rather than moralising.
- HMRC power questions need the statutory notice/assessment/penalty route, preconditions, time limits, safeguards, forum, and remedy.
- Current thresholds/rates must be verified from retrieved/current sources; if not verified, state the legal consequence without invented numbers.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify taxpayer and chargeable event.
- Apply statute before policy.
- If numbers are missing, state assumption and explain consequence rather than inventing figures.
- Anti-avoidance: legal form, commercial reality, purposive interpretation, statutory target.
- Computation sequence: gross receipt/gain, deductible expenditure or base cost, relief/exemption, timing, rates/threshold caveat, then compliance consequence.
- VAT sequence: taxable person, supply of goods/services, place and time of supply, consideration, exemption/zero-rating, input-tax recovery, and penalty/default issue.
- Treaty sequence: domestic charge first, then treaty allocation, beneficial ownership/permanent establishment/residence, anti-abuse, and relief mechanism.
- HMRC powers sequence: identify the notice/assessment/penalty route, statutory preconditions, time limits, taxpayer safeguards, appeal forum, and remedy.

## Case Brief Bank
- Ramsay: Facts: pre-arranged tax loss scheme. Held: purposive approach to composite transactions. Reasoning: tax statutes applied to realistic transaction. Answer use: avoidance.
- Furniss v Dawson: Facts: inserted company in share sale. Held: pre-ordained steps could be disregarded. Reasoning: fiscal purpose and composite transaction. Answer use: step transactions.
- Barclays Mercantile: Facts: structured finance leasing. Held: purposive construction respects genuine commercial transactions. Reasoning: Ramsay is statutory interpretation, not broad anti-avoidance discretion. Answer use: limits of Ramsay.
- IRC v Duke of Westminster: Facts: tax planning by legal form. Held: taxpayer may arrange affairs within law. Reasoning: formalism baseline, now qualified. Answer use: historical contrast.
- Scottish Provident: Facts: avoidance scheme exploited contingencies. Held: inserted contingencies could be ignored when they had no commercial role. Reasoning: purposive interpretation asks what the statutory concept realistically covers. Answer use: Ramsay after formalism.
- Tower MCashback: Facts: capital-allowances scheme used circular funding. Held: relief failed beyond real expenditure. Reasoning: purposive construction focused on economic reality of incurred cost. Answer use: allowances and avoidance.
- Mayes: Facts: life-policy scheme achieved tax result within detailed statutory code. Held: taxpayer succeeded. Reasoning: purposive interpretation cannot rewrite highly specific legislation. Answer use: limits of anti-avoidance construction.
- HMRC v Haworth: Facts: follower notice used against offshore trust planning. Held: HMRC’s notice failed. Reasoning: follower-notice power requires a properly reasoned view that the earlier authority is determinative. Answer use: tax administration safeguards.
- Prudential Assurance v HMRC: Facts: tax unlawfully levied under EU-law incompatibility. Held: restitutionary recovery was available within limits. Reasoning: tax remedies require attention to limitation and statutory restitution routes. Answer use: remedies and overpaid tax.
- Ben Nevis: Facts: overseas tax authority sought UK assistance recovering a historic tax debt. Held: treaty mutual-assistance machinery could apply according to its terms. Reasoning: international tax enforcement turns on the convention text and implementing legislation. Answer use: treaty enforcement and collection assistance.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not answer tax from policy alone; apply charge and relief provisions.
- Do not hide calculations; state figures/assumptions visibly.
- Distinguish avoidance, evasion, and mitigation.
- A strong tax answer is statute-led but not statute-only: after the charge is identified, explain why the authority changes the legal character of the transaction.
- In essays, avoid a one-sided “taxpayers bad / HMRC good” account; weigh certainty, legality, administrability, fairness, and revenue protection.
- In problem answers, state what more information is needed for exact computation rather than inventing tax rates, thresholds, or dates.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Begin with taxpayer, tax type, charging provision, taxable event, timing and computation assumptions. Do not start with GAAR, fairness or HMRC policy before the ordinary charge.
- Current rates, thresholds, filing deadlines and penalties must be verified. If not verified, state the legal consequence without invented numbers.
- Avoidance answers must distinguish mitigation, avoidance, evasion, sham, Ramsay purposive construction, GAAR, TAARs, DOTAS/disclosure and penalties.
- VAT answers need taxable person, supply type, place/time of supply, consideration, exemption/zero-rating, input-tax recovery and default/penalty route.
- HMRC powers/disputes require the exact notice, assessment, discovery, follower, closure, penalty or appeal route, statutory preconditions, time limits, safeguards, forum and remedy.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Income and corporation tax: Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003, Pts 2-7; Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005, ss 5 and 34; Corporation Tax Act 2009, ss 46 and 54.
- Capital gains and VAT: Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992, ss 1, 21, 28, 38 and 222; Value Added Tax Act 1994, ss 1, 4, 5, 24 and 25.
- Avoidance: Finance Act 2013, Pt 5; *WT Ramsay Ltd v Inland Revenue Commissioners* [1982] AC 300 (HL); *Barclays Mercantile Business Finance Ltd v Mawson* [2004] UKHL 51, [2005] 1 AC 684; *RFC 2012 plc (in liquidation) v Advocate General for Scotland* [2017] UKSC 45, [2017] 1 WLR 2767.
- Administration and penalties: Taxes Management Act 1970, ss 9A, 28A, 31 and 50; Finance Act 2007, Sch 24; *HMRC v Tooth* [2021] UKSC 17, [2021] 1 WLR 2811.
- Anti-avoidance judicial / statutory: *WT Ramsay Ltd v IRC* [1982] AC 300 (HL); *Barclays Mercantile Business Finance Ltd v Mawson* [2004] UKHL 51, [2005] 1 AC 684; Finance Act 2013, Part 5 (GAAR) as engaged.
- Income / corporation framework: Income Tax Act 2007; Corporation Tax Act 2009/2010 (as engaged); *IRC v Duke of Westminster* [1936] AC 1 (HL) (historical — do not overstate).
- VAT classic: *Card Protection Plan Ltd v C&E Commissioners* (C-349/96) [1999] ECR I-973; check UK VAT Act 1994 post-exit status.
- Discovery / enquiry procedure: check TMA 1970 provisions in force on the assessment date.

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- Distinguish statutory construction, Ramsay-style purposive analysis, and the GAAR — do not conflate.
- Always verify the Finance Act year and HMRC guidance date before advising rates or reliefs.

## Avoid
- Avoid unverified current thresholds unless retrieved/current.
- Avoid moral labels without statutory effect.
- Avoid saying Ramsay is a free-standing discretion to strike down avoidance.
- Avoid treating GAAR, TAARs, purposive interpretation, sham, evasion, and disclosure penalties as interchangeable.
- Avoid treaty conclusions without first identifying the domestic charge.
