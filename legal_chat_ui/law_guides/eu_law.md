# EU Law

## Answer Method
- Identify whether the issue concerns constitutional EU law, internal market, competition, rights, remedies, or retained/post-Brexit law.
- Problems need instrument-specific analysis: Treaty, regulation, directive, decision, Charter, or retained domestic provision.
- Essays need a thesis on integration, effectiveness, legitimacy, sovereignty, proportionality, or institutional balance.
- Use article numbers precisely.

## Topic Map
- Direct effect, supremacy, indirect effect, state liability, preliminary references, judicial protection.
- Free movement of goods/persons/services/establishment/capital; citizenship; proportionality and justification.
- EU competition and digital markets where EU-specific.
- Retained EU law and UK/EU divergence where relevant.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Internal-market answers should identify the freedom, measure type, restriction/discrimination, justification, proportionality, and remedy in that order.
- Goods questions need Article 30/34/36 discipline, distinctly applicable/indistinctly applicable measures, selling arrangements/use restrictions, and mutual recognition.
- Persons/services/establishment questions need status first: worker, self-employed, service provider/recipient, citizen, family member, or company.
- Post-Brexit discussion should be labelled and not silently mixed with pre-Brexit EU-law doctrine.

## For Each Topic Include
- Direct effect: instrument, clarity/precision/unconditionality, vertical/horizontal limit, remedy alternative.
- Free movement: scope -> measure/restriction -> market access/discrimination -> justification -> proportionality.
- Preliminary reference: court/tribunal, necessity, acte clair/acte éclairé, discretion/obligation.

## Case Brief Bank
- Van Gend en Loos: Facts: customs duty challenge. Held: Treaty can confer individual rights. Reasoning: new legal order. Answer use: direct effect.
- Costa v ENEL: Facts: nationalisation conflict. Held: EU law supremacy. Reasoning: Member States limited sovereign rights. Answer use: supremacy.
- Francovich: Facts: unimplemented directive. Held: state liability. Reasoning: effectiveness and rights protection. Answer use: remedies.
- Dassonville / Cassis: Facts: trading rules restricting goods. Held: broad MEQR and mandatory requirements. Reasoning: market access and mutual recognition. Answer use: Article 34.
- Keck: Facts: selling arrangements. Held: certain non-discriminatory selling arrangements fall outside Article 34. Reasoning: limit overreach. Answer use: goods structure.
- Factortame: Facts: UK statute conflicting with EU law. Held: interim relief against statute possible. Reasoning: effective EU rights. Answer use: supremacy/remedies.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not collapse regulations, directives, and Treaty rights.
- Explain whether horizontal direct effect is available or whether indirect effect/state liability is the route.
- Proportionality must test suitability, necessity, and balance.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Identify the instrument and legal status first: Treaty, regulation, directive, decision, Charter, Withdrawal Agreement/Windsor Framework, assimilated/retained EU law or domestic replacement.
- Remedies answers must choose the route: direct effect, indirect effect, state liability, supremacy/disapplication, preliminary reference or domestic implementation. Do not state generic EU effectiveness.
- Internal-market answers should classify the measure before justification: discrimination, indistinctly applicable restriction, selling arrangement, use restriction, market access and mandatory requirement.
- Directives need vertical/horizontal discipline and alternative routes. Do not imply ordinary horizontal direct effect where the proper answer is indirect effect, state liability or general principles within their limits.
- UK-linked questions require a post-Brexit status check before relying on pre-Brexit supremacy, Charter or Court of Justice authority.

## Full OSCOLA Authority Bank
- Foundational EU doctrines: Case 26/62 *NV Algemene Transport- en Expeditie Onderneming van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen* [1963] ECR 1; Case 6/64 *Flaminio Costa v ENEL* [1964] ECR 585; *R v Secretary of State for Transport, ex p Factortame Ltd (No 2)* [1991] 1 AC 603 (HL).
- Directives/state liability: Case 41/74 *Van Duyn v Home Office* [1974] ECR 1337; Case 152/84 *Marshall v Southampton and South-West Hampshire Area Health Authority* [1986] ECR 723; Joined Cases C-6/90 and C-9/90 *Francovich v Italy* [1991] ECR I-5357.
- Free movement/proportionality: Case 120/78 *Rewe-Zentral AG v Bundesmonopolverwaltung fuer Branntwein (Cassis de Dijon)* [1979] ECR 649; Case C-55/94 *Gebhard v Consiglio dell'Ordine degli Avvocati e Procuratori di Milano* [1995] ECR I-4165.
- UK post-Brexit status: European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018, ss 2-7; Retained EU Law (Revocation and Reform) Act 2023, ss 1-5; European Union (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020 where Northern Ireland / TCA issues arise.
- Citizenship / residence classic: Case C-413/99 *Baumbast and R v Secretary of State for the Home Department* [2002] ECR I-7091; Case C-34/09 *Zambrano* [2011] ECR I-1177 — label post-exit UK relevance before applying.
- Competition overlap (where free-movement vs antitrust): cross-refer competition bank (*Meta* C-252/21; *Google Shopping* C-48/22 P).

## Current-Law Update Checkpoints
- For UK domestic questions, identify whether the rule is assimilated law, preserved EU law, domestic statute, or merely persuasive CJEU authority after the REUL Act 2023.
- Do not assume direct effect / supremacy propositions still operate as they did pre-exit unless the Withdrawal Act route is spelled out.
- Northern Ireland / Windsor Framework trade issues need the specific Protocol/TCA provision — not generic free-movement rhetoric.

## Avoid
- Avoid generic “EU law prevails” without instrument and route.
- Avoid using pre-Brexit propositions without checking current status where UK law is asked.

## Anonymized Quality Upgrade
- A free-movement problem should use a numbered issue structure and tie each treaty or Directive limb and Court of Justice authority to the relevant fact rather than describing the regime in the abstract.
- Rank each outcome as likely or arguable, identify missing status/duration facts and branch the result, and explain any post-exit UK divergence where the question touches domestic law.
