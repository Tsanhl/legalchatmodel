# Evidence Law

## Answer Method
- Problems should identify admissibility route, exclusionary discretion, weight, safeguards, and trial consequence.
- Essays need a thesis on truth-finding, fairness, reliability, rights, or managerial discretion.
- Distinguish admissibility from weight.
- Use Criminal Justice Act 2003, Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984, and common-law principles precisely.
- For criminal-evidence problems, analyse prosecution and defence evidence separately; the route, burden, and fairness objection may differ.
- For essays, organise around the tension between epistemic reliability, party autonomy, managerial efficiency, jury protection, and Article 6 fairness.

## Topic Map
- Hearsay, bad character, confessions, improperly obtained evidence, identification, expert evidence, privilege, vulnerable witnesses.
- Judicial discretion and Article 6/fair trial safeguards.
- Character and credibility: defendant bad character, non-defendant bad character, lies, sexual history, previous inconsistent statements, and warnings/directions.
- Scientific and expert evidence: expertise, reliability, admissibility threshold, jury assistance, DNA/statistical presentation, and expert duties.
- Vulnerable witnesses: special measures, cross-examination limits, rape-shield controls, anonymity, and fairness to the accused.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Problem answers should produce a ruling: admissible, excluded, admitted with limits, or admitted with a warning/direction.
- Always classify the evidence before applying a gateway: hearsay, confession, bad character, identification, expert, previous statement, privilege, or improperly obtained evidence.
- Article 6/fairness arguments should operate through the domestic route first, then overall trial fairness.
- Scientific evidence needs reliability and presentation discipline; do not let technical language substitute for probative value.

## For Each Topic Include
- Hearsay: definition -> gateway -> interests of justice -> safeguards/direction.
- Bad character: definition -> gateway -> probative value -> fairness exclusion.
- Confession: voluntariness/reliability -> oppression/unreliability -> PACE exclusion.
- Expert evidence: relevance, expertise, reliability, assistance to court, directions.
- Identification: witness opportunity, Turnbull warning, supporting evidence, dock identification risk, PACE Code D compliance, and whether withdrawal from the jury is required.
- Improperly obtained evidence: relevance, breach type, PACE section 78 fairness discretion, Article 6 context, and whether exclusion or a direction is the realistic remedy.
- Previous statements/lies: classify whether the evidence proves truth, consistency, fabrication, or consciousness of guilt before choosing the rule.
- Vulnerable/sexual offence evidence: identify the statutory restriction first, then evaluate necessity, probative value, and fairness to both complainant and defendant.
- Silence: identify the later fact relied on, whether the accused could reasonably have mentioned it when questioned/charged, legal-advice context, and whether an adverse inference is fair.
- Absent co-suspect/accomplice material: gateway -> motive to shift blame -> centrality -> lack of cross-examination -> Article 6 fairness -> whether editing, direction, or exclusion is required.

## Case Brief Bank
- R v Turnbull: Facts: disputed identification evidence. Held: judge must warn jury of special need for caution. Reasoning: mistaken identification risk. Answer use: identification.
- R v Sang: Facts: improperly obtained evidence. Held: limited common-law discretion. Reasoning: relevance generally admits, fairness controls. Answer use: exclusion background.
- R v Galbraith: Facts: no-case submission. Held: withdraw case if evidence insufficient or inherently weak. Reasoning: jury cannot convict on no safe evidence. Answer use: sufficiency.
- R v Hanson: Facts: bad character gateway. Held: structured approach to propensity and fairness. Reasoning: probative value and prejudice must be weighed. Answer use: bad character.
- R v Horncastle: Facts: hearsay and confrontation rights. Held: hearsay regime compatible with fair trial. Reasoning: statutory safeguards protect reliability. Answer use: hearsay/Article 6.
- R v Riat: Facts: absent-witness hearsay relied on by prosecution. Held: court must use the statutory hearsay sequence carefully. Reasoning: gateway, necessity, reliability, and fairness are cumulative controls. Answer use: hearsay route.
- R v Z: Facts: similar allegations used as evidence. Held: similar-fact evidence may be admitted if sufficiently probative. Reasoning: relevance and fairness, not moral suspicion, justify admission. Answer use: propensity/similar facts.
- R v Lucas: Facts: prosecution relied on lies. Held: lies need a careful direction before supporting guilt. Reasoning: people may lie for reasons other than guilt. Answer use: lies directions.
- R v Makanjuola: Facts: complainant/witness reliability warning issue. Held: warnings are discretionary, not automatic categories. Reasoning: judge assesses case-specific risk. Answer use: corroboration-warning reform.
- R v Bonython: Facts: expert evidence admissibility. Held: expert field and witness expertise must be reliable enough to assist. Reasoning: expert evidence needs specialised knowledge beyond ordinary jurors. Answer use: expert threshold.
- R v Dlugosz: Facts: DNA evidence and evaluative opinion. Held: expert DNA evaluation may be admissible with proper limits. Reasoning: jury must not be overawed by scientific form. Answer use: expert/statistical evidence.
- R v A (No 2): Facts: sexual history evidence restricted by statute. Held: exclusion must be read compatibly with fair trial. Reasoning: complainant protection cannot make conviction unsafe. Answer use: rape-shield fairness.
- Ibrahim v UK: Facts: delayed access to legal advice after terrorism arrests. Held: overall fairness is the Article 6 question. Reasoning: procedural defects may be cured or may undermine trial fairness. Answer use: Article 6/PACE fairness.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not say evidence is “relevant therefore admissible” without exclusion/fairness analysis.
- Always separate admissibility from weight and directions.
- Name the statutory gateway.
- A high-scoring problem answer gives the judge’s likely ruling, not just arguments for both sides.
- Do not cite Article 6 as a free-standing override before applying the domestic statutory route.
- Where evidence is admitted despite risk, specify the safeguard: warning, direction, editing, exclusion of part, or limits on use.
- Where several items of evidence arise, decide each item separately. A confession, hearsay statement, prior incident, identification and expert opinion do not share the same gateway.
- Defence evidence and prosecution evidence may trigger different routes and fairness objections; do not analyse only from the prosecution's perspective.
- Current-law answers should verify whether any statutory amendments, criminal procedure rules, or appellate decisions have altered the point before treating older notes as complete.
- In identification questions, Turnbull alone is not enough where the facts include video identification, prior exposure, social-media naming, or procedural contamination. Check PACE Code D before concluding on admissibility/weight.
- Facial mapping or image-comparison expert evidence must be controlled tightly: transparent methodology, qualifications, limitations, probabilistic language, and no usurpation of the jury's identification function.
- For confessions, decide whether the better route is PACE s 76(2)(a) oppression or s 76(2)(b) unreliability. Threats about family/social services may be stronger under unreliability than narrow oppression; s 78 remains a distinct fairness fallback.
- For CJPOA s 34 silence, do not merely say the defendant was silent. Name the later fact relied on and ask whether it would have been reasonable to mention it then.
- For bad character, classify the person first: defendant, co-defendant, non-defendant witness, or absent declarant. The gateway and fairness balance change with that status.
- Old or generic convictions are often weak propensity evidence. Explain age, similarity, prejudice, and probative value rather than treating the conviction label as enough.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band hearsay answers identify whether the statement is hearsay, choose the exact CJA 2003 gateway, apply interests of justice, then state safeguards and trial consequence.
- Top-band bad-character answers identify whether the evidence is defendant or non-defendant bad character, choose the gateway, and weigh probative value against prejudice/fairness.
- Top-band confession/improperly obtained evidence answers separate PACE s.76 reliability/oppression from s.78 fairness and from Article 6 overall fairness.
- Weaker answers usually merge admissibility and weight, invoke fairness in the abstract, or omit the judge's likely ruling.
- Strong first-class evidence answers add procedural exactness: Code D for identification, s 76(2)(a)/(b) separation for confessions, s 34 later-fact analysis for silence, and status-specific bad-character gateways.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Classify the evidence item first, then use the exact gateway, exclusion/fairness discretion, weight/direction and final ruling. Relevance alone is not admissibility.
- Identification needs Turnbull plus PACE Code D where procedures, video ID, dock ID, prior exposure or social-media contamination arise.
- Confessions require s.76(2)(a) oppression, s.76(2)(b) unreliability and s.78 fairness to be separated; threats and inducements often fit unreliability more clearly than narrow oppression.
- Silence under CJPOA s.34 needs the later fact relied on, opportunity to mention it, reasonable expectation and fairness of the inference.
- Hearsay and bad character require status discipline: defendant, co-defendant, non-defendant witness or absent co-suspect changes the gateway and fairness analysis.

## Avoid
- Avoid broad fairness rhetoric without PACE/CJA route.
- Avoid expert evidence without reliability test.
- Avoid merging hearsay, previous statements, and bad character merely because all concern out-of-court material.
- Avoid assuming jury directions solve every reliability problem; explain when withdrawal/exclusion may be required.
- Avoid civil/criminal evidence crossover unless the prompt clearly asks for civil evidence.

## Anonymized Output Architecture
- In a multi-item criminal problem, list the proposed use of each item, classify it, apply the exact gateway, address exclusion and fairness, then state admissibility, limits and any jury direction. Admissibility and weight must not be collapsed.
- A reliability essay should select a few evidential controls for sustained evaluation, connect each statutory gateway to its trial consequence, and use Article 6 as overall fairness context rather than a freestanding shortcut.
