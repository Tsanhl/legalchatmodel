# Equality Law

## Answer Method
- Start with protected characteristic, field, respondent, conduct, route and remedy.
- Problems should classify the claim: direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, discrimination arising from disability, reasonable adjustments, harassment, victimisation, equal pay or public-sector equality duty.
- Essays need a thesis on formal equality, substantive equality, justification, structural disadvantage, positive action or enforcement.

## Topic Map
- Direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, disability discrimination, reasonable adjustments, harassment, victimisation, equal pay, public-sector equality duty, positive action and remedies.

## Material-Led Emphasis
- Equality answers should identify protected characteristic, field, respondent and route before evaluating fairness.
- Direct discrimination needs comparator and "because of" analysis; indirect discrimination needs PCP, group disadvantage, individual disadvantage and justification.
- Disability cases require route discipline: direct discrimination, discrimination arising from disability, reasonable adjustments and harassment are different claims.
- Public-sector equality duty answers should treat equality impact assessment and due regard as procedural discipline, not automatic substantive liability.

## For Each Topic Include
- Identify comparator or PCP where needed.
- State burden shift and justification route.
- End with liability, defence and remedy.

## Case Brief Bank
- Equality Act 2010, ss 15, 20-21 and sch 8: keep discrimination arising from disability, the reasonable-adjustments requirements, knowledge and failure to comply analytically separate.
- Shamoon v Chief Constable of the RUC: Facts: discrimination comparator dispute. Held: comparator analysis must focus on material circumstances. Reasoning: treatment and reason why are central. Answer use: direct discrimination.
- Nagarajan v London Regional Transport: Facts: discriminatory motivation. Held: protected characteristic need only have significant influence. Reasoning: causation can be subconscious. Answer use: "because of" analysis.
- Essop v Home Office: Facts: group disadvantage in assessment. Held: claimant need not prove why PCP disadvantages group. Reasoning: statistical disadvantage can be enough. Answer use: indirect discrimination.
- Homer v Chief Constable of West Yorkshire Police: Facts: degree requirement disadvantaged older worker. Held: indirect discrimination depends on particular disadvantage and justification. Reasoning: neutral rule can be discriminatory. Answer use: PCP/justification.
- Archibald v Fife Council: Facts: disabled employee redeployment. Held: reasonable adjustments may require favourable treatment. Reasoning: disability equality can require positive steps. Answer use: reasonable adjustments.
- Brutus v Cozens: Facts: harassment style assessment in earlier race-relations context. Held: ordinary meaning and tribunal evaluation matter. Answer use: harassment threshold with caution.

## Feedback Rules
- Do not say "discrimination" without naming the statutory route.
- For indirect discrimination, state PCP and comparator pool before justification.
- For disability, ask what the employer/public body knew or ought to know where the route requires knowledge.
- Remedies should include compensation, injury to feelings, declarations/recommendations or public-law relief as appropriate.

## Topic-Specific Marking Rubric
- Top-band answers classify each claim, state comparator/PCP/knowledge/burden, apply justification, and finish with remedy.
- Strong essays distinguish formal equality, substantive equality, positive action and structural disadvantage.
- Weaker answers use unfairness, diversity or disadvantage language without statutory gateway.

## Strong First-Class Accuracy Pass
- Keep direct discrimination, indirect discrimination and disability-specific routes separate.
- Justification needs legitimate aim and proportionality, not general business convenience.
- For public bodies, public-sector equality duty is procedural and must be separated from substantive discrimination.

## Avoid
- Avoid using "unfair" as a substitute for a discrimination route.
- Avoid mixing harassment, victimisation and discrimination without matching facts.
